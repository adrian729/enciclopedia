## 1. Tarot as a Procedural Algorithm

- **The 78-card tarot deck** — 22 Major Arcana + 56 Minor Arcana. Drawing 1–10 cards in a **spread** answers a **querent's** question. Each card carries multiple meanings; the reader picks relevance through associational construction.
- **Belief in divination is optional** — tarot is a fast intimacy-building ritual *and* a powerful procedural generator.
- **Scale** — 4.675 × 10²¹ possible 10-card Celtic Cross spreads. Yet tarot sidesteps the 10,000 Bowls of Oatmeal problem (Ch 1).
- **Why no oatmeal**:
  - No objectively correct reading — reader and querent decide when they're satisfied; extra cards can clarify.
  - Meaning comes from layered overlapping taxonomies + the reader's personal associations + the phrasing of the question.

## 2. Overlapping Taxonomies

- **Major / Minor Arcana division** — and within Minor, four **suits** (Pentacles/Coins, Cups, Swords, Wands), subdivided into **numbered** and **court** cards.
- **Suit variation across decks** — *Pentacles* may be *Coins* or *Disks*; *Wands* may be *Staves* or *Clubs*. Court cards vary: *Page/Knight/Queen/King*, *Prince/Princess/Queen/King*, or bespoke (*Son/Daughter/Mother/Father* in the Wild Unknown; *Amazon/Siren/Witch/Hag* in Dark Goddess).
- **Decks add expressive range** — Slow Holler has *Branches/Stones/Knives/Vessels*; PoMo has *Bottles/Bills/TVs/Guns*; Fifth Dimension adds *ether*; Silicon Dawn has a partial *Void* suit. Aleister Crowley's Thoth deck adds a Major Arcana card *the Aeon*.
- **Card imagery primes readings** — Thoth prints one-word prompts on Minor Arcana (*Victory*, *Ruin*); Rider-Waite-Smith leaves the reading nonverbal. A deck with no human figures may feel ill-suited to romance questions for some readers, perfect for others.
- **Reader variation** — each reader brings their own prior associations, skill, and practice. A reader who doesn't distinguish Page from Knight can still interpret consistently.

## 3. Spreads as Possibility Space

- **One-card spreads** — blunt; brief flashes of insight. *"Justice"* drawn for an upcoming meeting could suggest fairness or warn that you'll get back what you put in.
- **Three-card spreads** — **past-present-future** traces progression; **situation-action-outcome** suggests agency (an action that *could* change the situation).
- **Celtic Cross (10 cards)** — less linear, more contextual overview. Crosses past-present-future with *situation*, *subconscious*, *desire*, *hopes and fears*, *community*, *advice*, *outcome*. More cards = more hooks for associational meaning; can overwhelm novices.

## 4. Sample Reading

Question: *"Should I look for a safe and stable job or pursue a riskier but more creatively fulfilling path?"*. Three-card situation-action-outcome draw: **Two of Swords reversed** (situation) / **Ten of Cups** (action) / **Wheel of Fortune** (outcome).

- **Two of Swords reversed** — Swords is air, rational decision-weighed; reversed suggests a difficult decision imposed from without.
- **Ten of Cups** (water, emotion) — joy and fulfilment through harmony, community, real connection.
- **Wheel of Fortune** (Major Arcana) — inevitability of change, forward motion, could be progress or chaos.
- **Overall reading** — a difficult decision between two options; listening to the heart opens a third path with its own joys and challenges.
- **Taxonomies at play**:
  - **Suits** — Swords → Cups feels like moving *backwards* (Cups is a "lower" suit), but could be moving forwards emotionally. Invites reconsideration.
  - **Number** — Two → Ten → Major Arcana is a clear ascending progression.
  - Multiple taxonomies don't collapse into one; readers choose which salience to favour.
- **Swap outcome and action** — with Wheel in action and Ten of Cups in outcome, the read shifts: surrender to chaos, and emotional fulfilment follows.
- **Flip orientation** — reversing the Two of Swords to upright makes the decision internally imposed rather than external; changes cause without changing cards.
- **Substitute a card** — Seven of Swords for Wheel of Fortune flips the whole spread to a warning about subterfuge and betrayal; the emotional move is followed by a retreat into self-protection.

## 5. Implications for Designers

- **Why tarot avoids oatmeal** — substituting even one card asks readers to juggle multiple aspects without a concrete answer. Readers fill gaps; they supply the most narratively satisfying piece themselves.
- **Hard to digitise fully** — the Golden Thread tarot has a companion app; Emily Short's *Tarot of the Parrigues* is in draft. The reader–querent–cards back-and-forth is difficult to capture digitally in real time.
- **Takeaway for narrative design** — use **multiple co-existing layers of meaning** without insisting one takes precedence. Let readers choose their mode of interpretation; the investment of curating meaning from many possibilities produces more personal stories than unstructured advice.

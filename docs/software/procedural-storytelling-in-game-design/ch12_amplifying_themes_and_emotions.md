## 1. Two-Step Process

- **Mechanics generate emotion** — most players lack the habit of noticing these emotional undercurrents, so the designer must amplify them.
- **Step 1** — add stimuli or feedback to interaction loops that boost the emotions of play.
- **Step 2** — use resonant real-world themes to convert raw emotions into a meaningful experience.
- **Not linear plotting** — Cook's "narrative" is not book/movie-style; it is emotion + theme layered on systems.

## 2. Observing Emotions in Triple Town

- **Triple Town (2010)** — drop objects onto a grid; three adjacent same-level objects merge into a higher-level object. The basic mechanic later appeared in *Threes* and *2048*.
- **Bura's "Emotion Engineering in Videogames" framework** — put the player in states where critical variables are constrained or free to produce instinctual feelings:
  - **Variables** — anything with in-game value: time, territory, currency, items.
  - **Velocity of change** — slow drain vs. sudden loss produce different emotions.
  - **Direction of change** — strong gain vs. strong loss.
  - **Predictability of change** — steady predictable decline vs. rapid unpredictable fluctuation.
- **Cook's observed emotions while playing Triple Town**:
  - **Decisiveness** — clear information → smart plans
  - **Anticipation** — waiting for the right piece from the random draw
  - **Tension** — territory (free space) eroding as plans stall
  - **Relief** — the right piece arrives, objects combine, space reopens
- **Blockers amplified emotion** — black squares appearing randomly added **frustration**, **hostility** (players anthropomorphised the random blockers as intentional adversaries), and **defensiveness**. Every move became a conscious trade-off between **options planning** (long-term robustness) and **opportunism** (short-term capture). Sid Meier's *density of interesting decisions* rose sharply, and each move produced a layered emotion instead of a single note.

## 3. Finding a Theme — Colonialism

- **Cook's childhood in rural Maine** — lived alongside Native American classmates and descendants of English settlers; read in school about atrocities committed against local tribes while playing basketball and dating the same classmates. The dissonance: how could humans do such horrible things to other humans?
- **The prototype as petri dish** — players in *Triple Town* learn to hate neutral black squares purely through the pressures of territory acquisition. That process mirrored social-psychology and game-theory experiments: break a system into agents with rule-based interactions, assign costs and payoffs, watch strategies emerge that match real-world behaviour. Behaviour ascribed to habit or history often has strong structural reinforcers; people behave as the rules encourage them to. Nicky Case's work uses similar models for discrimination, segregation, and mob rule.
- **The thematic claim** — colonisation is a system of exploitation and conquest rooted in a zero-sum value structure where even innocents are marked as enemies or resources.

## 4. Using Theme to Amplify Gameplay Emotion

- **Emotion occurs on three levels**: (1) **Body** — physiological response (fight/flight, tend-and-befriend); (2) **Mind** — labelling and memory retrieval; (3) **Feedback loop** — the two combine to moderate or intensify (a friend tackling you spikes heart rate, then labels as play → laughter). See two-factor theory of emotion.
- **Design provides labels** — the designer's job is to suggest labels for the raw physiological response. Triple Town's labels:

| Abstract system | Themed label |
|---|---|
| Playing grid | A small island — limited territory |
| Upgradeable objects | Forests, buildings, mansions — growth |
| Score | Expansion of a fictional **Empire** |
| Black squares | Peaceful giant **bears** |
| Removing a black square | Using a **military** weapon |

- **Why bears instead of people** — a direct victim of colonisation would trigger pre-existing schema and skip the introspection. Dehumanising the obstacle (*"they aren't humans; they're animals"*) is also historically how moral inhibitions are lowered, so the obfuscation itself was thematic.
- **Theme-driven mechanical changes** — bears stopped teleporting and walked turn by turn (**migration**); players could pen bears in with trees to clear building areas (**reservations**); bears packed too tightly died and left gravestones that matched into **churches** (the historical balm of religion built on the bones of the locals).

## 5. Where Triple Town Failed

- **Personal growth — success**: Cook learned how system balance shapes perceived player morality.
- **Communication — failure**: Few players grappled with colonialism while playing.
  - *Cute* is culturally coded as infantile/safe (with a streak of sexism), so the dissonance between cute bears and horror didn't provoke interrogation — players doubled down and filtered the dissonance out.
  - *Casual* match-3 games carried no cultural expectation of serious subtext; players don't look for wolves in a mall.
  - *Subtle references* — most players noticed only the First Read (buildings + bears), not the Second Read labels.
- **Taste — failure**: Those who noticed the theme were offended that a cartoony casual game would handle colonialism this way. Some critiqued that *Triple Town* risked reinforcing colonialist norms by pleasurably naturalising them.
- **On game journalism** — cute and casual games coded female get dismissed as "mindless time wasters" while a game about men shooting men in the head is still more likely to be reviewed as serious art.

## 6. Lessons

- **Practice** — the two-step process is not a rote pattern. Mastery comes through many imperfect attempts. Cook's later work: *Road Not Taken* (a traditional-society figure forced to give up having children), *Leap Day* (differentiated means of production yielding cooperative micro-communities), *Beartopia* (persistent communities where immigrant strangers become close friends). Each carried old mistakes and new ones, but also measurable progress.
- **An uneducated audience** — most players don't deeply read subtext. Blunt labels are sometimes necessary to overcome limited systems literacy. Educating the audience is a generational task for many authors across many works.
- **Ongoing conversation** — a game is not a static missive. *Triple Town* has been rebuilt four times (e-Ink Kindle, Facebook, Steam, mobile); each release sharpens the message based on audience response. Communication is iterative.

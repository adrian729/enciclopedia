## 1. Story, Plot, and Froth

- **Hemingway's iceberg** — if the writer knows enough of what he is writing about, the reader feels the omitted things as strongly as if they had been stated.
- **Gardner (On Becoming a Novelist)** — feelings become real only when they take the form of events.
- **"Froth"** — LARPers' word for the post-match phase where people reconstruct what just happened. Dwarf-fortress colony postmortem blog posts are froth. Pandemic discussions after a close game are froth. Good games generate good froth.
- **Overland goal** — make a post-apocalyptic road-trip turn-based game with *great froth*; the team added "story generator" to the to-do list.

## 2. Why Plot Instead of Story

- **The player is the story generator** — what the game needs is a **plot generator**.
- **Plot = actions and events** — subjects verbing nouns at locations: *"Alice got an avocado at the store."* *"Jeff crashed his car near the lake."*
- **Story = sequence, editing, prose, motivation** — the whole enchilada.
- **Advantages of targeting plot** — no tricky sentences, easier localisation, skip the uncanny valley of synthetic prose. All games are plot generators already, so it's refinement rather than invention.
- **Plot parallels good writing** — the author omits a detail; the reader fills it in. A film montage joins two unrelated images; the viewer extracts meaning. Good plot generators find surprising arrangements so players can weave the story.

## 3. Enriching a Repetitive Plot Generator

- **Most games are repetitive plot generators** — Tetris ("an L block fell, then a T, then a square"), NBA Jam ("Detroit scored two points"). Serviceable but thin.
- **Early Overland** — *"smack monster with stick, nibble, heal, search dumpster, die, drive away"*. Most sessions collapsed to "smack, search, smack, search, smack". Deriving a human story was a chore.
- **Looking for plot-altering actions**:
  - Obvious: CPR to revive a character.
  - Not-so-obvious: patting a dog, worth an action point even with no payoff.
  - Second-order: adding diagonal movement enabled emergent narrow-escape kitty-corner squeezes.

## 4. Turning Stories into Verbs

- **Popup-dialog encounters (FTL, Oregon Trail)** — scenarios with a narrative/player choice. Overland inverted this: instead of a popup, the stranger shouts a brief greeting ("I got left behind, please take me with you!"; "She's running on fumes, can I join up?"; "Get me outta here!").
- **Events broken into atomic actions** — Move, Invite, Attack, Loot. Players navigate the grid, click the stranger, pick *Invite* to recruit, *Attack* to fight, or *Invite* only to grab inventory and abandon them. *"Dupe stranger into giving up items"* emerges from atomic actions rather than being a menu button.
- **Space for unexpected disasters** — a noble rescue attempt gets engulfed by a wildfire; an attack on a stranger reveals hidden companions; treating a stranger's injuries makes them change their mind about joining. These improvisations are the froth.
- **Strong player-character connection unlocks the rest** — improving plot paid off only after the art director replaced the stoic character models with cuter ones and the UI foregrounded the player's group. Generated plot without emotional investment produces nothing worth retelling.

## 5. Micro-Story Couplets

- **First attempt — generated backstory paragraphs** — cheap to make, but there was nowhere to put them; players wouldn't pause to read multi-paragraph bios of found strangers.
- **Second attempt — traits and adjectives** (*lonely*, *angry*) — didn't work; adjectives carry no event-grounded meaning.
- **Hemingway's six-word story** — *"For sale: baby shoes, never worn."* Inspired micro-stories that evoke feelings through events. Even crude examples — *"Grew up an orphan,"* *"Used to attend anger management classes"* — were huge improvements.
- **Couplet format** — two micro-stories per character: one "past", one "present". Sample couplet: *"Skipped prom to watch the new monster movie. Hoping to find a dog."* Past + present evoke a montage-like emotional arc across the end of the world.
- **Contradictory couplets are assets** — *"Hated camping as a kid. Sleeps under the stars now."* invites interpretation (frustrated, grown up, afraid, all three). The team doesn't filter or weight; any cross-column combination is allowed. Even creepy results (*"Tortured squirrels when younger. Hoping to find a dog."*) leave the player's imagination to do the work.
- **Almost no internal systems** — the only generative step is mashing two columns; within a sentence there are only gentle nudges for gendered pronouns. The "best results came from removing almost all the systems" and drawing pre-written cards from two decks.
- **Every sentence is plot-focused** — losing, finding, hiding, missing. Emotions arise from events and the juxtaposition of two cards, exactly as Gardner described.

## 1. Games, Stories, and Human Values

- **Games as a medium should move past "just fun"** — *That Dragon, Cancer*, *This War of Mine*, *Train*, *Frostpunk* all examine the human condition. Entertainment is fine; content can be worthwhile.
- **Humans see the world in stories** — an automatic, involuntary cognitive process (see Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*).
- **Game structure is cyclical, not linear** — books and films build beat → scene → sequence → act. Games stack action-feedback loops, tactical/short-term goal loops, strategic long-term loops, social/emotional loops, and cultural-meaning loops.
- **The values most games use** — life vs. death, victory vs. defeat, progress vs. setback. Overused. *This War of Mine* layered in the mercy-vs-selfishness dilemma of stealing medication from an elderly couple. *Frostpunk* set out to push further.

## 2. Prototype 1 — Society

- **Starting principle** — Oscar Wilde's *"society exists only as a mental concept; in the real world there are only individuals"*. Drive society from individual agents rather than modeling it as an abstract system.
- **Maslow-style needs** — each AI citizen has a set of basic psychological/safety needs (shelter, food). Unmet needs increase *frustration*.
- **System-driven questions** — when enough agents are "frustrated enough", a pop-up asked the player what to do: *"Your people are angry and want homes. What will you do?"*. Food-shortage frustration produced the question *"Should we eat the dead?"*. Child-labour accidents triggered the question of banishing it.
- **Playtest verdict** — two serious problems: no player agency (questions fired at random times from the player's perspective), no overarching narrative (random pool, no beginning/middle/end). But the questions themselves were compelling.

## 3. Prototype 2 — The Prophet

- **New systemic need — "meaning of life" / angst** — explaining it to players was hard, but it drove a narrative arc.
- **Inline call-to-action UI** — instead of pop-ups, a tracker appeared over a building or agent; clicking opened the dilemma. Two-step flow: CTA, then choice.
- **Discontent bar** — a weighted average of all citizens' frustrations surfaced as a single red bar.
- **The Prophet arc** — fear-driven movement around a charismatic leader promising survival. Player options included building guard stations, investigating. Resolution choices: public execution, let him go, and more.
- **Playtest verdict** — narrative did form in players' heads, but felt like a separate game (city-builder + pick-your-own-adventure). Conclusion: shorter stories, tighter integration with base loops. Coffee-room discussions revealed people deeply disagreed about killing the Prophet — which surfaced the game's core question: *"The end justifies the means. … Or does it?"*

## 4. Prototype 3 — The Book of Laws

- **Split decision types** — individual moments stayed as in-world CTAs; big structural issues (child labour, corpse disposal) became **laws** enacted through the player's Book of Laws.
- **Agreement for reforms** — controversial laws raise discontent; desired ones lower it.
- **Fear, hope, discontent → hope, discontent** — initial three-parameter model collapsed because fear read as "counter to hope"; the team dropped fear and kept the 2D plane. Hard definitions matter: *hope* = will to live / belief in survival; *discontent* = anger at the ruler. High hope + high discontent = belief in survival but not with this ruler; low hope + low discontent = needs met but no reason to endure. Losing conditions: discontent max, hope zero.
- **Tech-tree Book of Laws** — after experimenting with "book-inspired" interfaces, they settled on a simple tech-tree with cooldowns; enacted laws are permanent.
- **Playtest verdict** — players felt agency, planned ahead, managed hope and discontent. Complaints focused on how laws connected to each other, which led to the **boiling frog** metaphor: gradual normalisation. Laws were restructured so each step pushes morality only a little further than the last. *"You cannot wake up in the morning and decide that everyone who likes pineapple pizza should be incarcerated, but things of similar absurdity have happened many times in history."* Purpose laws were built around this pattern.

## 5. Final Stretch — Consequences and Reflection

- **Every law got consequences** — short narrative arcs using CTA + dilemma structure showed how laws reshaped individual lives (radical medical treatment is efficient but leaves someone disabled).
- **Tone iteration** — first attempts were either cartoonishly grim (broke immersion) or so subtle only the writer got them. Brute-force rewriting over weeks found the sweet spot.
- **No in-moment empathy like This War of Mine** — but post-playthrough reflection was strong. Players asked themselves whether there was even a right choice in such times.
- **Endlog** — a replay of the whole playthrough ending with the question *"Was it worth it?"*. The goal was to support the reflective layer, not answer its questions.

## 6. Takeaways

- **Frostpunk is brainier than This War of Mine** — reflection, not immediate empathy, carries the emotional load.
- **Not universal** — some players skimmed the society layer and read the Book of Laws purely as gameplay. That's fine; the market is receptive to more ambitious content, and there's room to do better next time.
- **Worthwhile making worthwhile games** — players reward games that try to transcend genre conventions.

## 1. Innovation vs. Emotional Labour

- **Narrative designers are obsessed with structure** — branching, linear, non-linear, open-world, choice-driven, player-generated, diegetic. Structure is easy to measure and test; emotion is not.
- **What's missing** — a thorough investigation of the human condition. Innovating in delivery without moving the player to their core is a distraction from the emotional labour of story craft.
- **Innovation is next to procrastination** — theorising lets designers live in the "about to write" state indefinitely, trading the mess of committing to specifics for tidy structural diagrams.

## 2. Bad Dreams: Structural Experiments

A suite of interactive-fiction structures Murray built during *InterProject* at Ubisoft Montreal (2011). Each began as a dreamy structural idea and turned into a "story prison" once production constraints were applied.

- **Another Door Opens** — simple A/B decision, but previews all 16 possible outcomes of outcomes. Weight of information made choice paralyzing and indistinguishable from a coin flip.
- **The Quantum Circuit** — choose container A or B; contents only exist after choosing. Choice becomes both all-important and meaningless until the player has played enough times to map the possibility space.
- **You Don't Know What You're Missing** — one chapter per day. Miss a day and you get a summary; the more days missed, the more summaries decay and lie. Emphasised and undermined the author's role simultaneously.
- **Down the Rabbit Hole** — a distraction engine offering tangents to tangents, eventually dumping readers into the open internet. Accelerated the author's loss of control and the reader's loss of closure.
- **Lesson** — the structures communicated mostly "help me". Murray had not innovated narrative; she had destroyed reading. A month of InterProject yielded charts but zero actual story, characters, or drama.

## 3. Character as Anchor

- **Assassin's Creed: Liberation (2012)** — *Aveline de Grandpré*, daughter of a French merchant and an enslaved mother in 18th-century New Orleans, led the team through production. Love of character is what kept a linear story advancing inside an open world.
- **Linear stories inside open worlds** — the player's focus and pacing can't be assumed. For many, *Skyrim* is about adopting pets or herding cheese; questing is what happens between explorations. Hero's Journey structure gives way to whatever the player feels like doing.
- **Character-driven production** — when designers and players invest in a character, the character can pull the story forward even without traditional pacing controls.

## 4. Sparse Generation and Player Attachment

- **10,000 Bowls of Oatmeal applied to characters** — generated quest-givers risk becoming skippable-dialogue oatmeal unless each character is anchored to game mechanics (Emily Short's argument, see also Ch 28).
- **Less is more** — Murray's experiments in Twine, then JavaScript, generated short attribute lists and let users react:

```
Name:                        Nat
Age:                         28
Gender:                      Non-binary
Dominant trait:              Introverted
Favourite colour:            Purple
Kids:                        2
Worst fear:                  Spiders
Strongest childhood memory:  Bullied one friend only once and immediately regretted it
Ambition:                    Go back to school and get a masters' degree
Emotional need:              Confidence
Hobby:                       None
```

- **What readers did** — filled in their own stories. Small tweaks shifted interpretations drastically (changing age from 28 to 23 implied young kids and quit school; changing trait from introverted to extroverted implied a bully seeking attention).
- **Too many details = oatmeal** — users start gaming the system instead of empathising with the character. Sparse outlines invite participation; dense outlines invite analysis.
- **Players already project** — *Shadow of the Tomb Raider*'s Lara Croft is one authored character, yet players produce wildly different portraits of her. Generation can deliberately provoke this projection and offload "processing" to the player's imagination.
- **Final thesis** — the hard part of game narrative isn't innovation in structure; it's understanding and connecting to people — characters, players, collaborators, ourselves.

## 1. Why Look at TTRPGs

- **Fast iteration** — game release cycles stretch to 6 months to half a decade; a tabletop RPG session lets designers rapidly test creative output on real humans.
- **Collaborative storytelling** — one **game master** (GM, dungeon master, MC) arbitrates rules and builds the plot; three to six players each run a single character. The only limits on player actions are what makes sense in the fiction — *"No, you can't climb a tree; there's no tree nearby"*.
- **Recommended entry point — Dungeon World** (Adam Koebel & Sage LaTorra). Free online, no prep, classic fantasy. Introduces three pieces of technology missing from Dungeons & Dragons that are directly relevant to procgen: **Moves**, **Dangers**, **failing forward**.
- **Railroading is the failure mode** — when GMs face distractable players and force them toward the intended scene. A better approach shares structure with procedural generation.

## 2. Moves

- **Moves are fictional-mechanical actions** that either a player or the GM can take. *"I scan the sides of the canyon"* is *Discern Realities*; *"I thrust my sword into the goblin's chest"* is *Hack and Slash*.
- **GM Moves can be fully narrative** — *"Suddenly, the ground below you groans and shifts"* is *Show Signs of an Approaching Threat*. Or fully mechanical — *"Chunks of stone break loose, crashing down — take 5 damage"* is *Deal Damage*.
- **Triggers** — mechanical changes (a clock advances) or narrative situations (the weakened ceiling collapses).
- **The GM's list of Moves** (selected): use a monster, danger, or location move; reveal an unwelcome truth; show signs of an approaching threat; deal damage; use up their resources; turn their Move back on them; separate them; offer an opportunity, with or without cost; put someone in a spot; tell them the requirements or consequences and ask.
- **Why Lumpkin likes the list** — it provides a framework of narrative categories, not concrete events. "Use up resources" inspires a broken lockpick, a shattered potion bottle, a snuffed torch. Each GM fills the category with specifics on the fly; the rulebook constrains fairness (a goblin's dagger does a defined amount of damage). Like procgen algorithms pulling from pre-authored content blocks.
- **Design equivalent** — GMs don't pre-define every possible "sign of approaching threat", but video-game designers *could* build that list. Lumpkin includes a sample table with three Dangers (Cult Leader Sylvar, the Demon Vorilex, the Risen Dead) and four signs each.

## 3. Dangers

Dungeon World organises long-running pressures as **fronts** composed of **Dangers**. A Danger is a structured bundle:

| Field | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Ambitious organisations / planar forces / arcane enemies / hordes / cursed places |
| **Identity** | Specific instance within the type (misguided good, thieves guild, cult) |
| **Impulse** | What the danger ultimately wants (to do what is right no matter the cost; to take by subterfuge; to infest from within) |
| **Moves** | How the danger likes to behave toward its enemies (attack by stealth, observe, ambush) |
| **Grim portents** | A 5–6 step sequence of events if unchecked — how the situation advances without player intervention |
| **Impending doom** | End state if the Danger wins (the peaceful village enslaved, the dead reigning over the living) |

- **Dangers are like programming classes** — a pre-defined structure filled in however you like, always doing the same job. Whenever the GM is unsure what happens next, they consult each Danger: what's its instinct? Should I advance its grim portents? What Moves could I make?
- **Design takeaway for video games** — expose similarly narrative-heavy categories as content blocks. The framework multiplies improvisational output without pre-writing every permutation.

## 4. Failing Forward and Snowballing

- **Failing forward** — when players fail, the story should advance, not stop. Failed lockpick in classic D&D just blocks the door; in Dungeon World, the GM responds with a Move ("You hear the heavy breathing and thudding footsteps of an ogre coming down the passage behind you").
- **Moves snowball** — each Move escalates from the last. A flock of crows → the cult steals a key guard contact → the cult keeps tabs on players through carrion crows → Cult Leader Sylvar himself ambushes the party.
- **Other systems in the same family** — **Apocalypse World** (progenitor of the Powered-by-the-Apocalypse system), **Blades in the Dark**, **Stars Without Number** (by Kevin Crawford — generates entire star systems from die rolls and even plays out events between sessions).

## 5. Applying the Lessons

- **The West Marches hexcrawl** — Lumpkin's preferred D&D style. Players explore a hex grid; encounter tables (weighted) determine what they meet in plains, forest, swamp. Pre-seeded with orcs, living plants, undead crocodiles, static storms, murders of crows cawing the names of the recently dead.
- **Emergent narrative in play** — a recent session had players hunting a trio of lizards worshipping a false god (a randomly rolled boast from a magical drinking horn). Random encounters drained resources (four Orcs, then a van-sized burrowing Ankheg). With spells dwindling, a player negotiated with the next random encounter — a set of living plants — whose motivation Lumpkin invented on the fly: securing the primacy of plant life by killing flesh-bound creatures. The party exchanged safe passage for killing a basilisk placed in an adjacent zone during prep. Crossing that adjacent zone revealed old statues of petrified adventurers; players dug a pit trap and killed the basilisk (one party member turned to stone — future plot hook). What players don't yet know: the basilisk *was* the false god the lizards were worshipping.
- **Why it works** — the randomness meets the GM's knowledge of the prepared world and the players' depleted resources. The brain, a pattern-recognition engine, pulls narrative justifications out of the randomness (the plants hate something nearby; the wolves follow because players have been smoking bear meat).
- **Design principle** — procgen at the table is strongest when it drives against the mechanics into interesting decisions and inspires **eureka moments of spontaneous justification**. Strong ties between generated content and the rest of the world (motivations, instincts, goals for every entity) build a web of connections that make random results feel intuitive.
- **Closing advice** — passionate storytellers should try a TTRPG. Even at worst it's a window into how your brain (and your players') build stories out of disjointed facts.

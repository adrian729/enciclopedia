## 1. Thesis

*Procedural Storytelling in Game Design*, edited by Tanya X. Short and Tarn Adams (2019), is a collection of essays from working designers, writers, and researchers who have all built or shipped systems that mix human authorship with procedural logic. Its argument is not that generated stories can or should replace authored ones, but that the two collaborate. Procedural systems produce **plot** — actions and events — while the player supplies the **story** — the sequencing, prose, and motivation that turn those events into something worth retelling. The contributors repeatedly land on the same observation: the player's pattern-recognition brain is the actual engine of meaning, and the designer's job is to feed it the right scaffolding.

Across five sections — Introduction, Structure and Systems, Worlds and Context, Characters, and Resources — the book builds a craft vocabulary. It distinguishes the tools of generation (grammars, constraint solvers, rule systems, tag-based selection, agent simulation) from the tools of curation (story daemons, knowledge models, dirty narrative, failing forward). It insists throughout that the point is neither novelty nor infinite content but the **emergent narrative** that occurs when a player recognises themselves, a character, or a theme inside a dynamic system.

## 2. Foundations

### 2.1. What Makes a Generator

Kate Compton's opening chapter is the book's map. Before any technical choice, the designer should name the **artifact** the generator will make and list the properties a good one has and the constraints a bad one violates. That list defines a **possibility space** — the set of all artifacts the generator *can* produce — which must be biased toward good and away from bad, with enough range that repeated outputs don't feel identical. The **Artist-in-a-box** approach, developed on *Spore*, starts by sitting with someone who hand-makes the artifact and capturing their process, vocabulary, and trade-offs.

Compton enumerates the major generative methods: **distribution** (monsters scattered across a map, loot rolled from tables), **parametric methods** (Perlin/Simplex noise, Voronoi, diamond-square, *No Man's Sky* creatures), **tile-based** approaches (*Settlers of Catan*, *Civilization*, 18th-century musical dice games), **grammars** (Compton's own **Tracery** library), **constraint solvers** (answer-set solving, Horswill's **Craft**), and **agents and simulations** (Boids, Braitenberg vehicles, genetic algorithms, cellular automata — the technique behind *Dwarf Fortress* and *Minecraft*). Each method suits different needs; grammars handle structural depth but not cross-cutting constraints, distribution handles options but not structure, and so on.

### 2.2. Why Generators Fail

Compton names the **10,000 Bowls of Oatmeal problem**: a generator can produce mathematically unique outputs that feel identical to a player. Mathematical uniqueness is not perceptual uniqueness. Humans respond to evidence of process and forces (soil pushed up at a tree's base, grass sheltering beside a gravestone). Kevin Lynch's *Image of the City* and the general principle "most background should be drab so a few memorable artifacts stand out" set the bar.

Darius Kazemi's chapter doubles down on restraint. Brian Reynolds' advice when building AI for *Rise of Nations* was to pick a decision *out of a hat at random* and see how it plays — then complicate only if it fails. *Spelunky*'s celebrated level generator hides a one-line treasure rule (chance of treasure is proportional to adjacent solid surfaces) and a 30-line kludge for spider placement; both are simple, both shipped. Players invent complexity where there is none — Kazemi's *Two Headlines* Twitter bot is assumed to use NLP, but is actually a find-and-replace scraper. **Context** turns trivial into impressive: the *"You Must Be"* generator wraps a random dictionary definition in a pickup-line template. And if a Mario-style level generator keeps producing unjumpable gaps, the fix is often a run button or rising lava, not the generator.

Jill Murray's chapter argues that narrative designers are over-obsessed with structure because structure is measurable. What's missing is "a Thorough Investigation of the Human Condition." Her *Bad Dreams* collection of Ubisoft-era IF structures all became prisons; love of the character Aveline de Grandpré, not structural cleverness, carried *Assassin's Creed: Liberation* through production. When she later experimented with sparse character lists — eleven lines including *Name*, *Age*, *Dominant trait*, *Strongest childhood memory*, *Emotional need* — readers filled in stories themselves. Detail beyond that threshold turned Nat back into oatmeal.

### 2.3. The Ethics of Generating

Michael Cook's chapter frames the ethical dimension. A generator is a *little digital apprentice* that encodes the designer's opinions and runs them in every player's home. A gravestone generator using separate male and female name lists silently declares that only heterosexual couples marry in this world; the rule of thumb is *don't introduce distinctions that don't mean anything*. Importing live data amplifies the risk: *ConceptNet* includes the "fact" that women are sluts; Cook's *A Rogue Dream* used **Google milking** (Tony Veale's technique of harvesting knowledge from autocomplete of *"why do X…"* queries) and collapsed when queries like *"why do Muslims"* returned hate speech. Microsoft's *Tay* bot learned to parrot racism within hours. Dualist banlists, restricted vocabulary, and treating players as potential adversaries are mitigations.

Cook also audits the language of procgen marketing. *"A new gaming experience every time"* (from the *Diablo* design document), *"infinite replayability"*, *"17.5 million guns"* (Guinness-certified for *Borderlands*) are euphemisms for *"we shuffled a deck"*. Experienced players translate automatically; newcomers inherit the hype as literal promise. *Spelunky*'s modest *"the walls are shifting"* line describes what is actually happening, and does more for trust.

Jimmy Maher's retrospective on *Murder on the Zinderneuf* (1983) — Jon Freeman and Paul Reiche III's procedurally generated airship mystery — completes the foundations. It is a clever system with eight master stories that replay visibly quickly. The game's limits (48 K of memory, manual-dependent character briefings, Lovecraftian cults that repeat every few playthroughs) show how a working generator can still fail to sustain a genre that is fundamentally about fiction.

## 3. Structure and Systems

### 3.1. Keeping Stories Moving

Jon Ingold's chapter on **narrative momentum** argues that the dreaded slump happens when players have nothing to do and the designer has given them nothing to think about. Action games solve this with *only-forwards* corridors dressed up as open worlds. Inkle's **ink** scripting language builds only-forwards directly into its syntax: the **weave** structure flows top-to-bottom, paragraph labels can only be set once and never unset, options default to once-only. *80 Days* shows ~3 % of its 750,000 words per playthrough and prevents backtracking through its time-limited premise.

For *Heaven's Vault*, Ingold added a **knowledge model**: an acyclic directed graph of facts where learning a state also learns every preceding state, and no beat unlearns. Scripts gate their lines with `between(...)` intervals — *"does the player know enough for this to make sense, but not so much as to make it redundant?"*. Two rules follow: no scene may fire outside an acceptable interval, and every scene must move *something* forward. Dialogue becomes the inexhaustible resource: a dedicated conversation button pulls lines from the script based on the current knowledge model, turning the Roomba-style "hoover up hotspots" loop into a detective loop of interact, walk, discuss, deduce.

### 3.2. Curated Plus Generated

Tim Keenan and Benjamin Hill's chapter on *Duskers* faces the opposite tension: how do you put hand-crafted story into a procedural roguelike without killing the player story? Their first attempt — a linear plot bible — fought the game. They found three techniques that made curated fiction bend to the player's unique run. First, **order matters**: they ran a two-log experiment (a scientist worrying about a rat, some techs dismissing the complaint) and confirmed readers anchored to whichever log came first. Duskers fragments lore into logs from many viewpoints across five alternative human extinctions, with recurring corporations (Muteki, Leyland) as anchor points. Second, **let the player drive**: curated storylines are series of objectives reachable in any order or ignorable entirely, surfaced through the AI companion *JIL* who sorts logs. Third, **narrative as a lure**: a pandemic storyline requires the player to lure an organic enemy into the docking bay for scanning, coaxing them to re-create a genuinely emergent tactic the developers had stumbled on during playtesting.

Kevin Snow's *Southern Monsters* blends static and procedural text in a semi-autobiographical Twine game about a south-Arkansas teenager coping with domestic-violence trauma. His shower generator surprised a convention attendee who took nine showers in a row; his cat-video generator picks genre, paragraph shape, and horizontal context (mention snow once and later clauses respect it). Simple systems, iterated until bad output is rare, do the work. Snow's *Memory Blocks* / *Animal Town* uses deliberately uncanny procedural *Animal Crossing* letters to comment on the static letters the player sends the villagers — the uncanny is the point.

Riad Djemili's *Curious Expedition* layers three abstractions: a **world** layer that triggers diary scenes from points of interest or catastrophes, an **event** layer written in declarative syntax (`reqSanity: 20..50`, `charEffects { count: 2, reqCharFlags: +humanoid -special }`), and a **sentence** layer with inline variants (`[He|She] suggested [we set up camp|we should move on]`). The *"trek member falls in love with a villager"* event, by combining different trek members and pronoun variants, produces very different tonal readings — a British soldier falling for a tribal woman, a bearded sailor for a tribal man, a missionary for a woman. Djemili also notes the **NetHack apples lesson**: choking deaths from apples made him assume the code was elaborate; it was six lines. Players fill gaps; consistent scaffolding is enough.

### 3.3. Emergent Narrative from Simple Rules

Tarn Adams' chapter on *Dwarf Fortress* is a design-from-examples manifesto. Produce a plausible player-told snippet — *"A kobold crept into the workshop and stole Urist's masterpiece scepter. Urist was distraught for days"* — and ask *"what's the least I can do to generate more of these?"* Each word loads implications: how are kobolds defined, is there stealth, are items owned, where do names come from, are emotions simulated? Answer only the questions that sit at narrative nexuses; skip the rest. Research is a dead end in *Dwarf Fortress* because it doesn't link to time-tested narratively potent mechanics; engraving is central because it connects to dwarves, walls, historical events, and personality traits. Fallen-fortress stories centre on the last survivor's engraving choice.

Adams emphasises the player's role as the *"official will of the fortress"* — an abstract presence that can order mining and assign functions and names, while dwarves retain autonomy. Exposition is selective: combat deaths appear in the main announcement queue because they matter, but verbose combat reports are opt-in. Between a fully autonomous narrator and complete player freedom sits the sweet spot where players focus, query, and fill in gaps, including mechanics that don't exist.

### 3.4. Systems That Carry Theme

Daniel Cook's chapter on *Triple Town* is the book's honest post-mortem of thematic ambition. He started with Stephane Bura's emotion-engineering framework (variables, velocity, direction, predictability) and observed that the addition of random blocker squares to his merging-grid prototype produced **frustration**, **hostility**, and **defensiveness** — players hated a neutral entity purely because of territory pressure. Seeing colonialism in that hatred, he applied labels: the grid became an island, the upgrading objects became buildings of an Empire, the black squares became peaceful giant bears, removal became a military action. Mechanical changes followed: bears migrated instead of teleporting (making reservations possible), dead bears stacked into gravestones that matched into churches (the balm of religion built from the bones of the locals).

Cook's verdict is mixed. The project succeeded as personal growth and failed as communication. Cuteness and the casual match-3 framing made cognitive dissonance easy to dismiss; few players read the Second Read. Those who did often found the subject matter distasteful for a casual game, and some critics argued the game normalised colonialist pleasure. Cook treats the failure as a reason to iterate — he has rebuilt *Triple Town* four times, and has tried similar two-step emotion-plus-theme design in *Road Not Taken*, *Leap Day*, and *Beartopia*.

Richard Rouse III's *The Church in the Darkness* shows heavy authorship sitting inside a randomised cult. The game's mystery — is this cult radical or apocalyptic? — only works if the answer changes. Rouse randomises the cult leaders' personalities among a small number of discrete starting states so bespoke dialogue can ship for each, then layers on randomised spawn locations, resource placements, and document ordering. The document system is one of the game's most complex: map clues appear in order of increasing detail, Alex's story beats hold their sequence, other characters' stories stay internally ordered but shuffle between sets, and flavour documents scatter. Chapter Titles appear on-screen as the player acts, so they can feel the weight of their decisions afterward in an end log. Rouse is explicit that the choice to build this system was driven by the story, not the tech.

Marta Fijak and Jakub Stokalski's *Frostpunk* chapter charts the three-prototype journey from a Maslovian needs simulation (no agency, no arc) through a Prophet narrative (too separate from city-building) to the Book of Laws tech-tree that finally put long-term strategic pressure on a *society* rather than a *city*. Their central question emerged from coffee-room disagreements about whether killing the Prophet was justified: *"The end justifies the means. … Or does it?"* The **boiling frog** metaphor — gradual normalisation — shaped the law tree so that each step pushes morality only a little further than the last. The end log asks *"Was it worth it?"* and supports the reflective layer the team wanted all along.

## 4. Worlds and Context

### 4.1. Generating History

Jason Grinblat's chapter on *Caves of Qud* builds a model of history as the interplay of **entities** (people, places, objects as bags of properties) and **events** (functions that modify those properties), related by **historical logic** and exposed through **historical accounts**. History has two flavours: as a *process* (the unfolding of rules) and as an *artifact* (a flattened narrative viewed through a contemporary lens). *Qud* chose the artifact angle because its theme is exactly that — engagement with the past without its original context.

Five mythic rulers (**sultans**) each get a handful of core properties, a **domain** (glass, jewels, ice, might, scholarship, chance) that acts like an epithet in epic poetry, and twelve randomly chosen life events. Each event generates a **gospel** via a Tracery-like replacement grammar whose slots reference existing sultan state. The chapter's best example shows the *sieges a city* event rationalising itself backwards: the `#injustice#` slot inspects the sultan's current allied factions; if none produce a suitable cause, the event *creates* one retroactively by adding frogs to the alliance list. This is **historical rationalization** — the effect causes its own cause. *Antixerpur* (domain: ice) is found as a babe with icicles in her hands, assassinates the sultan over a prohibition on encasing things in ice, wields a frosty hammer named *Frostycus Catsfriend*, and leaves cities renamed for being devastated by icy winds. Apophenia — the player's over-perception of patterns — does the narrative lifting.

### 4.2. Generating Place

Bruno Dias' *Voyageur* builds on Emily Short's *Annals of the Parrigues*: one-way travel through procedurally described planets powered by his open-source **Improv** library. Improv is Tracery plus a world-model sanity check. Phrases are grouped with metadata; a **filter stack** assigns **salience** scores and a culling formula (`c = m − 10` for *Voyageur*) decides which score counts as "high enough". Seven filters handle contradictions (don't mention tundra on a desert world), repetition (mark used phrases dry), bias toward desired tags, lensing (each planet is seen through one aspect), and hard-coded per-phrase tweaks.

Dias walks through the trade-offs: the theoretically elegant pure-reincorporation approach (let the corpus dictate world frequency) collapsed under the need to balance an actual economy, so *Voyageur* pre-seeds planets with hard facts and lets Improv handle description. The generator lives on a razor's edge: too random and it produces **bowls of oatmeal**; too salient and it produces what Dias calls the **brutalist building problem** — planets that surface their model so nakedly that all farm worlds feel identical. His final lesson: tune with manual guardrails rather than meta-systems, and accept that much of the knowledge is specific to your content.

### 4.3. Generating in the Physical World

Mx. Lazer-Walker's chapter pulls procedural ideas into real-world space. Immersive theatre already handles procedurality through brute-force authored content (*Sleep No More*'s six-story McKittrick Hotel), through one-on-one scene density (*Then She Fell*), and through framing that co-opts random noise (Janet Cardiff's binaural audio walks). Cardiff's *Her Long-Black Hair* keeps listeners facing forward because the story is about regret and not looking back — design solving what sensors can't.

Their own *Computational Flâneur* generates poetry in Fort Mason, San Francisco. Early prototypes ruled out visual AR (isolates players), head-tracked audio (phone can't distinguish head from body), and movement speed as input (players have no mental model beyond "faster = more intense"). The final piece is judged as a "meditation gong": interesting enough to interrupt your train of thought, not interesting enough to hold you. Neural nets on pre-existing poetry produce templates just real enough, rare enough moments of synchrony with the environment feel like magic. Most players only visit once; physical procgen needs both low-friction return and explicit reasons to come back, and Computational Flâneur had neither.

### 4.4. Dirty Narrative and Authored Dynamism

Alex Epstein's chapter on *We Happy Few* names a concept that ripples through the whole book: **dirty narrative**, storytelling that demands interpretation. The game uses five techniques: **translucent lies** (Joy and the smiling masks you can see through), **absences** (the missing children of Hamlyn Village, Sebastian Dainty's hidey-hole, the anguished letters about an uneaten birthday cake), **mysteries** that should not be fully solved (Foggy Jack and his *"I'm afraid you've come to the end of your time"* cards), **inconsistencies** (the same Arthur-Sally scene at different locations with different explanations across playthroughs), and **tangents** (two bobbies talking about Bobby Hickinbotham going to the Reform Club to be punished for his guilt — which is both a quest hook and a worldbuilding statement about a society that institutionalises guilt).

Dirty narrative is execution-dependent — done poorly, it reads as sloppy; QA will file bugs about "inconsistencies." But games have thousands of assets and the real world has billions, so a naturalistic game needs the player to fill the gap. Generated lore items alone won't do it; good procedural story works like Epstein's Jack-and-Jill example, where two beats mean strong but different things in different orders. Long sequences need hand-authored connective tissue, so real procgen story stays shallow, giving the player the gift of never being sure they have all the facts.

### 4.5. Lessons from the Tabletop

Steven Lumpkin's chapter pulls tabletop RPG technology into the digital conversation. *Dungeon World* (Adam Koebel and Sage LaTorra) equips game masters with three pieces of technology useful to digital designers: **Moves** (narrative-or-mechanical actions chosen from a pre-defined category list like *Show Signs of an Approaching Threat*, *Deal Damage*, *Offer an Opportunity with or Without Cost*), **Dangers** (structured bundles of type + identity + impulse + moves + five-step grim portents + impending doom), and **failing forward** (on a failed roll, the game master advances the fiction with a new Move rather than blocking the player). Moves **snowball** — each escalates from the last. Lumpkin's own *West Marches* hexcrawl shows what this looks like in play: random encounters with Orcs, an Ankheg, and hostile plant creatures collide with the game master's prep (a basilisk in an adjacent zone) and dwindling party resources to produce a negotiation, a pit trap, and a petrified party member — none of which the game master planned.

## 5. Characters

### 5.1. Making Personalities Visible

Tanya X. Short's chapter on generated personalities gathers six tips that recur throughout the book. First, pick a direction: does reasoning drive behaviour or does behaviour reveal reasoning, and which does the player see? Passive media can hide reasoning and let players infer it; procedural games usually surface reasoning because the fun is in learning the systems. Second, personalities are already subtle — hidden systems go unnoticed (Short worked at *Age of Conan: Hyborian Adventures* for weeks without realising every NPC ran a full Maslovian needs hierarchy under the hood), near-average traits read as indistinct (*Crusader Kings II* characters are *Wroth*, not *sometimes irritable*), and passive/avoidant traits (chastity, temperance) produce weak generated personalities unless expressed actively. Third, comedy arrives faster than drama because generated characters are already symbols, and jokes use symbols. Fourth, let players investigate after the fact with logs, rewinds, or in-character queries. Fifth, **reactions** beat actions for personality-per-second because multiple characters can react to the same event simultaneously. Sixth, **change** is powerful — *Darkest Dungeon*'s quirk gains and losses are relatively arbitrary, but players readily invent explanations for the transition from state 1 to state 2.

### 5.2. Characters Without Contradiction

Geoffrey Card, Jørgen Tjernø, and Matthew Bozarth's chapter on *State of Decay 2* faces two failure modes — **blandness** and **contradictions** — and explains their tag-winnowing system. Traits are written in first person ("I used to sleep in a tree to avoid zombies") and carry tags with co-occurrence rules; applying `firefighter` eliminates all other career traits, because *"on a randomly generated character, seeing two different pre-apocalypse jobs doesn't make the character seem interesting and complex — it makes them seem like a mistake."* When a tester spotted a marathon-running couch potato, a new `active`/`inactive` mutually-exclusive pair fixed it without touching content. A hierarchical trait tree lets tags apply to subgroups, so a request to cap starters at low-level skills became drag-and-drop rather than a thousand-trait edit.

Their chapter on cultural identity is one of the book's strongest practical statements on representation. India is internally as diverse as Europe, so a single "Indian" namelist would read as mishmash to Indian players; the team started with one region and had local advisors vet it. A similar red-pen exchange between Spain and Mexico localisation advisors showed that their Spanish nicknames were specifically Mexican and needed their own list. Lao *seu lin* (informal nicknames unrelated to the given name) fit an independent nickname slot already in the data; Ethiopian characters finally felt right to the Ethiopian advisor when the team added the Old Testament name list. The authors' regret is order-of-operations: voice was a late choice and became tightly constrained by mission VO limits, so they'd build all features (not just traits) under a unified tag system and add backtracking when later choices run out of options.

Jongwoo Kim's chapter on *The Shrouded Isle* layers three elements — **family**, **traits**, **aesthetics** — to generate 30 flawed villagers. Each character gets one virtue and one vice (minor or major) with severity up to ±30 on a cult value. Trait reveal states (unrevealed, partial, full) create leap-of-faith gameplay; random Work-phase outcomes (15 %/70 %/15 % for 1.5×/1×/0.5×) make players extrapolate competence from luck. Family opinion mechanics force the player to use deeply flawed advisors at least once per season; the Sacrifice-phase inversion (worse vice → better example) makes players re-examine their dependence on each character. The *Sunken Sins* expansion added **contagions** and the **Purification Tower**, which randomly can transform Major-Virtue characters into the *Awoken* — mute, mushroom-like mutants blessed by Chernobog, needed for a secret ending. Moral dread decays quickly; Kim's post-mortem lists what the team tried and what worked.

### 5.3. Plot Before Story

Adam Saltsman's chapter on *Overland* introduces the book's crispest terminology. LARPers call the post-match telling-and-retelling **froth**; good games generate good froth. The player, not the game, is the story generator — what the game needs is a **plot generator**. Plot is actions and events; story is sequencing, prose, and motivation. All games are already plot generators (Tetris, NBA Jam), but most are repetitive. Early *Overland* was *"smack with stick, look in dumpster"* ad nauseam. The team added atomic actions (Move, Invite, Attack, Loot) for stranger encounters so that *"dupe stranger into giving up items"* emerges from play rather than from a menu. Disasters (wildfires, ambushes, changed minds) become the retellable moments.

None of it paid off emotionally until the art director replaced stoic character models with cute ones. Then **micro-story couplets** — one past, one present, per character, drawn from two independent columns — started to land: *"Skipped prom to watch the new monster movie. Hoping to find a dog."*. Accidentally contradictory couplets (*"Hated camping as a kid. Sleeps under the stars now"*) are the most interesting; the only generative step is the mash-up. Gardner's insight — feelings become real only when they take the form of events — turned out to be the answer to a year of generator experiments.

### 5.4. Dialog as Rules

Elan Ruskin's chapter on *Left 4 Dead* dialog presents the architecture that recurs across the book as "a pile of facts and a set of matching rules." Every dialog query is a flat key-value dictionary merging function parameters, speaker state, speaker memory, and global state. Every rule lists criteria; all rules whose criteria match are candidates; the one with the most criteria wins. Callbacks set once-only flags, increment running-gag counters, and send follow-up events to other characters for back-and-forth. New context keys can be invented by writing to them from a callback; no programmer needs to be involved. Performance tricks (sorted criteria, hierarchical partitioning by speaker, level-scoped rule sets) keep a thousands-of-rules database fast. Ruskin's eight takeaways end with *"most of all: keep it simple"*. The same architecture powers much of the book's other systems: Horneman's *Mainframe* tag-based scene selection, Rouse's *Church in the Darkness* document placement, Ingold's knowledge-interval gates.

## 6. Resources and Practice

Cat Manning's tarot chapter pulls the book out of pure digital. Tarot is a procedural algorithm with 4.675 × 10²¹ possible Celtic Cross spreads that avoids the oatmeal problem because meaning is constructed through overlapping taxonomies — suit, number, Major/Minor Arcana, deck-specific imagery — and through the reader's personal associations with all of them. Manning's worked example (Two of Swords reversed / Ten of Cups / Wheel of Fortune for the question "safe job or fulfilling path?") shows how the same three cards shift drastically when reordered or reversed, and how substituting one card reshapes the entire reading. The narrative-design takeaway is that allowing multiple layers of meaning to co-exist — rather than forcing one to dominate — produces more personal and satisfying stories.

George Buckenham's Twitterbots chapter surveys a form that thrives on short, fragmentary worldbuilding (*@neighbour_civs*), procedural travel guides (*@unfamiliar_city*), generated images (*@soft_landscapes*), Unicode star fields (*@tiny_star_fields*), emoji skeletons (*@skeletonsday*), and the long-running *@ArpObservatory* Atacama-desert alien-signal mystery. Jokes land through the tweet's built-in setup-plus-punchline length (*@vg_erotica*, *@darndesttruisms*). *@everyword* delivered its own joke by running for seven years and then posting *éclair* after *zymurgy* because the sort order put diacritics after 'z'. *@everyletter123* never got past C because "D username message" is Twitter's shorthand for a direct message. Ethical responsibility applies: Everest Pipkin moved to protest bots like *@Abolish_ICE_Now*, which lists US detention facilities with Spanish translations. The transparency of broken and template-visible bots, Buckenham argues, makes it easier to take them seriously as independent entities.

Emily Short's closing chapter on tools brings the book to its practical landing. The first five minutes matter — new authors need to compile and play quickly (Inform, Character Engine, Twine all do this). The sculptural authoring mode, where the writer plays until a better response occurs to them and inserts it in place, needs tight replay cycles and the ability to duplicate state into a new node (as *Alabaster* did, switching to authoring mode when a player asked a question with no answer). Adding variety must be balanced against content quality — more variants can lower average output by diluting specificity. Crowd-sourced and web-sourced data (Scheherazade's Mechanical Turk stories, Jeff Orkin's *Restaurant Game*, DBpedia) can seed systems with content, but usually require curation.

Short warns against building GUIs too early (they lock in data structures before you know them), against designing only for small datasets (they never scale cleanly), and against the **Founder Principle** — the first significant works made with a tool lock future work into their shape, so ship diverse examples early. Hacks need first-class support; complexity cost is a real tax on content volume; no tool is ready until two or three sizeable projects have shipped. Porting existing work to a new tool exposes both strengths and gaps. Budget ongoing iteration — *Inform 7* has been refined for two decades. Let the tool help authors test dead ends, unreachable content, and average playthrough length. And expect authors to be forgetful: no one holds the whole branching structure of an interactive narrative in their head for long.

## 7. Key Takeaways

- **The player is the story generator; the game should be a plot generator.** Emergent narrative happens in the player's head when the system gives them enough scaffolding to assemble meaning.
- **Simple beats clever.** Brian Reynolds' random-out-of-a-hat rule, Spelunky's one-line treasure generator, and the most-criteria-wins dialog engine all argue that a plain system shipped beats a sophisticated one stalled.
- **Context turns trivial into impressive.** A dictionary-definition generator becomes a joke generator in the right frame. Lean into what your generator is actually good at; don't sell what it isn't.
- **The 10,000 Bowls of Oatmeal problem is real.** Mathematical uniqueness is not perceptual uniqueness. Design for perceived memorability, not combinatorial scale.
- **Only forwards is the most reliable momentum engine.** Gate content with knowledge-state intervals; make every action advance something in the model; let dialogue be the inexhaustible resource.
- **Dirty narrative demands interpretation and rewards it.** Absences, translucent lies, inconsistencies, and tangents ask the player to do the cognitive work that naturalism needs, which is the only way a handful of assets can stand in for a whole world.
- **Generators encode values whether you mean them to or not.** Don't split name lists by gender if gender doesn't matter. Don't import live web data without banlists. Don't oversell procgen in marketing language newcomers will read literally.
- **Characters become real through constraints, not through parameters.** Tag-winnowing systems, mutually exclusive traits, and first-person trait text make characters feel like people rather than coordinates in a space.
- **Comedy arrives before drama.** Generated characters are symbols; symbols are what jokes run on. Plan for laughs early and drama late.
- **Tools are never done.** Ship diverse examples, accommodate hacks, budget iteration, and watch real authors use the system before you trust it.

## 1. Anatomy of a Personality

Short breaks personality into four components split between **reasoning** (the why) and **behaviour** (the what):

| Side | Components | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Reasoning** | Motivations | needs, desires, virtues, vices, values, moods, impulses, patterns |
| **Reasoning** | Knowledge | memories, experiences, understandings, beliefs |
| **Behaviour** | Relationships | friends, enemies, strangers, objects, self |
| **Behaviour** | Abilities | verbs, vocabulary, skills, limitations |

Pick a reasoning + a behaviour and you've "generated a personality". Whether it's *interesting* — whether the connections are perceptible and satisfying — is a separate craft problem.

## 2. Tip 1 — Define the Interpretation Process

- **"Show don't tell" is character-judgment** — the player watches behaviour and infers reasoning (*"Arron usually tells the truth, therefore he is honest"*). Works in passive media (novels, *Uncharted*).
- **System-driven games tend to "tell then show"** — they expose traits up-front and let players map them to behaviour (*Crusader Kings II*, *Black & White*, *Dwarf Fortress*). Learning the systems *is* the gameplay, and hiding causality behind fuzzy interpretation frustrates that appetite.
- **The Shrouded Isle** — each villager has a virtue + vice that may be hidden, partially revealed, or fully revealed. Once the virtue is known, the player can factor it into any new accusation event about that character.
- **Moon Hunters (reversed direction)** — the game inferred players' behaviour into mythic hero traits (*cunning*, *foolish*) rather than assigning them. Works, but might have felt stronger if players could also influence the reasoning.
- **Pick a direction and commit** — decide whether behaviour determines personality or personality determines behaviour, and how each is exposed to the player, before applying the rest of these tips.

## 3. Tip 2 — Personalities Are Already Subtle

Physical cues (faces, eyes, colour signals) tap biology. Personalities are cultural, 4-dimensional, and interpretation-dependent. Players default to assuming invisible personality is missing. Normal storytelling subtlety backfires three ways:

- **"Secret" systems are a risk** — *Age of Conan: Hyborian Adventures* (2008) modelled every NPC on a Maslovian hierarchy of needs (safety, hunger, sleep, socialising, self-actualising). Short played 40 hours pre-hire without noticing; she only saw it as "nice scripting flourishes" around soldiers eating, drinking, chatting. The simulation was sophisticated and invisible. *Dwarf Fortress* exposes aggressively for this reason; *Civilization*'s rulers have grown coyer but still hint at hidden traits to prime curiosity.
- **Subtle behaviour patterns are a risk** — real psychology (Big 5) produces mostly-average personalities with one or two mild extremes. Mirroring that makes most characters forgettable. *Crusader Kings II* characters are *Wroth*, not *sometimes irritable*. Archetypes already lean extreme — Ravenclaws aren't "kinda smart".
- **Beware passivity and non-action** — a character who *wants to do* something reads more clearly than one who *wants to not do* something. Chastity and temperance, defined by abstention, make weak generated personalities. *King of Dragon Pass*'s most successful personality type was the trickster (flamboyant, proactive) among measured rivals. If your design calls for a chaste character, express it actively: recoil from sexual content, shame flirters, rant against indecency, actively avoid temptation.

## 4. Tip 3 — Comedy Is Close at Hand

- **Don't try to fool the player** — players know the characters are system-driven; don't sell roller skates to someone who wants a bicycle.
- **Drama takes hours of system-learning** — players need to internalise the systems before they can map humanity onto characters. *Dwarf Fortress* may never have become popular if its core loop had been personality-driven instead of survivalist; the emotional payoff came 40+ hours in.
- **Jokes arrive faster** — generated characters are already symbols of people, exactly the setup jokes use: *"A/an (insertnoun) and a/an (insertnoun) walk into a bar…"*. Surprising outcomes + symbolic agents = comedy lands before drama can.

## 5. Tips 4–6 — Investigation, Reactions, Change

- **Tip 4: Allow after-the-fact investigation** — players miss things. Provide logs, journals of actions, the ability to rewind and watch events from another actor's POV, or in-fiction investigation (ask characters questions). Adds scope, but the gameplay *is* about personality anyway.
- **Tip 5: Reactions ≥ actions** — *"It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters"* (Epictetus). Reactions let you compare multiple personalities *in parallel* instead of sequentially. A crowd reacting to a misfortune surfaces personalities at a glance. Caveat: reactions are high-expectation — human priorities are messy (when a character dies, does the AI care most that it hated the victim, that it's a kind person, that a judgmental friend is in the room, that the death was embarrassing, that the murderer was the character's lover, etc.?). Whatever priority order you pick, use it constantly and it communicates personality even when the character isn't the protagonist.
- **Tip 6: Change is powerful** — transformations can be more compelling than the personalities themselves. Adversity makes people weaker, stronger, or weirder. *Darkest Dungeon* hinges on quirk gains/losses, which designer Tyler Sigman confirmed are relatively arbitrary with broad mission-content filters. The player only needs (state 1, trigger, state 2) to invent their own narrative explanation for the shift.

## 6. Summary

- **Chicken or egg** — commit to a direction (reasoning → behaviour or behaviour → reasoning) and communicate it.
- **Don't hide too much** — invisible systems go unnoticed.
- **Avoid "normal"** — extremes are easier to see and understand.
- **Avoid passivity** — actions read; non-actions don't.
- **Embrace comedy** — AIs were never human; use that.
- **Give out the magnifying glass** — investigation tools help players decode what happened.
- **Reactions outpace actions** — cheaper, parallel comparison.
- **Transformations are gold** — strong change is compelling on its own, even if the underlying traits are subtle.

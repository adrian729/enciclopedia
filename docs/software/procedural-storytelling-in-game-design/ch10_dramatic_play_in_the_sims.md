## 1. What Dramatic Play Is

- **Dramatic gameplay** — a distinct mode of play, common in LARPing, acting, action figures, children's pretend play. Rare in video games. Kline distinguishes it from tactics-and-progression "roleplaying". Four fundamentals:

  1. **Take on a role** — empathy as play; players suppress their own knowledge or desires to inhabit someone else.
  2. **Make the role personal** — active choice along multiple axes (looks, clothes, traits, skills, careers, relationships).
  3. **Perform the role** — expressive verbs, many available choices (including clearly inappropriate ones), acting that feels free not preset.
  4. **The game recognizes the role** — responses from NPCs, world, and systems acknowledge the chosen role. Reinforcement encourages more play.
- **The Sims** — over 100 million games and expansions across two decades; its success comes from nailing all four. Players adopt different roles for each Sim and switch between first and third person when talking about them.

## 2. Design Techniques in The Sims

- **Go broad** — *The Sims 4* has shipped 22 packs post-launch (Cats & Dogs, Get to Work, Parenthood, Jungle Adventure, Vampires, etc.). Runs counter to "elegant, tight design" orthodoxy, but dramatic play needs a *critical mass* of choices and orthogonal-ish content.
- **Fail forward** — if failure is just reload-and-retry, players stop performing and start grinding. Instead, failure should change the world: a cooking fire, a social faux pas, an office mistake. Aim for ≥20 % of choices to fail forward. Death is a failing-forward example: surviving Sims mourn, can plead with Grim to revive the dead. *"Never assume things will work the same way twice"*.
- **Super successes** — recognize role mastery (professional chef making a masterpiece), but less impactful than failing forward because "getting a bonus" is less interesting than something disruptive.
- **Story surprises** — *"A hot guy randomly walks up to your Sim — what do you do?"* sparks an instinctive role-driven reaction (flirt, slap, apologize to a jealous wife). Surprises generate call-to-action, expose character "but then" moments, push players out of ruts, teach what's possible (this is how Sims players discover romance as a system), and keep players curious.
- **Things to watch** — watching slows play, disengages the twitch mind, opens creative thinking. Zoomed-in + watching = dramatic play active; speeding time or ignoring characters = disengaged. Support it with expressive animations, thought balloons, and variable outcomes.
- **Reactions** — the world should gradually mirror the character. *The Sims 4* plays 1–3 extra back-and-forth responses during key interactions (a parent scolding a toddler) even at the cost of blocking the player's next action. Nearby Sims react to major events. Interactive objects let the role show in the environment.
- **Realistic fantasies** — familiar archetypes are instant hooks. Modern suburban life is naturally personal; fantasy jargon is harder to relate to. *The Sims* goes all-in on modern life, and cases like "Alice and Kev" in *Sims 3* show players telling personal stories the designer never planned.
- **Transgressive play** — taboo and forbidden roles are powerful for identity exploration: LGBTQ relationships, transgender characters, interracial romance, vampires, witches. Even "pet owner" can be transgressive for a child forbidden a pet, or "young mother" for a teenager first thinking about sex and pregnancy. Ask: what real-life issues might players want to explore?
- **Low challenge** — heavy punishment or skill-gated success ends role play. Mastery in dramatic play is in performing the role and knowing the game, not combos or bosses. Balance can be loose — the businessman Sim should get rich, the rich-person role opens up, the player keeps playing.
- **Player commitments** — lightweight quests keep players focused. *Sims 3* Wishes and Promises let players choose four wishes to commit to; a lifetime wish (based on traits) gives an ultimate "I win" goal.
- **Dramatic currency** — lifetime satisfaction points in *Sims 3* are spent on "cheat-y" traits/objects. Attach rewards to the role, not just the player.
- **Abstraction and humor** — dramatic play tolerates heavy abstraction (Sims teleport, spin into new clothes, make friends in hours, "Woohoo"). As long as the role can be performed and the world reacts, the game gets away with shortcuts. Leaning into their absurd humour helps players accept them.

## 3. Traits for Three Purposes

- **Different needs → different trait designs**:

| Purpose | What it does | Good traits |
|---|---|---|
| **NPC** | Emphasize social interactions or NPC behaviors; must be visible or interactable | *Evil* (classic) |
| **Player identity** | Define the role at character creation; engage fantasy | *Cheerful, Music Lover, Romantic, Aspirations* |
| **Dramatic gameplay** | Express the character moment-to-moment | *Bro* (visible in pie menu during socials), *Lazy* (visible when sleeping), *Loves Outdoors* (happy when outside) |

- **Don't force one trait to do all three jobs** — an elegant unified system can restrict content. Enable as many roles as possible over purity.
- **Orthogonality is optional** — useful for dramatic gameplay archetypes, less for NPC/player-identity needs.
- **Communicate what a trait is doing** — Sims 4 puts trait icons on pie-menu buttons, adds tooltips when choices change because of a trait, gives random buffs with explanations. Without feedback, players forget traits exist.

## 4. Anti-Patterns

- **Narrative and cut scenes** — the authorial voice puts players in a passive mode and blocks personalising/performing the role.
- **High challenge, fast pace, difficult controls** — engage the optimisation brain, eat all attention, block performance.
- **Fixed characters with prescribed verbs** — a preset character can be okay (the player opts in buying the game), but during play dramatic players need range beyond looks. Without it, dramatic play morphs into follow-the-breadcrumbs linear play.
- **Where to look for more** — Stardew Valley, Middle Earth: Shadow of Mordor, RimWorld, Mass Effect, SimCity, Dwarf Fortress, Skyrim. Indie RPGs *Fiasco* and *Blades in the Dark* show that pen-and-paper's emergent storytelling is about *playing to explore a role*, not about the Game Master.
- **Closing thesis** — dramatic play is fundamental to how humans learn and grow. *The Sims* is the tip of a deep iceberg.

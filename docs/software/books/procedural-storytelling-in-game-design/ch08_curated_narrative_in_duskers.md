## 1. Player Story vs. Curated Story

- **Duskers** — sci-fi roguelike where the player pilots drones into derelict ships via a retro command-line interface, rebuilding the cause of a universal extinction. Every death resets the universe.
- **Player story** — the unique story each player recounts from emergent play (the crew you suffocated in *FTL*, the cannibalism arc in *Sunless Sea*). Doesn't need to rival literature; its uniqueness *is* the value.
- **Curated story** — hand-crafted content carries craftsmanship and emotion, but when forced onto players it robs games of what makes them games.
- **Initial mistake** — the team tried to structure a linear plot bible with a grounded protagonist. The linearity fought the procedural play loop. They were trying to *control* the game, not set-dress it.
- **Goal** — curated narrative that conforms to the player story rather than the other way around. Three axes: same content must mean different things to different players; content must respond to player choices; narrative must push gameplay in novel directions.

## 2. Order Matters

- **Her Story as proof** — identical static video snippets produce different experiences depending on the order players find them in.
- **Two-log experiment** — Duskers team wrote a scientist's concerned log about a dead rat near a food replicator and a pair of support techs dismissing such complaints. Served them in random order on a website, then asked how concerning the rat was.
- **Result** — readers anchored to the first perspective. Those reading the scientist's email first leaned "very concerning"; those reading the techs first leaned "slightly". The trend blurred with more data, but the direction held.
- **Applied to Duskers** — narrative split into many short ship logs from different perspectives, plus five storylines for how humanity ended. Players find them in whatever order gameplay dictates. Pillars of recurring subjects (corporations like *Muteki* and *Leyland*, recurring characters, consistent existential threats) gave players anchor points so the fragmented narrative could still connect.
- **Backstory messages** — originally meant to deliver in order, but after experimenting they shuffled all middle messages (keeping only the first and last fixed). Every player gets a differently-ordered backstory.
- **Isolation pillar** — one of the game's emotional pillars. Logs from someone who seems to know the player remind them they have no one to talk to except their drones.

## 3. Let the Player Drive

- **Gone Home lesson** — environmental storytelling gives the player agency over what to look at and when. Each player becomes the seed number for the experience.
- **Her Story's search bar** — players control the ordering via their own curiosity (follow a character or follow a location).
- **Duskers' objective structure** — curated storylines are series of objectives the player can attempt in any order, or ignore. Example: to test the cosmic-event pandemic theory, commandeer an old ship and deliver it to a research station for scanning with a special algorithm. The *how* is entirely procedural — what ship, which enemies, which equipment, where the station is.
- **JIL the AI companion** — analyses found logs; when it identifies an objective, it stores that log in a specific folder. Unobtrusive objective system that fits the in-fiction framing.
- **Narrative as persistent element** — logs are kept across runs even though the universe resets. The player remembers them, so the fiction should too. Fits the realism design pillar (the real computer screen *is* the in-game computer screen).

## 4. Narrative as a Lure

- **A Virus Named TOM precedent** — the best achievements forced players to play in entirely new ways instead of rewarding normal play. Can narrative do the same?
- **Alien-in-the-docking-bay story** — the team once accidentally taxied an alien from ship to ship inside their mothership's docking bay. Later they reproduced the trick intentionally to clear an enemy from a path. Most players would never stumble into this.
- **Dr. J. Holmes pandemic arc** — designed an objective where the player must lure an organic enemy into the docking bay to scan it with a downloaded algorithm, then find Holmes's ship for analysis. The narrative motivates the unusual docking-bay tactic.
- **Player report (Reddit, Nathin)** — shows both the emergent tactic ("cunning plan to capture a quarantined biological enemy in my docking bay") and the way curated narrative coloured the player's self-told story ("Dammit, I might die, I might run out of fuel, but I was gonna burn what I had if it gave me a chance to deliver that sample"). The curated arc didn't replace the player story; it enriched it.

## 5. Takeaway

- **Fragment and randomise order** — start. Small step, big effect.
- **Give players agency over the order of discovery** — deepens the link between player and curated narrative.
- **Integrate narrative with gameplay, then push gameplay in new directions** — the curated story starts to live inside the player story. Players on forums embed the fiction back into their own retellings (*"'for science' I tepidly boarded the ship, and you won't believe what happened…"*), which was the authors' highest aim.

## 1. Emergent Narrative

- **Opening anecdote** — streamers playing *Caves of Qud* meet Ugo, a procedurally generated snapjaw faction leader loved by snapjaws, hated by mollusks "for casting doubt on their beliefs," and admired by antelopes "for sharing freshwater with them." From these tags they invent a feud, cross-reference their own bad antelope reputation, and ultimately empty their canteen as a defiant gesture before being killed.
- **The designers did not author Ugo's story** — they set the stage. Emergent narratives belong specifically to procedural games; they are personal and unrepeatable.
- **Authentic because scriptless** — emergent stories result from the collision of systems rather than an author's script, so they mirror everyday life and historical anecdote. Ian Bogost's **procedural rhetoric** frames games as making arguments through rule-based representations; emergent stories are manifestations of those arguments.

## 2. The Voice in the Machine

- **Designer concern** — losing authorial voice (theme, tone, mood) when handing storytelling over to systems.
- **Storyline vs story volume** — the traditional author crafts a single storyline through narrative space; the procedural designer crafts a **story volume** that encloses a family of adjacent emergent stories sharing themes or mood. The volume is not arbitrary; it is bounded by carefully curated system parameters.
- **Where voice lives** — themes and mood are inserted both into the rules of generative systems and into the handcrafted atoms that get combined. *Caves of Qud* embeds themes about morphology (mollusk beliefs) and resource scarcity (water as currency), so the streamers' freshwater-wasting only reads as defiant in that world.
- **Darkest Dungeon example** — afflictions that party members accrue from traumatic events constrain the narrative space to stories that explore the effects of trauma and the mood of hopelessness; success is framed as overcoming afflictions, not avoiding them.

## 3. Fiasco Case Study

- **Fiasco** — Bully Pulpit Games, 2009. A collaborative tabletop RPG simulating "the caper-gone-wrong subgenre," drawing heavily from Coen brothers films and their themes of human stupidity and overreaching ambition. Short sessions, role-play over rules, no game master.
- **Procedural setup** — players roll a pool of d6s and consult tables of plot/character elements (locations, relationships, needs), choosing entries by matching dice to table indices. Each player must share a need and a relationship with each neighbor. Then they role-play scenes; halfway through they re-roll for the **Tilt**, an unexpected event that derails the story and cannot be avoided.
- **Rules that shape the volume** — requiring a need plus a relationship per neighbor and constraining choice via dice forces conflicting, sour-prone setups. The Tilt deliberately introduces conditions misaligned with character goals.
- **Curated atoms** — example tables (Tales from Suburbia, Work and Crime relationships) include "business rivals in a dying industry," "service worker and client," "embezzler and company accountant," "drug manufacturer and dealer." These are chosen for built-in tension; combined with a Tilt like "someone develops a conscience," tragedy is almost guaranteed. Fiasco's rules and content together enforce the theme: "a game of powerful ambition and poor impulse control."

## 4. Unpacking Your Themes

- **First step** — identify themes, which is harder than it sounds for designers driven by intuitions ("a strong-willed and principled mollusk"). Examine the intuition, find the kernel, formulate it as a broader theme, then design systems that produce a story volume around it.
- **Caves of Qud morphology theme** — inspired by the Gamma World tabletop RPG (TSR, 1978) and its absurd mutant cast. The character creation system uses combinable mutations (carapace, wings, telepathy). The kernel of the mollusk-belief intuition turned out to be "our relationship with our morphology" — anthropomorphizing then deanthropomorphizing alien creatures to examine our own embodiment.
- **Caves of Qud scarcity theme** — water-as-currency began as a riff on roguelike hunger/thirst, then became a way to model dependency and the social dynamics that emerge from it. These two themes together generated the Ugo network of relationships.

## 5. Grist for the Narrative Mill

- **Small details enable narration** — character and place names, item descriptions, level layouts, and sound effects all serve as raw material that helps players narrativize their experience.
- **Why Ugo has a name** — mechanically a name is superfluous, but it implies culture and acts as a narrative springboard. Stories need characters; naming positions Ugo as one. Emergent narratives surface more readily when their subjects look like narrative components.
- **Detail shapes the volume** — Ugo's "calloused tot-eater" title evokes mood; combined with snapjaw aggression and water scarcity, the player anticipates conflict. His admirable freshwater gift to antelopes adds texture, sketching a complex figure whose interactions take on meaning beyond "monster in a dungeon."

## 1. Interview stages

- **Screening interview** — one or two phone (or in-person for top-school students) rounds before on-site. Bar is as high as in-person; usually involves coding and algorithms.
- **Coding medium** — online synchronized editor, pen-and-paper with read-back over phone, take-home "homework," or emailed code after the call.
- **On-site round** — 4–6 in-person interviews, one usually over lunch.
- **Lunch interview** — typically non-technical, interviewer often does not submit feedback; good opportunity to discuss interests and ask about company culture.
- **Other on-site interviews** — mostly technical (coding + algorithm), with some resume questions mixed in.
- **Post-interview** — interviewers meet to discuss and submit written feedback. Recruiter responds within about a week.
- **No response** — at major tech companies, silence does *not* mean rejection; always follow up.

## 2. Where questions come from

- **No central list** — interviewer training is minimal (half day at Google, mostly on legal do-nots and how to handle troublesome candidates). No official question list, no topics to avoid.
- **Each interviewer picks their own pool** — usually five or so questions, borrowed from their own past interviews, colleagues, the internet (including CareerCup.com), or tweaked from existing questions.
- **"Recent" questions are a myth** — questions don't change much because nobody at the company is telling interviewers what to do.
- **Company differences are broad, not specific** — web-based companies lean toward system design; DB-heavy companies toward database questions. Most questions fall into "data structures and algorithms" and could be asked anywhere.

## 3. Preparation timeline

The book's "preparation map" breaks prep into phased milestones. Earlier phases build the raw material; later phases are about practice and polish.

| Phase | Focus |
|-------|-------|
| **1+ years before** | Build projects outside school/work, expand network, build website/portfolio; professionals focus on "meaty" projects, students find internships and take classes with large class projects |
| **3–12 months** | Keep building projects, draft resume and get it reviewed, make target company list, read intro sections of CtCI, practice in Java or C++, form mock-interview group, mini-projects to solidify concepts |
| **1–3 months** | Do several mock interviews, continue practicing, track mistakes you've made |
| **4 weeks** | Create interview prep grid; review/update resume |
| **1 week** | Re-read CtCI intros (Tech & Behavioral), do another mock interview, continue practicing (writing code on paper), re-read 5 Algorithm Approaches, buy headset for phone screens, dry-clean outfit for on-site, rehearse stories from prep grid |
| **Day before** | Review list of mistakes; rehearse each story from prep grid once; continue to practice |
| **Day of** | Review Powers of 2 List; print 10 resumes for on-site; eat breakfast; "be confident (not cocky)" |
| **After** | Write thank-you note to recruiter; check in after one week if no response; if no offer, ask when you can re-apply |

## 4. Evaluation

- **Four stated factors** — prior experience, culture fit, coding skills, analytical ability. Most weight goes to coding and analytical.
- **Prior experience** — rarely a direct deciding factor at big tech, but impressive resume talk biases the interviewer to overlook later mistakes ("Wow, she's brilliant!" effect).
- **Culture fit** — matters more at smaller companies; may surface indirectly (e.g., if the culture is independent decision-making and you need direction).
- **Not binary right/wrong** — evaluation is holistic: optimality of final solution, time to get there, cleanliness of code.
- **Evaluated in comparison to other candidates** — a harder question allows more mistakes; an easy question expects an optimal fast answer.
- **"Flawless" is essentially never seen** — in thousands of Google hiring packets the author saw only one flawless set; even hired candidates made mistakes.
- **Arrogance / defensiveness kills offers** — a candidate who blames the interviewer or pushes back rudely is flagged as a red flag, regardless of technical skill.
- **Resume prep is high-leverage** — not the most important factor, but "great bang for your buck."
- **Hiring committees are swayed by loud voices** — decision-making isn't perfectly fair; there is randomness in both performance and committee dynamics.
- **Rejection is not permanent** — candidates can typically reapply after 6–12 months; many rejected candidates later get offers.

## 5. Dress code

Rule of thumb: **dress one small notch better than the employees in your position.** Software engineering and testing roles dress less formally; Program Manager / Dev Lead / business-adjacent roles lean dressier.

|        | Start-Ups | Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Facebook, e.t.c. | Non-Tech Companies (including banks) |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Men    | Khakis, slacks, or nice jeans. Polo shirt or dress shirt. | Khakis, slacks, or nice jeans. Polo shirt or dress shirt. | Suit, no tie. (Consider bringing a tie just in case.) |
| Women  | Khakis, slacks, or nice jeans. Nice top or sweater. | Khakis, slacks, or nice jeans. Nice top or sweater. | Suit, or nice slacks with a nice top. |

## 6. Top 10 mistakes

1. **Practicing on a computer** — like training for an ocean swim in a pool. Practice with pen and paper; use a compiler only to verify *after* hand-testing.
2. **Not rehearsing behavioral questions** — interviewers judge these too, and behavioral weakness biases perception of technical performance. Rehearse stories from projects and positions.
3. **Not doing a mock interview** — silent rehearsal is like rehearsing a speech only in your head. Grab another engineer and trade mock interviews.
4. **Trying to memorize solutions** — won't help on new problems. Struggling through problems yourself builds transferable strategy; quality beats quantity.
5. **Not solving problems out loud** — if you go silent, the interviewer assumes you're stuck. Talking shows your approach, lets them redirect you, and demonstrates communication.
6. **Rushing** — leads to mistakes and suggests carelessness; go slowly and methodically; you'll actually finish faster.
7. **Sloppy coding** — duplicated code, messy data structures, no modularization. Write as if it were production; break into sub-routines.
8. **Not testing** — always "run" (walk through) your code after writing, or test while writing on complex problems.
9. **Fixing mistakes carelessly** — flipping a return value without understanding the bug creates more bugs and signals carelessness. Think through *why* a bug occurred before fixing.
10. **Giving up** — tough questions are part of the test; meet them eagerly, don't shrink back.

## 7. FAQs

- **"Should I tell my interviewer if I know a question?"** — Yes. Honesty points; the question may have been tweaked; if you ace it effortlessly the interviewer will see through the pretense and mark you unqualified.
- **"What language should I use?"** — The author recommends C, C++, or Java (interviewers are comfortable with these); her own preference is Java because it's fast to write and readable even by C++ coders. Most solutions in the book are in Java.
- **"I didn't hear back. Am I rejected?"** — No. Silence usually means recruiter vacation, reorg, or a backlog. Always follow up.
- **"Can I re-apply after rejection?"** — Almost always, after a 6–12 month wait. The prior bad interview rarely hurts the next one.

## 1. What all five share

- **Interviewing is a black box** — candidates rarely see how decisions get made, whether interviewers talk to each other, or what the company truly cares about.
- **Five top companies profiled** — Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Apple, Facebook, and Yahoo!. Each has its own quirks (e.g., Amazon's **bar raisers**, Google's **Hiring Committees**).
- **Coding and algorithms are universal** — almost all software firms test these; company differences lie in what they stress *on top*.

## 2. Microsoft

- **Wants smart, passionate geeks** — not C++ API trivia, but you will write code on the board.
- **Typical day** — arrive in the morning, paperwork, short recruiter interview with a sample question (usually not a real grill).
- **Be nice to your recruiter** — they can become your biggest advocate and may push to re-interview you after a stumble.
- **4–5 interviews, often across two teams** — interviews happen in the interviewer's office (a chance to read team culture), not a conference room.
- **Hiring manager meeting is a good sign** — means you passed the team's interviews; it's now the hiring manager's call.
- **Follow up after a week** of silence from HR; non-responsiveness usually means busy, not rejection.

> **Definitely Prepare: "Why do you want to work for Microsoft?"** — show passion for technology and for Microsoft products specifically (e.g., "I've been using Microsoft software as long as I can remember").

> **What's Unique** — you only reach the hiring manager if you've done well, so getting that meeting is itself a great sign.

## 3. Amazon

- **Two phone screens** with a specific team, on a shared document editor like **CollabEdit**; broad technology-familiarity questions in addition to coding.
- **On-site in Seattle** — 4–5 interviews with one or two teams; whiteboard coding; some interviewers stress non-coding skills.
- **Interviewers are each assigned a specific area** to probe and can't see each other's feedback until the hiring meeting.
- **Bar Raiser** — a specially trained interviewer from a *different* team, charged with keeping the hiring bar high; has veto power. If one interview seems significantly harder and different, that's most likely the bar raiser.
- **Judged relative to other candidates**, not on a simple "percent correct" basis — struggling in the bar raiser's round doesn't necessarily mean failure.
- **Follow up after a week** if Amazon hasn't responded.

> **Definitely Prepare** — Amazon is a web-based company, so prepare **scalability questions** (see Scalability and Memory Limits chapter) and **object-oriented design** questions.

> **What's Unique** — the Bar Raiser is brought in from a different team to keep the bar high; you need to impress both this person and the hiring manager.

## 4. Google

- **Rumors are mostly just rumors** — the process isn't dramatically different from Microsoft's or Amazon's.
- **First phone screen done by a Google engineer** — same bar as on-site; may involve coding via shared document.
- **On-site: 4–6 interviewers**, one of whom is a lunch interviewer.
- **Feedback is kept confidential** between interviewers, so every interview starts with a blank slate; the lunch interviewer does *not* submit feedback (good chance to ask honest questions).
- **No assigned focus areas** — each interviewer runs the interview however they like.
- **Four feedback categories** — Analytical Ability, Coding, Experience, Communication — scored 1.0 to 4.0 for an overall score.
- **Hiring Committee (HC)** — engineers/managers who never met you make the hire / no-hire call from written feedback. HC wants at least one **"enthusiastic endorser"** — a packet with scores of 3.6, 3.1, 3.1, and 2.6 beats straight 3.1s.
- **HC recommendation** goes to a compensation committee, then the executive management committee; final decision can take several weeks.

> **Definitely Prepare** — scalability (Google is web-based) and **Bit Manipulation**; phone-screen performance is usually a strong factor in the final decision.

> **What's Different** — interviewers don't make the hire decision; the HC does, and it can (rarely) be overruled by Google executives.

## 5. Apple

- **Minimal bureaucracy**, passion for the company is very important; Mac familiarity recommended (not required).
- **Recruiter phone screen** for basic skills, then technical phone screens with team members.
- **On-site** — greeted by recruiter with a process overview, then 6–8 interviews with the team and key cross-functional partners.
- **Mix of 1-on-1 and 2-on-1 interviews** — don't stress 2-on-1s; same difficulty as a 1-on-1.
- **Lunch with the potential future manager** — more casual but still an interview.
- **Assigned focus areas** — each interviewer usually sticks to a different topic and avoids sharing feedback unless flagging something for follow-up.
- **End-of-day huddle** — if still viable, you get an interview with the director and VP of the org. If unsuccessful, you are escorted out without ever knowing you failed.
- **Director/VP thumbs up or down** — VP typically isn't present but can still veto; recruiter follows up a few days later.

> **Definitely Prepare** — if you know the team, read up on their product; bring specific improvement recommendations to show passion.

> **What's Unique** — 2-on-1 interviews are common (don't be thrown); Apple employees are huge Apple fans, so show the same passion.

## 6. Facebook

- **Online engineering puzzles** are just one way in — standard online applications and university career fairs also work.
- **Minimum of two phone screens** (local candidates may get just one before on-site). Phone screens are technical, coding on **Etherpad** or similar.
- **On-site interviewers are primarily software engineers**; hiring managers join when available. All interviewers go through **comprehensive interview training** — who interviews you has no bearing on the offer.
- **Each interviewer is given a role** — algorithm/coding, architecture/design, or success in Facebook's fast-paced environment — to avoid repetition and build a holistic picture.
- **Feedback submitted before discussing** with other interviewers to avoid bias.
- **Consensus decision** by interviewing team + hiring manager → recommendation to the hiring committee.
- **"Ninja skills"** — Facebook looks for the ability to hack together an elegant, scalable solution in any language; knowing PHP isn't especially important.

> **Definitely Prepare** — Facebook wants developers with an **entrepreneurial spirit**; show you love to build stuff fast.

> **What's Unique** — interviewed for the company "in general," not for a specific team. New hires go through a **six-week "bootcamp"** with mentorship from senior devs and then choose a project.

## 7. Yahoo!

- **Recruits heavily from top twenty schools** — others enter via Yahoo!'s job board or, better, an internal referral.
- **Phone screen with a senior employee** (tech lead or manager — more influence than typical).
- **On-site: 6–7 interviews**, 45 minutes each, same team, each interviewer with a focus area (e.g., databases, computer architecture).
- **Interview structure (typical)** — 5 min general conversation, 20 min coding (e.g., implement merge sort), 20 min system design (e.g., design a large distributed cache).
- **End-of-day Program Manager meeting** — product demos or discussion of competing offers; usually not a factor in the decision.
- **Hiring manager has ultimate say** and weighs positive feedback against negative.
- **Often same-day decision** (if hired), though not always — the team may want to interview others first.

> **Definitely Prepare** — Yahoo! almost always asks **system design** questions; they want to see you can design software, not just write code. Reason your way through even without direct background.

> **What's Unique** — the phone interviewer tends to be a higher-influence employee (e.g., hiring manager); decisions often come the same day.

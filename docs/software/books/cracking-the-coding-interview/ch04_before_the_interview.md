## 1. Getting the right experience

- **Resume is what gets you the interview** — offer decisions turn on interview performance, but it's prior experience that gets you in the door. Actively enhance both technical *and* non-technical experience.
- **Two things companies want to see** — that you're smart and that you can code. Everything in this section serves those two signals.

### 1.1. For current students

| Action | What it gives you |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Take the Big Project Classes** | "Meaty" class projects belong on the resume; the more real-world, the better. |
| **Get an Internship** | Freshmen/sophomores can use programs like **Microsoft Explorer** and **Google Summer of Code**; start-ups are a strong fallback. |
| **Start Something** | Entrepreneurial side projects built on weekends/breaks show initiative; ask a professor to "sponsor" the work as independent study. |

### 1.2. For professionals

- **Shift work responsibilities more towards coding** — without revealing intent to leave, ask your manager for bigger coding challenges. Aim for "meaty" projects using relevant technologies that can become resume bullets.
- **Use your nights and weekends** — build a mobile app, web app, or desktop project. Few things are as impressive as someone who built something "just for fun"; it also keeps you current on modern tech.
- **Think ahead about career direction** — if you want to move into management later, start developing leadership experience now even while seeking a dev role.

## 2. Building a network

- **Friends-of-friends matter more than friends** — if you have *N* friends, you have roughly *N²* friends of friends. Both immediate and extended network are critical.

### 2.1. What makes a good network

A good network is both **broad and close**:

- **Broad** — focused on your industry (technology) but spanning others too. An accountant, for example, may have lots of friends outside accounting who are right for you. Be open to connecting with anyone.
- **Close** — access through a close friend beats access through a distant acquaintance. "Professional networkers" or "card collectors" are written off as fake; keep connections deep and meaningful.

### 2.2. How to build a strong network

1. **Use Meetup.com or your alumni network** to discover relevant events. Bring business cards (or make some if you're a student/unemployed).
2. **Walk up and say "Hello"** — worst case, they don't connect with you and you never see them again.
3. **Be open about your interests** and ask about theirs; if they're running something interesting, ask to grab coffee.
4. **Follow up** via LinkedIn and email; better, invite them for coffee to discuss their work.
5. **Be helpful** — lending a hand makes you seen as generous; people help those who have helped them.

- **Online networks count** — blogs, Twitter, Facebook, and email extend your reach, but strictly online interactions require much more work to form a real bond.

## 3. Writing a great resume

- **Resume screeners want the same signals as interviewers** — smart, can code. Prepare the resume to highlight these; non-technical hobbies rarely carry weight.

### 3.1. Appropriate resume length

In the US:

- **One page if < 10 years of experience**, no more than two pages otherwise.
- **Recruiters spend ~20 seconds** scanning; extra content dilutes the most impressive items.
- **Some readers refuse long resumes outright** — risk not worth taking.
- Long resumes signal **poor prioritization**, not abundant experience.

### 3.2. Employment history

- **Include only relevant positions** — your ice-cream-serving job doesn't show you're smart or can code.

**Writing Strong Bullets** — use the formula **"Accomplished X by implementing Y which led to Z"**. Examples:

- *"Reduced object rendering time by 75% by implementing distributed caching, leading to a 10% reduction in log-in time."*
- *"Increased average match accuracy from 1.2 to 1.5 by implementing a new comparison algorithm based on windiff."*

Show **what you did, how you did it, and the measurable result**.

### 3.3. Projects

- **Include 2–4 most significant projects** — great for college students and recent grads to look more experienced.
- **State the project and the languages/technologies used** — optionally note individual vs. team, and course vs. personal (only if it helps).
- **Don't pad** — avoid cluttering the resume with 13 minor projects.

### 3.4. Programming languages and software

- **Software** — don't list Microsoft Office; highly technical software (e.g., Visual Studio, Linux) *can* help but rarely moves the needle.
- **Languages** — list the languages you've used *with experience level*, rather than dumping everything you've touched. Example:
  - *Languages: Java (expert), C++ (proficient), JavaScript (prior experience).*

### 3.5. Advice for non-native English speakers and internationals

- **Get a native English speaker to proofread** — some companies discard resumes for a single typo.
- **For US positions, do *not* include age, marital status, or nationality** — this information creates legal liability and is unwelcome.

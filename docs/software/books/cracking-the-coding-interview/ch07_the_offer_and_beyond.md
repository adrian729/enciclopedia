## 1. Handling offers and rejection

- **Offer Deadlines and Extensions** — offers almost always come with a deadline, usually one to four weeks out. If you are waiting on other companies, ask for an extension; companies will usually try to accommodate.
- **Declining an Offer** — how you decline matters. You may want to work there in a few years, or your contacts may move to another exciting company. Keep the line of communication open.
- **Give a non-offensive, inarguable reason** — e.g., if declining a big company for a start-up, explain that you feel a start-up is the right choice for you at this time. The big company can't suddenly "become" a start-up, so they can't argue.
- **Handling Rejection** — big tech companies reject around **80% of interview candidates** and recognize that interviews are not a perfect test of skills. Companies are often eager to re-interview previously rejected candidates; some reach out to old candidates or expedite their application *because* of prior performance.
- **After a rejection call** — thank the recruiter, explain you are disappointed but understand, and ask when you can reapply.
- **Finding out why you were rejected is incredibly difficult** — recruiters rarely reveal the reason. Slightly better luck asking where to focus preparation. Self-reflection on one question is unreliable; you struggled, but so did other candidates on that same question. Candidates are typically rejected for **coding and algorithm skills**, so focus preparation there.

## 2. Evaluating the offer

### 2.1. The Financial Package

The biggest mistake candidates make is **focusing too much on salary** — they can end up worse off financially. Salary is just one part; also weigh:

| Component | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Signing Bonus, Relocation, and Other One Time Perks** | Amortize the cash over three years (or however long you expect to stay) before comparing. |
| **Cost of Living Difference** | Silicon Valley is about **20–30% more expensive** than Seattle (partly due to 10% California state income tax). Do not overlook this across multi-location offers. |
| **Annual Bonus** | Tech-company annual bonuses range from **3% to 30%**. Ask the recruiter for the average, or check with friends at the company. |
| **Stock Options and Grants** | Can form a large share of annual compensation. Compare by amortizing over three years and lumping that value into salary. |

**Long-term caveat** — what you *learn* and how a company *advances your career* often makes far more difference to long-term finances than salary. Be careful how much emphasis to put on money right now.

### 2.2. Career Development

Odds are, in a few years you'll be interviewing again — so evaluate how this offer impacts your path:

- How good does the company's name look on my resume?
- How much will I learn? Will I learn relevant things?
- What is the promotion plan? How do developers' careers progress?
- If I want to move into management, does this company offer a realistic plan?
- Is the company or team growing?
- **If I want to leave, is the company situated near other companies I'm interested in, or will I need to move?**

The last point is often overlooked but important. Microsoft in Silicon Valley means you can leave for almost any other company; Microsoft-Seattle limits you to Amazon, Google, and a handful of smaller tech companies; AOL in Dulles, Virginia leaves even fewer options. You may find yourself forced to stay because there's nowhere else to go without uprooting your life.

### 2.3. Company Stability

- **Don't focus too much on stability** — if a company lets you go, you can usually find an offer from an equivalent company. The question to answer: if laid off, do you feel reasonably comfortable in your ability to find a new job?

### 2.4. The Happiness Factor

- **The Product** — matters, but for most engineers there are more important factors, like who you work with.
- **Manager and Teammates** — when people love or hate their job, it's usually because of these. Have you met them? Did you enjoy talking with them?
- **Company Culture** — tied to how decisions get made, the social atmosphere, and how the company is organized. Ask future teammates how they would describe it.
- **Hours** — ask future teammates how long they typically work; make sure it meshes with your lifestyle. Hours before major deadlines are typically much longer.
- **Team switching** — at companies like Google that let you switch teams easily, you can find a team and product that match you well.

## 3. Negotiation

Story: a negotiations class was asked how much cheaper a no-negotiation dealership would have to be to beat a dealership that allows negotiation on a $20,000 car. On average, students said **$750** — they were willing to pay $750 to avoid an hour of negotiation. Most of those same students also accepted whatever their job offer gave them.

> **Do yourself a favor. Negotiate.**

1. **Just Do It** — recruiters will not revoke an offer because you negotiated. You have nothing to lose.
2. **Have a Viable Alternative** — recruiters negotiate because they're worried you'll go elsewhere. Alternative offers make that concern real.
3. **Have a Specific "Ask"** — asking for *an additional $7000 in salary* is more effective than asking for "more." A vague "more" can be satisfied by throwing in $1000.
4. **Overshoot** — negotiation is back-and-forth. Ask for more than you want because the company will likely meet you in the middle.
5. **Think Beyond Salary** — salary too much higher than peers makes recruiters uncomfortable. Ask for more equity, a bigger signing bonus, or relocation paid as cash (great for college students with cheap moves).
6. **Use Your Best Medium** — conventional advice says phone only, but email is better if you aren't comfortable on the phone. What matters most is that you negotiate at all.

**Big-company "levels"** — companies like Microsoft have well-defined levels where everyone at a level is paid in roughly the same range. You can negotiate freely within your level's range; going beyond it requires convincing the recruiter and team that your experience matches a higher level — difficult but feasible.

## 4. On the job

Navigating your career path doesn't end at the interview — it's just getting started.

### 4.1. Set a Timeline

- **The complacency trap** — common story: you join a company, you're psyched, five years later you're still there and realize the last three added little to your skill set or resume.
- **Outline your career path before starting a new job** — where do you want to be in ten years, and what are the steps to get there?
- **Check in yearly** — ask what the next year's experience will bring and how your career or skill set advanced over the last year.

### 4.2. Build Strong Relationships

- **Your network is critical** when you move on — applying online is tricky; a personal referral is much better, and your ability to get one hinges on your network.
- **At work** — build strong relationships with your manager and teammates. When they leave, stay in touch — a friendly note a few weeks after departure helps bridge a work acquaintance into a personal one.
- **Personal life** — friends and friends-of-friends are valuable connections. Be open to helping others; they'll be more likely to help you.

### 4.3. Ask for What You Want

- **Managers vary** — some actively grow your career, others take a hands-off approach. Either way, it's on you to pursue the right challenges.
- **Be (reasonably) frank with your manager** — if you want back-end coding, say so; if you want leadership opportunities, discuss how you'd be able to do so.
- **Be your own best advocate** so you can achieve goals on your timeline.

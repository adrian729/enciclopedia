## 1. Experienced candidates

- **Same questions, same standard (on average)** — experienced candidates get the same data structures / algorithms questions as new grads; the big companies hold everyone to this test.
- **Two opposing interviewer biases cancel out** — some hold experienced candidates higher (they *should* know better); others hold them lower (they've been out of school longer and forgetting details is expected).
- **Exceptions** — **system design and architecture** questions, and resume questions, scale with experience. Students and recent grads are still asked system design and should attempt it.
- **Behavioral depth expected** — experienced candidates must give a more in-depth, impressive answer to prompts like *"What was the hardest bug you've faced?"*.

## 2. Testers and SDETs

- **SDETs must be great coders *and* great testers** — coding standards are slightly lower than SDE, but still strong.
- **Core testing problems** — practice classics like *"how would you test a light bulb / pen / cash register / Microsoft Word?"*.
- **Coding rejections are the #1 cause** — SDETs are still expected to solve the same coding and algorithm questions a dev would.
- **Test-the-coding-question format** — a common SDET pattern is *"write code to do X,"* then *"OK, now test it."* For any coding question, silently ask *"how would I test this?"* — any problem can be an SDET problem.
- **Strong communication matters** — testers work with many different people; don't neglect the Behavioral Questions section.

**Career Advice** — SDET is often used as an "easy" way into a big company, but **moving from SDET to dev is very difficult**. Keep coding and algorithms sharp, and try to switch within 1–2 years; otherwise dev interviews will stop taking you seriously. Never let coding skills atrophy.

## 3. Program and Product Managers

- **Role varies wildly** across and within companies — at Microsoft some PMs are customer evangelists (closer to marketing), others code most of the day and will be tested on coding.
- **Skills evaluated** — Handling Ambiguity, Customer Focus (Attitude), Customer Focus (Technical Skills), Multi-Level Communication, Passion for Technology, Teamwork / Leadership.

| Skill | What interviewers look for |
|-------|----------------------------|
| **Handling Ambiguity** | Don't stall — seek information, prioritize the most important parts, solve in a structured way. Usually judged indirectly. |
| **Customer Focus (Attitude)** | Put yourself in the customer's shoes. Ask *who* uses the product and *how* (e.g., *"Design an alarm clock for the blind"*). |
| **Customer Focus (Technical Skills)** | Some products need deep technical knowledge (e.g., Windows Security) to walk in the door; others (e.g., MSN Messenger) don't. |
| **Multi-Level Communication** | Communicate with everyone from executives to engineers. Tested directly (*"Explain TCP/IP to your grandmother"*) and via how you discuss prior projects. |
| **Passion for Technology** | Happy employees are productive; show enthusiasm for the field, the company, and the team. |
| **Teamwork / Leadership** | Likely the most important. Behavioral prompts like *"Tell me about a time when a teammate wasn't pulling his / her own weight."* Rehearse these. |

- **Area weighting tracks real-job weighting** — skills are emphasized in roughly the proportion they matter on the job.

## 4. Dev Leads and Managers

- **Strong coding almost always required** — especially at Google, which holds managers to dev-level coding standards.
- **Teamwork / Leadership** — evaluated explicitly (*"how did you handle X?"*) and implicitly (how you interact with the interviewer); arrogance or passivity both hurt.
- **Prioritization** — how you meet tough deadlines by asking the right questions, cutting less-important parts, keeping critical work on track.
- **Communication** — engage friendly and fluent conversation with both technical and non-technical people; this doubles as a personality evaluation.
- **"Getting Things Done"** — arguably the most important trait; strike the right balance between preparing and implementing, and motivate the team toward goals.
- **Prepare very thoroughly with the interview preparation grid** — outcomes ultimately come back to prior experience and personality.

## 5. Start-Ups

Highly variable — the process at any specific start-up may deviate from these general pointers.

- **Application via personal referral** is the best way into hot start-ups; the referrer doesn't need to be a close friend, just someone willing to pass along your resume.
- **Visas and work authorization** — most smaller US start-ups can't sponsor work visas; if you need one, target bigger start-ups or work through a professional recruiter specialized in start-ups.
- **Resume selection factors** — start-ups want smart coders who also thrive in an **entrepreneurial environment**. Show **initiative** and ability to **"hit the ground running"** in their language stack.

**The interview process** evaluates three dimensions in addition to standard coding and algorithms:

| Dimension | Tested via |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Personality Fit** | How you interact with the interviewer — friendly, engaging conversation is the ticket to an offer. |
| **Skill Set** | Specific programming languages — brush up on the language the start-up uses. |
| **Prior Experience** | Lots of behavioral questions about past work — pay special attention to the Behavioral Questions section. |

- **Coding and algorithm questions still appear** — they are very common on top of the start-up-specific areas.

## 1. Why behavioral questions matter

- **Purpose of behavioral questions** — interviewers use them to get to know your personality, probe your resume more deeply, or simply ease you into the interview. Either way, they are important and can be prepared for.
- **Form** — usually "Tell me about a time when you..." and often require an example from a specific project or position.
- **You're telling them about yourself** — when answering, you're not just finding a story that matches the question; think deeply about what each story communicates about you.

## 2. The preparation grid

Fill out a grid before the interview. **Columns = major resume items** (projects, jobs, activities). **Rows = common questions.** Each cell holds the corresponding story, reduced to a couple of keywords so it is easy to study and recall.

| Common Questions |
|------------------|
| Most Challenging |
| What You Learned |
| Most Interesting |
| Hardest Bug |
| Enjoyed Most |
| Conflicts with Teammates |

- **Keyword cells, not paragraphs** — keywords trigger memory and produce a fluid spoken response instead of a re-read paragraph.
- **Phone interviews** — keep the grid in front of you during the call.
- **Softer questions** — for dev lead, PM, or testing roles, build a second grid covering conflicts on a team, failures, and times you had to persuade someone.

## 3. Tricky self-assessment questions

- **"What are your weaknesses?"** — give a **real weakness**. Answers like "I work too hard" signal arrogance or an unwillingness to admit faults; nobody wants to work with that. A better answer states a legitimate weakness and shows how you work to overcome it (e.g., careless mistakes from working quickly → always have someone double-check).
- **"What was the most challenging part of that project?"** — do *not* say "I had to learn a lot of new languages and technologies." This is the **"cop out" answer** that signals nothing was really hard.

## 4. Questions to ask the interviewer

Most interviewers give you a chance to ask questions; their quality will factor into the decision, consciously or subconsciously. Prepare questions in advance via research on the company or team. Three categories:

| Category | Purpose | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Genuine Questions** | Things you actually want to know to gauge day-to-day life | "How much of your day do you spend coding?" / "How many meetings per week?" / "Ratio of testers to devs to PMs?" |
| **Insightful Questions** | Demonstrate deep knowledge of programming/tech and passion for the product | "I noticed you use technology X. How do you handle problem Y?" / "Why X protocol over Y?" |
| **Passion Questions** | Show interest in learning and in being a strong contributor | "I'm very interested in scalability. What opportunities are there to learn about it?" / "I'm not familiar with technology X but it sounds interesting — could you tell me more?" |

## 5. Handling behavioral questions

Interviews usually start and end with "chit chat" / "soft skills" — a time for the interviewer to ask about your resume and for you to ask about the company. It is also meant to relax you.

- **Be Specific, Not Arrogant** — arrogance is a red flag, but you still need to sound impressive. Specificity is the lever: give facts and let the interviewer derive the interpretation.
  - Weak: "I basically did all the hard work for the team."
  - Strong: "I implemented the file system, which was considered one of the most challenging components because..."
- **Limit Details** — rambling on loses an interviewer unfamiliar with the subject. State key points and offer more on request: *"By examining the most common user behavior and applying the Rabin-Karp algorithm, I designed a new algorithm to reduce search from O(n) to O(log n) in 90% of cases. I can go into more detail if you'd like."*

## 6. Structured answer formats

Two techniques for structuring responses. They can be used separately or together.

### 6.1. Nugget First

- **Open with a "nugget"** — a one-sentence summary of what the response will be about.
- **Example** — "Sure, let me tell you about the time when I convinced my school to let undergraduates teach their own courses. Initially, my school had a rule where..."
- **Benefits** — grabs the interviewer's attention, makes the topic clear upfront, and keeps ramblers focused because you have committed to the gist.

### 6.2. S.A.R. (Situation, Action, Result)

Outline the **Situation**, explain the **Actions** you took, then describe the **Result**. Keep situation and result succinct; the interviewer does not need many details and may be confused by them.

*Example: "Tell me about a challenging interaction with a teammate."*

- **Situation** — On an operating systems project, three teammates; one stayed quiet in meetings, rarely chipped in on email threads, and struggled to complete his components.
- **Action** — Pulled him aside after class, asked open-ended questions about how he felt the project was going and which components he was most excited about; offered the write-up; realized he was confused and lacked confidence, not lazy; broke components into smaller pieces and complimented him often to boost confidence.
- **Result** — Still the weakest member but much improved; finished all his work on time, contributed more in discussions, happy to work together again in the future.

Using the **S.A.R. model** with clear situations, actions, and results lets the interviewer easily identify how you made an impact and why it mattered.

## 1. Seeds of a Conviction

- **Innate sense that birth should be gentle** — Mongan describes holding the belief from childhood that babies are "angels" and should arrive peacefully; this instinct preceded any formal study
- **Research paper as turning point** — a high-school assignment ("Achieving Safe and Gentle Childbirth") led Mongan to medical textbooks; she found them almost entirely focused on abnormalities and interventions, with virtually no discussion of normal, uncomplicated birth
- **Void in obstetric education** — the textbooks treated the human body as a mechanical system without addressing the emotional or spiritual dimensions of the person giving birth, and one text explicitly warned doctors to be wary of "the educated, sophisticated woman who may harbour a notion that she knows how to bring a baby into the world"

## 2. The Damage of Fear-Filled Birth Stories

- **"War stories" as cultural programming** — Mongan grew up hearing women recount births defined by danger, fear, and misery; these narratives functioned as social conditioning, training girls to expect suffering as an inevitable part of womanhood
- **Mother's birth story as catalyst** — Mongan's own mother repeatedly told the traumatic story of Mongan's forceps-assisted birth, which caused Mongan years of guilt before transforming into a determination to find a better way
- **Victimisation cycle** — the cultural framing of menstruation as "the curse" and birth as peril created a self-reinforcing identity of female victimhood that could discourage women from even attempting natural birth or push them toward elective surgical birth out of fear

## 3. Discovering Dick-Read and the Missing Logic

- **_Life Magazine_ (January 1950)** — a cover story on Grace New Haven Hospital's adoption of Grantly Dick-Read's theories showed a calm, smiling mother who had given birth "with no fear, little pain"; this was the first published evidence Mongan found that matched her own beliefs
- **Fight-or-Flight mechanism** — Dick-Read's core insight: when fear is absent, the autonomic nervous system does not trigger the fight-or-flight response, neuropeptide firing slows, and birthing muscles can work without the self-defeating tension that causes pain. The logic was "hiding in plain sight"
- **Decades of suppression** — Dick-Read had been presenting these theories since the late 1920s; medical boards dismissed his papers unread and threatened his licence when he persisted

## 4. Mongan's Own Births

- **First and second births — calm labour, stolen birth** — Mongan practised daily deep relaxation during pregnancy and achieved pain-free labours of under two hours each; both times the doctor was absent, nurses held her legs together and forced ether anaesthesia against her stated wishes, and the babies were delivered by forceps. After the second time, she vowed "Never again!"
- **Third birth — advocating successfully** — Mongan confronted her doctor directly: "If you don't feel comfortable with this, I'll have to walk." She also insisted her husband be present (unheard of in the late 1950s when husbands were not allowed beyond the hospital lobby). The doctor wrote down everything she asked for. He swore her to secrecy because they both knew that if word got out in that small town, the birth she was planning would never have occurred. Maura was born naturally in an hour and a half in the most beautiful birth Mongan could have imagined
- **Fourth birth** — her son Shawn (8 lb 6 oz) was born with just over an hour of labour. After Maura's birth a nurse had commented that "she's only 6 pounds, 3 ounces. I guess anyone could birth a baby that small without too much trouble." There was no such comment after Shawn's birth

## 5. From Personal Experience to a Movement

- **Lamaze as a partial step** — the Lamaze programme brought natural (no drugs) births into birthing rooms, but many instructors moved away from eliminating pain and shifted to coping with it, leaving mothers awake but often exhausted. Separately, since women were no longer routinely anaesthetised, the concept of pushing the baby out came in, replacing forceps — but many staff, accustomed to forceps, did not believe babies would come out on their own
- **Maura's pregnancy re-ignited the mission** — when Mongan's daughter became pregnant, Mongan compiled her philosophy and techniques into a small guide; the success of that birth and others turned HypnoBirthing into a formal programme
- **Uphill climb for acceptance** — mainstream healthcare providers remained reluctant to acknowledge what they witnessed directly; Mongan asked graduating OB/GYN students on three occasions how many had seen a natural birth — not one hand was ever raised, highlighting why many doctors are uncomfortable supporting natural birth: it is uncharted territory in their training

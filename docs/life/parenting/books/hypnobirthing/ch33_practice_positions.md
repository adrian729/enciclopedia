## 1. Birth Breathing and Crowning

- **What is happening** — the baby descends to the rim of the perineum, the head becomes visible (crowning), and with a few final breaths the baby passes through to the world
- **Birth Breathing is the reverse of Slow Breathing** — take in a short, deep breath and breathe down (instead of drawing the surge up). The birthing companion prompts you to direct your breath and love downward to help the baby move smoothly to crowning
- **Visualise the opening** — as you exhale downward, picture your vagina opening like the petals of a rose, folding outward as the baby reaches the perineal rim
- **Remarkably few breaths needed** — HypnoBirthing mothers report needing only three or four Birth Breaths to bring the baby past the perineum and out

## 2. Avoiding Episiotomy

- **Gradual thinning prevents the need for episiotomy** — just as the cervix had to thin and open, the thick rim of the perineum needs to gradually thin and unfold through each surge by the natural pressure of the baby's head. The more nature takes its course, the less likely an episiotomy will be needed
- **Birth Breathing is faster than forced pushing** — the gradual unfolding may appear slow, but it is accomplished more swiftly with Birth Breathing than by any other means

## 3. Birthing Positions

- **Toilet sitting** — the body is conditioned to release and let go in this position; the Natural Expulsive Reflex (NER) is supported by Birth Breathing. Benefits: opens the pelvic area, uses gravity, relieves you from supporting your own weight. Place pillows behind your back. Move to another position as crowning approaches for safe delivery
- **Lateral position** — lying on your side; you can remain here and simply breathe down until the baby's head is visible
- **Slanted "J" position** — rest just above the tailbone to give the baby room to move out
- **Companion-supported squat** — the birthing companion supports the mother while she squats
- **Supported position on birthing stool** — supported position on a birthing stool
- **Companion-supported upright** — upright position with the companion providing physical support
- **Leaping frog** — can be adopted without companion assistance
- **Hands-and-knees** — allows the baby to move back from the lower pelvic area and turn to a more beneficial position if needed
- **Birth ball** — used during the birthing stage as an alternative support position

## 4. Correcting Baby's Position

- **Polar Bear position** — from hands-and-knees, lower forearms to the floor and rest forehead on hands. Not a birthing position, but used when the baby is in a less-than-optimal position; it allows the baby to back away from the lower pelvis and rotate to a better alignment
- **Rebozo technique** — developed by midwife Guadalupe Trueba. A long scarf is placed under the mother's abdomen at the pelvic region and lifted upward while she is in Polar Bear position. This lifts the baby out of the current position and gives her the opportunity to back up and return to the birth path in a more favourable orientation

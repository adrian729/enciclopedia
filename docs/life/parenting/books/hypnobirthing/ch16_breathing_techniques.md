## 1. Overview of the Three Breaths

- **Only three breathing techniques** — HypnoBirthing uses Calm Breathing, Surge Breathing, and Birth Breathing. Each is named for when it is used, and together they cover every phase of labour

## 2. Calm Breathing

- **Purpose** — brings you into a relaxed state for visualisation practice, for resuming relaxation between surges during labour, and for conserving energy during the thinning and opening phase. Also useful any time you feel tense, frightened, or fatigued, and especially for quiet bonding with your baby after birth
- **Technique** — settle into a supported position; eyes gently closed, lips lightly touching, tongue tip placed where teeth meet the palate. Take in a short breath through the nose to a count of four ("In-2-3-4"), feeling the stomach rise. Pause. Exhale slowly through the nose to a count of eight ("Out-2-3-4-5-6-7-8"), directing breath energy down and inward to the back of the throat; let shoulders droop into the frame of the body
- **Verification check** — left hand on stomach, right hand on lower chest. On inhale, the left hand should rise (stomach inflates like a balloon). On exhale, both hands fold inward as chest and stomach create a crevice
- **Conditioning** — after only a few practice sessions, the count and hand-check become unnecessary; the body enters deep relaxation automatically

## 3. Surge Breathing

- **When to use** — throughout the entire thinning and opening phase of labour, with every uterine surge or wave, until it is time to breathe the baby down to crowning
- **How it works with the uterus** — assists the vertical muscles as they draw up the horizontal muscles to open the cervix. Because the breath works with the body rather than against it, HypnoBirthing mothers often find surges last a shorter time and the opening phase is shortened overall
- **Technique** — lie in a lateral position with a pillow under back and shoulders; place both hands across the top of the abdomen with fingers loosely meshed. Breathe down into the abdomen to clear breath. Using only abdominal muscles, inhale slowly to a rapid count of 20 while expanding the abdomen like a large balloon into the cupped fingers. Pause. Exhale at the same slow pace, directing breath down into the abdomen as it recedes
- **Practice early and often** — begin as soon as possible and continue throughout pregnancy so the body becomes conditioned to the abdominal expansion

## 4. Birth Breathing

- **When to use** — once the cervix has reached a degree of opening sufficient for the baby to pass down through the birth path; replaces Surge Breathing for the birthing phase
- **How it works** — assists the natural expulsive reflex (NER) to move the baby gently down to crowning and birth. The body's natural pulsations do the work; the breath provides a gentle nudge, not force
- **Technique** — assume a comfortable position; mouth softly closed (not pursed), all breath through the nose. Breathe only downward and only during a surge. Direct the energy of the breath to the lower back of the throat and allow that thrust to move down through the back, gently nudging the baby toward the vaginal outlet. Keep the vaginal outlet and anal passage open between breaths to maintain momentum. Use Calm Breathing between surges to stay relaxed and avoid pushing
- **Practice on the toilet** — the most natural place to condition this technique is during daily bowel movements, which use the same expulsive muscles. Practicing each time builds the reflex so it is automatic during birth
- **Many mothers need only a few breaths** — some report using only two or three Birth Breaths for full emergence, because the sphincters at the outlet are relaxed and open rather than tense and closed

## 5. Why Pushing Is Counterproductive

- **Historical origin of forced pushing** — for centuries, anaesthetised women had babies extracted with forceps. When unanaesthetised birth returned (via Lamaze), practitioners who had never witnessed natural descent assumed the baby needed to be actively "pushed" out; the violent "purple pushing" protocol (holding breath, bearing down to a count of 10, staff coaching in unison) became entrenched
- **Physiological problem** — forced pushing creates stress, which causes the sphincters of the vagina to close ahead of the descending baby. Any woman who simulates forced pushing will immediately feel the muscles tighten rather than release. This can exhaust the mother, press the baby against a resistant passage, and extend pushing for hours
- **Oxygen deprivation risk** — surrendering to the conditioned "urge to push" limits oxygen to the baby, potentially causing heart-rate decelerations and triggering the very interventions the family has worked to avoid
- **The paradox of epidural "labouring down"** — women with epidurals are allowed to "labour down" (letting the body naturally expel the baby), yet women who have deliberately planned a natural birth are still expected to forcefully push; the double standard is not evidence-based
- **Let the body and baby set the pace** — the natural birthing process has a purpose that must be trusted. Women in comas have given birth undetected, demonstrating that the body can birth without conscious pushing

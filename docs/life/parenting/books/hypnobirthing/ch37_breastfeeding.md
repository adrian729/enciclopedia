## 1. Why Breast Milk Is Irreplaceable

- **Custom-made for each infant** — mothers of premature babies produce milk with extra antibodies and proteins specific to that baby's needs; formula cannot replicate this
- **Unique components** — breast milk contains growth hormones, substances that enhance visual and brain development, and infection-fighting antibodies that scientists cannot put into formula
- **Health outcomes for children** — breastfed children have fewer ear infections, diarrhoea episodes, and upper respiratory infections; as adults they show lower cholesterol and fewer instances of heart disease, and they score higher on IQ tests
- **Health outcomes for mothers** — women who breastfeed have lower rates of breast and cervical cancer
- **WHO priority ranking** — the World Health Organisation ranks infant nutrition sources in this order: (1) mother's own milk directly from the breast, (2) expressed mother's milk, (3) donor human milk from a milk bank, (4) infant formula
- **Financial value** — formula costs roughly $1,200-$2,700 per year; equivalent human milk from a milk bank would cost about $36,000. Breast milk is produced at no cost

## 2. Getting Started

- **Colostrum in the first days** — the small volume of early milk lets the baby practise latching and sucking before milk volume increases; colostrum is a natural laxative that clears meconium and is loaded with protective antibodies
- **Skin-to-skin stimulates interest** — holding the baby skin-to-skin encourages feeding; the breast secretes a scent similar to amniotic fluid that attracts the newborn
- **Expressing colostrum as a bridge** — if the baby is sleepy and uninterested, express colostrum onto a spoon and offer it to entice feeding
- **A learned skill** — breastfeeding improves with practice, like any skill; the first couple of weeks may feel awkward but become natural over time

## 3. Troubleshooting and Support

- **Discomfort is not normal** — pain while breastfeeding signals a problem that needs attention; the earlier it is identified, the easier it is to solve
- **Maintain supply while solving problems** — remove milk from the breast 5-8 times per day (by nursing, hand expression, or pump) to keep production going even if the baby cannot yet feed effectively
- **Feed the baby regardless** — use expressed milk or, if necessary, supplement with formula until supply increases; a baby gaining weight will be more eager to learn to breastfeed
- **Set up support before the birth** — identify lactation consultants, breastfeeding support groups such as La Leche League, and knowledgeable healthcare providers in advance so help is immediately available

## 4. Involving the Family

- **Many roles beyond feeding** — fathers and grandparents can walk, hold, rock, and burp the baby after feeds, change nappies, and give the baby a bath; these activities support the mother and build the baby's bond with other family members
- **Positive attitude matters** — well-meaning acquaintances may share negative breastfeeding experiences, but those difficulties often stemmed from lack of timely help, not from breastfeeding itself; informed preparation makes the difference

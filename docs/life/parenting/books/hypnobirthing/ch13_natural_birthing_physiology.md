## 1. Gifts That Develop During Pregnancy

- **Uterine seal** — the instant the fertilised egg attaches to the uterine wall, a seal forms at the cervix that protects the baby from bacteria throughout pregnancy; it releases at the beginning of labour as the "birth show"
- **Hormonal softening of the cervix** — from early pregnancy, hormones transform the cervix from a hard, cartilaginous substance (like the tip of your nose) into soft, spongy tissue (like your earlobe), so that opening during labour is easier and less uncomfortable than commonly feared
- **Vertex turn** — between weeks 32 and 37, the baby usually turns head-down in response to gravity; if the turn happens later, there is no cause for alarm and no reason to anticipate alternative procedures prematurely
- **Engagement** — two to three weeks before the due date, the widest part of the baby's head may settle at the widest part of the pelvis; this is a positive sign, though the mother's walking and sitting may change
- **Relaxin release** — the hormone relaxin, secreted in late pregnancy, lubricates and expands the vaginal walls, softens the lower uterus, relaxes the baby's ligaments for a more supple descent, weakens the amniotic membrane so it can release, and loosens the mother's skeletal ligaments so the pubic bone can shift forward

## 2. Gifts That Activate During Labour

- **Endorphin release** — relaxation triggers endorphins, which cannot co-exist with catecholamine (the constrictor hormone); when endorphins are flowing, oxytocin is released in the right amounts, birthing muscles function properly, and discomfort is minimised. Endorphins multiply themselves: the more you practise deep relaxation, the faster you reach that state
- **Prostaglandins trigger labour** — as birthing time nears, prostaglandins are secreted, eventually triggering oxytocin, which initiates uterine surges and releases the uterine seal
- **Uterine surges** — the longitudinal muscles smoothly draw the lower horizontal muscles up and out of the way of the baby's head, gently nudging the baby forward until the cervix is thin, short, and open; surges may pause between phases to let mother and baby rest — this is not a "shut down" and should not prompt forced pushing
- **Time distortion** — deep relaxation and focused awareness cause the mother to lose track of time; descent may seem long to onlookers but feels much shorter to the mother, especially because instinctive birthing often progresses faster than expected

## 3. Gifts Specific to the Birth Phase

- **Amnesiac state** — toward the end of the opening phase, a deeper relaxation drifts over the mother; she withdraws from conversation, connects more strongly with the baby's downward movement, and can selectively tune out everything except her birth companion's voice. Dr. Gregory White confirms there is no physiological reason for discomfort in this phase and that the mother appears calm and purposeful
- **Fontanels and moulding** — the baby's skull is not a rigid structure; flexible membranous fontanels allow the skull bones to overlap ("mould") during descent, significantly reducing the head's circumference. After emergence the bones return to normal position, leaving the familiar "soft spot." It is very rare for a baby's head to be too large for the birth journey
- **Natural expulsive reflex (NER)** — the body's own pulsations rhythmically move the baby down; the mother assists with soft, gentle Birth Breathing during surges rather than forced pushing. Practise Birth Breathing daily on the toilet during bowel movements, as the same expulsive muscle action is used
- **Perineal folds** — the toned, supple perineum unfolds naturally as the baby passes through; pressure from the baby's head has its own numbing effect (the same sensation as when you rest on a hand and it goes numb). Most mothers do not need an episiotomy, and the absence of forced pushing enhances gradual unfolding

## 4. Birth Breathing vs Forced Pushing

| Mother-Directed Birth Breathing | Staff-Directed Forced Pushing |
|---|---|
| Parents maintain control of birthing | Tires mother, reduces her effectiveness and participation |
| Conserves mother's energy | Closes and constricts vaginal passage ahead of baby |
| Provides continuous oxygen to baby | Emergency intervention can result |
| Gently opens the birth path for smooth descent | Mother becomes exhausted; baby is distressed |
| Increases prospect of intact perineum | Ruptures eye and facial blood vessels |
| Perineal tissues unfold naturally for gentle emergence | Limits oxygen to baby, often causing heart-rate deceleration |
| Baby maintains healthy heart rate during descent | Surrenders control of birthing to others |
| | Contributes to tearing or need for episiotomy |

## 5. After Baby Emerges

- **Umbilical pulsations** — leaving the cord unclamped and intact allows the baby's blood supply to continue providing oxygen while he gradually adjusts to breathing on his own; there is no sudden, painful gasp for air, and no need for vigorous rubbing or prodding. The placental blood also increases the baby's iron storage and helps prevent anaemia
- **Cord around the neck is usually not dangerous** — more than a third of babies are born with the cord around the neck; the only true danger is when the cord is compressed enough to cut off circulation. Most care providers simply slip it over the baby's head at emergence
- **Placental release** — the cord continues to pulse after birth until the optimum blood is returned to the baby; the baby will instinctively crawl to the nipple, lured by the scent of colostrum. Suckling releases more oxytocin, which helps detach the placenta and deepens the mother-baby bond

## 1. Moving Through the Thinning and Opening Phase

- **Preparation steps in automatically** — the weeks of conditioning now allow you to turn birthing over to your baby and your body; your role is to "do nothing" but remain loose and limp
- **Nesting enhances relaxation** — creating a nest (as practised in class) helps produce a feeling of lightness and buoyancy that deepens relaxation
- **Labour as a continuum** — unlike most other programmes, HypnoBirthing does not view labour in fragments; see it as one continuous process rather than fixating on stages
- **Ignore centimetre counts** — dilation numbers tell you only where you are at that moment and are not a valid gauge of how long labour will last; HypnoBirthing mothers frequently jump from four centimetres to seven or eight between exams. Focus on visualising the cervix continuing to open

## 2. Hallmarks of Labour

- **Body temperature fluctuations** — you will alternately feel overheated (kicking off bedding) and cold (requesting a warmed blanket); this is a normal sign of progress
- **Spotting on sanitary pad** — a spot of blood when you get up to empty your bladder signals the body is directing its efforts downward
- **Digestive reactions** — hiccupping, burping, nausea, or vomiting may occur as the diaphragm reacts to lower pulsations that will soon move the baby down; this is brief and happens rarely with HypnoBirthing mothers because their bodies remain calm
- **The urge to escape** — even a mother having a smooth birth may suddenly feel "I don't think I want to do this any longer" or "I can't do this anymore"; this is the most exciting hallmark because it means the baby's birth is imminent. The birthing companion should recognise and name this hallmark to shift the mood for everyone in the room

## 3. Working With Your Body, Not Against It

- **What the uterus is doing** — the longitudinal fibres of the uterus draw back the circular fibres so the cervix progressively thins and opens; surges feel like the abdomen tightening upward, then receding, lasting no more than 35-45 seconds at this point
- **Stay limp, not active** — clutching, clenching, or curling into a foetal position creates counterproductive tension; keeping the body loose and relaxed allows this phase to pass with little or no discomfort
- **Resist pressure to "get moving"** — well-meaning staff may suggest walks, rocking chairs, or other activities; many HypnoBirthing mothers report that these suggestions broke their deep level of relaxation. A 1997 University of Texas Southwestern Medical Centre study found no significant difference in labour length between women who walked and those who rested
- **Walk only if you want to** — movement is fine if it appeals to you, but do not walk because you are being pressured into it
- **Relaxation speeds dilation** — HypnoBirthing mothers find that relaxation and oxygenated blood flow help the cervix open more easily and quickly than forced activity
- **Avoid manipulating labour** — attempting to overcome or control labour causes fatigue and discouragement; trust your conditioning and follow your techniques so you arrive at the moment of birth fresh, alert, and energised

## 4. Water Birthing as a Complement

- **HypnoBirthing and water birthing together** — water birth is an extraordinarily good complement to HypnoBirthing; the combination keeps the mind free and relaxed while the body benefits from the softening effect of water on birthing muscles and the perineum
- **Buoyancy and weightlessness** — water provides natural weightlessness that enhances comfort; the baby is born into an environment that eases the transition from the womb to air breathing
- **Water and sensuality** — the association between water and pleasure promotes feelings of contentment and well-being, helping this phase of labour pass with greater ease

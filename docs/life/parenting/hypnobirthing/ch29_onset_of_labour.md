## 1. Timing and Phases of Labour

- **Definition of labour** — the period from when the cervix begins to thin and open until the baby is born
- **Two HypnoBirthing phases** — the programme recognises only two: the Thinning and Opening Phase and the Birthing Phase (baby descends as the mother assists with Birth Breathing)
- **Estimated Due Date (EDD) is an estimate** — labour starts anytime between 37 and 42 weeks; from 40 weeks, gestation is expressed as 40.1, 40.2, 40.3, etc. First-time mothers average 41.3 weeks. A woman is not post-date until she reaches 42.0

## 2. Recognising the Signs

- **Common onset signals** — release of membranes (leaking or a gush), release of the uterine seal and birth show, uterine surges up to 30 minutes apart, a feeling of constipation or loose stooling, surges forming a pattern
- **Some mothers feel very little** — some report only tightness around the abdomen and are not convinced labour has begun; others notice only what feels like constipation
- **True labour confirmation** — rhythmic uterine surges that tighten and release in a distinct pattern indicate true labour has arrived
- **Uterine seal ("birth show")** — a stringy discharge that can be clear, tinged, slightly pink, or bright red; it may dislodge before or after the first surge

## 3. When Membranes Release

- **Check the fluid** — confirm it is clear with no particles (except occasional white vernix), has no colour, and has no putrid odour
- **Call your care provider** — report the fluid's condition, state your wish to remain at home until labour is under way, and say you will call before leaving for the hospital
- **Infection risk is overstated** — while infection is possible, it is rare and not imminent after membrane release; bacteria must be introduced into the body for infection to occur. The vagina does not draw in water or bacteria passively
- **Avoid early vaginal exams** — accepting a vaginal exam can be the first step toward artificial induction; "being checked" often translates to "check-in" followed by pressure to jump-start labour
- **Delay between membrane release and active labour is normal** — labour will most likely start on its own within twenty-four hours; buy time, request antibiotics if pressured, politely decline vaginal exams, and use natural initiation methods if needed

## 4. Settling Into Early Labour

- **Use Slow Breathing and relaxation** — breathe gently down toward the vagina with each surge, visualising the opening rosebud to encourage the uterus to surge effectively
- **Eat and drink** — the calm HypnoBirthing approach inhibits catecholamine secretion, so digestion continues normally; snack for energy and drink fluids to avoid dehydration; keep the bladder empty
- **Birthing companion's role** — at your signal (usually closing your eyes), the companion strokes your arm and recites cues from the Birth Companion's Prompt Card; prompts need not be followed line by line but serve as suggestions for keeping you relaxed
- **Trust your body over the clock** — do not get caught up in timing and charting; listen to your body to sense when surge intervals are shortening, and trust it to tell you when it is time to move to a birthing facility
- **Lateral position** — lying on your side is a comfortable position during labour

## 1. Ancient Birth as Celebration

- **Birth as a holy rite** — in pre-Christian Europe and the ancient world, birth was treated as the highest manifestation of nature; villagers gathered around the pregnant mother to pray for a healthy, wise, strong child. Nothing in these cultures suggested labour was a long, painful ordeal
- **Women as healers and nurturers** — women developed herbal medicines and tended the ill, overseen by the wise women of the village; men gathered herbs and materials. Roles were different but equal
- **Hebrew women birthed quickly and easily** — historical records from the time of Jesus confirm babies were regularly born in fewer than three hours; it was common for mothers to give birth unassisted away from the village and return carrying their babies. Scholar Helen Wessels found no evidence that ancient women considered childbirth a curse or suffered in it

## 2. Grecian Medicine and the Mind-Body Connection

- **Hippocrates: birth should not be interfered with** — he viewed birth as natural and warned against "meddlesome interference"; he established formal midwifery to give birthing women emotional support and believed "Nature is the best physician"
- **Aristotle: deep relaxation during birthing** — he wrote about the mind-body connection and recommended deep relaxation to relieve any discomfort, including during complications
- **Soranus compiled and extended their work** — in the last century B.C., Soranus put Hippocratic and Aristotelian writings into book form, emphasising listening to the mother's needs and using the power of the mind for relaxation. He made no mention of pain except in abnormal, complicated births. This attitude persisted for several centuries

## 3. The Church, the War on Women, and the Curse of Eve

- **Nature worship outlawed (late 2nd century onward)** — church-influenced governance swept Europe, destroying birthing rites, ceremonies, statues, and stone temples; anything connected to worship of nature or a "Mother Creator" was forbidden
- **Healers and midwives executed** — women's healing talents were recast as proof of a connection to the devil; midwives and doulas were publicly burned at the stake. The term "Wehmutters" (mothers of woe) was applied to any midwife who dared attend a birth
- **"The Curse of Eve" inserted into scripture** — Clement (later sainted) embedded the notion of a childbirth curse into Bible translations; previously no such curse appeared. This doctrine was used to justify the isolation and suffering of birthing women
- **Birthing women left to die** — women were deemed "undeserving" because they conceived in "carnal sin"; doctors were forbidden to assist even in complications. The only attendant permitted was the goat gelder, who cut the baby out while the mother was left to die. This period (2nd-16th century) entrenched the association of birth with agony and death

## 4. The Rise of Medical Obstetrics

- **Soranus rediscovered (15th century)** — the lost books prompted a re-examination of birthing attitudes, but women were not fully freed from subjugation; poorer women still received no mercy
- **Queen Victoria and anaesthesia (1853, 1857)** — her insistence on anaesthesia for her births made it standard in obstetrics, and because administering it required a hospital setting, upper-class mothers moved into hospitals while lower-class women stayed with midwives
- **All births treated as pathological (mid-1900s)** — following Dr. Joseph DeLee, doctors concluded that since normal births were rare in hospitals, all interventions used for highly complicated cases should be applied to every birth to "prevent the evils of labour"
- **Child-bed fever, not childbirth, killed women** — large numbers of women died from infections in filthy maternity wards staffed by inept or alcoholic physicians; Florence Nightingale's fundraising and reform eventually cleaned up the wards

## 5. The Natural Birth Counter-Movement

- **Grantly Dick-Read's Fear-Tension-Pain Syndrome** — the English obstetrician proposed that pain is not inherent in birthing; when a mother is taut with fear, her body is also taut, creating pain. His papers were rejected and he was ridiculed
- **Lamaze: focus and breathing** — Dr. Fernand Lamaze introduced the first widely adopted natural-birth approach, teaching women to use focus and breathing to distract from pain, but still assumed pain was present and taught women to push babies to crowning
- **Robert Bradley: relaxation-centred birth** — Bradley championed Dick-Read's relaxation approach and built a programme around it
- **Fear, not birth itself, was always the problem** — looking back with modern knowledge, extreme fear of complications and death (not the physiology of birth) caused women's bodies to tense and their cervixes to lock, creating the very agony that confirmed the myth. The answer to why women's bodies shut down can be found in one word: fear

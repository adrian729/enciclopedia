## 1. Early Signals of Approaching Labour

- **Practise labour** — tightening surges that prepare the uterus for birthing. For first babies, they usually appear toward the end of the seventh month; for subsequent pregnancies, as early as the sixth month. They are erratic and infrequent until the final month, then intervals shorten to 10–20 minutes. Notably, these surges produce pressure but not pain — possibly further evidence of the mind-body connection, since the mind "knows" the body is not yet in labour
- **Practise surges may be real labour** — some practitioners believe these surges do begin opening the cervix. As you approach term, pay closer attention rather than dismissing them; trust your instincts to recognise when real labour has started
- **Lightening** — several weeks before labour, the baby drops into the lower pelvis. Breathing becomes easier as the rib cage is freed, but pressure on the lower pelvis increases and walking changes noticeably
- **Vaginal discharge** — a clear or whitish discharge may increase due to higher blood flow to the region, signalling the body is preparing for birth

## 2. The Estimated Due Date

- **EDD is only an estimate** — one doctor calls it "The Guess Date." A more realistic framing is a birth month or segment of the month (e.g., "toward the end of September") rather than a single date
- **How EDD is calculated and why it is imprecise** — count back three months from the first day of the last menstrual period, then add 7 days. Recent studies suggest adding 15 days for first-time mothers and 10 days for mothers who have birthed before. Factors that skew the estimate: calendar months differ in length, menstrual cycles vary in duration and frequency, gestation length varies between pregnancies, and foetal development timelines differ just as children's do
- **Only ~5% of babies arrive on the EDD** — the normal gestation range is 265–300 days from the first day of the last period (average 282 days, i.e., ~40 weeks). A mother is not technically "post date" until 42 weeks. First-time mothers may reasonably go beyond 42 weeks
- **Multiple due dates are a red flag for imprecision** — many women today are given as many as three different due dates during pregnancy, which itself demonstrates how unreliable the calculation is

## 3. Resisting Pressure to Induce

- **Emotional toll of waiting** — once the EDD passes, anxious family members call, care providers increase surveillance, and each day can start and end with disappointment. Well-meaning friends share induction stories, and providers may subtly suggest convenience-based induction ("you can be home for the weekend")
- **Trust your baby's timing** — continue to relax and wait. The baby knows when it is time to be born. Resist the temptation to accept induction simply because the Guess Date has passed
- **Risks of unnecessary induction** — if the due date was miscalculated, induction can deliver a premature baby. If the cervix is not ripe, induction can lead to prolonged, painful labour and an increased chance of surgical birth. Even seemingly minor interventions ("just popping that bag," "doing a little sweep," a "little jump-start") can change the entire course of labour
- **Bishop's Score** — a clinical tool that rates cervical readiness for induction on five factors (position, consistency, effacement, dilation, station), each scored 0–3. A low score means the induction is likely to be difficult and may lead to lengthy, painful labour with higher surgical risk. A score of 8–9 suggests probable success but also that spontaneous labour may be near anyway. Always ask for your Bishop's Score before agreeing to induction
- **Induction only for valid medical indication** — ask for a clear explanation of why induction is recommended, along with its risks and benefits at that specific time. Many physicians will not consider artificial induction before 42 weeks in the absence of special circumstances

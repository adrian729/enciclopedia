## 1. The Baby's Transition

- **From womb to world** — inside the womb, the baby was nestled by warm membranes, soothed by swirling waters, rocked by the mother's movement, and surrounded by familiar voices; birth ends that secure environment abruptly
- **Sensory overload at birth** — the baby's first moments involve a rush of new stimuli: air brushing across skin, the roughness of fabric rubbing away the protective vernix. How these are managed shapes the baby's birth memory, which can affect personality and spirit for life
- **Ongoing brain development** — significant neurological and physical development continues after birth, making the quality of the postnatal environment especially important during the early weeks

## 2. Creating a Gentle Welcome

- **Birthing room as sanctuary** — the environment after birth should have the same reverence and calm as a place of worship; voices should be subdued and soft lighting restored immediately
- **Parents-only holding** — assuming all is well, only the mother and her partner should hold the baby initially; exposure to the scent and energy of others is overwhelming for a newborn still adjusting to breathing and life outside the womb
- **Protect from overstimulation** — most hospitals now offer at least one hour of uninterrupted family time, but the author stresses this is not nearly enough. In some cultures, parents and baby seclude themselves for two to three weeks in dimness and calm
- **Educate family and friends** — when parents explain that the early period is genuinely overwhelming for the newborn, most relatives will understand and honour requests to observe and extend wishes without passing the baby around

## 3. The Babymoon

- **A "honeymoon" for the new family** — the first weeks should be treated as a period of getting to know each other, analogous to a honeymoon; the focus is bonding, not hosting
- **Minimise outside contact** — simplify daily life to basic needs: wear pyjamas all day, order food in, and limit visitors. A constant stream of guests disrupts the calm the baby needs to adjust
- **Respond to cries** — the baby's cries are his way of communicating that he needs comforting and support; responding teaches the baby that he is accepted and that he is a loved and loving human being
- **Practical visitor policy** — allow friends and relatives to visit only if they are bringing a meal, doing laundry, shopping, or cleaning; this gets needs met while preserving the family's peace
- **Allow the baby time** — some babies transition to the outside world easily, others with difficulty; either way, keeping life as simple and womb-like as possible in those first weeks eases the adjustment

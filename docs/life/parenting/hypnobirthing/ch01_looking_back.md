## 1. Origin of the Programme

- **Maura's birth** — Mongan developed HypnoBirthing for her daughter Maura; grandson Kyle was born in Concord, New Hampshire without epidural, in a dimmed room with soft music — hospital staff were stunned a mother could labour this calmly
- **Beyond simple relaxation** — from the outset the programme introduced many advanced hypnosis techniques into the birthing classroom, not just simple relaxation alone
- **Organic spread** — word of mouth drove early growth: a nurse who witnessed Maura's birth immediately wanted the same for her own baby; hypnotherapists called asking to be trained; the National Guild of Hypnotists invited Mongan to present. No marketing — just people who saw it work

## 2. Early Growth and Medical Resistance

- **NBC Dateline exposure** — a full-hour TV feature generated ~5,000 calls/emails in weeks, but it also revealed a gap: the public was ready for natural birth long before doctors were
- **Nurses embraced it, doctors didn't** — nurses and midwives became early adopters; most doctors called HypnoBirths "remarkable" but treated them as flukes and showed no curiosity about how to replicate them
- **Friedman Curve pressure** — doctors would interrupt calm labours to suggest membrane ruptures or Pitocin drips if the mother was not progressing in line with the Friedman Curve, especially when their shift was about to end. A beautiful calm birthing was often given over to manipulation with little thought to the dramatic change in the mother's comfort level
- **Practitioner network built the movement** — growth came from trained HypnoBirthing practitioners (midwives, doulas, hypnotherapists), not from hospital endorsement. Midwife Nancy Wainer called it "the missing link we have all been seeking for years"
- **UK expansion via midwifery model** — the first certification workshop at St. Thomas Hospital (London) introduced Mongan to the midwifery model, which is significantly more mother-baby friendly than the American obstetric model. The two philosophies were blended into the UK edition

## 3. The Shift Toward Natural Birth

- **Hospital environments transformed** — many facilities replaced the frightening apparatus (IV poles, tubes, wires, belts) with homelike rooms: birth balls, tubs, dimmed lights, queen beds, walking paths. Some hospitals conceal medical equipment around the corner so it's available within seconds but not visible to the labouring mother — removing visual fear triggers matters for maintaining relaxation
- **"Purple pushing" declined** — the old practice of scrunching up the body, holding breath, and forcefully pushing the baby out is still portrayed in movies and TV, but many settings are moving away from it. A quarter century later, some still subscribe to it, but the shift away is well underway
- **Feeding in labour** — hospitals began allowing low-risk mothers to eat during labour. The physiological basis: when endorphins are present and the constrictor hormone catecholamine is not, digestion is not arrested because the mother's body is not in a state of alarm
- **Birth centres and home births expanded** — a vast increase in birth centres offering a homelike atmosphere for couples seeking natural birth; home births are considered an option by families of all kinds of lifestyles. Many hospitals are also accommodating families who want to birth naturally yet have the comfort of medical staff on hand
- **Baby stays with the mother** — progressive hospitals began keeping the baby with the mother immediately after birth (bringing identification bands to the baby rather than taking the baby away) and offering family beds where the infant rooms in and sleeps with the parents

## 4. A Counter-Shift: Over-Medicalisation

- **Scheduled inductions on the EDD** — a parallel trend emerged where women are told (not advised) they will be induced on their estimated due date if they have not gone into labour by then. This ignores that the EDD is just an estimate and that first-time mothers average 41.3 weeks of gestation
- **Fear-driven "dangerous complications"** — growing numbers of women are being flagged for possible complications that then trigger strict protocols, frequent interruptions, and overuse of technology that make normal birth almost impossible. The natural rhythm and flow of labour is destroyed and, as a result, labour can often go awry
- **Parental input marginalised** — in this model, the parents' wishes, emotions, and expectations are swept aside in favour of chemical, chronological, and technological factors

## 5. What This Means for Birthing Families

- **"Standard care" is not automatically evidence-based** — "routine protocol" does not always mean optimal treatment. Parents often accept interventions by default because they don't know what they don't know. Educating yourself before labour is essential
- **Choose your provider and facility before labour, not during it** — understand your care provider's philosophy and what is routine "standard protocol" for the facility where you intend to birth. The time to make these decisions is before the birthing day, not when you are in labour
- **Asking for less, not more** — HypnoBirthing families are not requesting special treatment; they are requesting fewer unnecessary interventions when everything indicates a normal, healthy pregnancy
- **Consumer-led change** — Mongan argues the next paradigm shift must come from educated families, not from within the medical establishment. The families who have the best birth experiences are those who take responsibility for learning their options and actively selecting providers who will support their approach

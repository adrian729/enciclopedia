## 1. General Exercise Approach

- **Exercise regularly but do not make it an ordeal** — vary exercises and weave them into daily activities (some can be done in bed upon waking or before sleep)
- **Walking is among the best prenatal exercises** — park farther away, take stairs, walk within your building at work; brisk walking is fine early to mid-pregnancy, but slow down in later months so blood is not diverted from the uterus to arms and legs, reducing oxygen to the baby
- **Join a prenatal fitness group** — the social element helps consistency, but avoid groups that trade negative birth stories

## 2. Posture and Back Care

- **Imaginary string alignment** — envision a string from the front of your earlobe through your shoulders and hipbone to just behind the ankle bone; keeping your head in line prevents "leading with your head," tilts the pelvis correctly, and avoids the premature "pregnancy waddle" of leaning back and projecting the abdomen forward
- **Avoid slouching into the pelvic area** — slouching encourages the baby to settle into a less favourable position for birth; place a pillow on bucket car seats to level them, and avoid recliners entirely
- **Birth ball (exercise ball) for sitting** — using one at your desk or at home keeps you erect, tones inner thighs and pelvic region, and doubles as a rocking surface during labour; fill the bottom with approximately 2 inches of sand for stability
- **Pelvic rock / pelvic tilt** — relieves back strain, strengthens abdominal muscles, increases lower-back flexibility, and promotes spinal alignment
  - **Standing method** — hold a sturdy chair, lean forward from hips with buttocks back and back straight, then bend knees and tuck buttocks under; repeat several times
  - **Lying method (early pregnancy only)** — on your back with knees bent, tighten lower abdominal and buttock muscles so tailbone rises and the small of the back presses to the floor; hold, release, arch; repeat. Avoid lying flat once the baby gains weight

## 3. Inner Thigh and Leg Toning

- **Why it matters** — during the birthing phase you may need your legs in unusual positions for Birth Breathing; the muscles in your inner thigh will need to be ready
- **Position One** — sit on floor or bed with soles of feet together, elbows resting on inner knees, and gently press knees down; over time, gradually pull heels toward crotch until knees almost rest on the floor. The birthing companion can assist by pressing upward under the knees to create resistance while the mother presses down, then reversing the direction
- **Position Two** — resting on tailbone with knees bent upward toward shoulders, palms against inner knees, push knees outward; bring them together and repeat about ten times per session
- **Lifting safely** — always bend at the knees, not the waist, and do not attempt to lift heavy objects

## 4. The Leaping Frog

- **Origin and purpose** — a forward squat from Virgin Islands midwives, used worldwide; it tones muscles and serves as one of the best positions for the birthing phase
- **Benefits during Birth Breathing** — widens the pelvic opening, relaxes and opens perineal tissues, helps avoid tearing and episiotomy, relieves lower-back strain, increases oxygen supply to the baby, shortens the birth path, allows the mother to see the birth, and uses gravity
- **Two arm positions** — arms thrust forward inside spread knees, or arms behind at the sides of the hips (the second gives clear access for both baby and attendant)
- **Requires practice** — Western women do not squat routinely, so untoned muscles risk pain or injury; practise regularly in advance, and have the birthing companion assist by holding hands or lending knees to lean on as you lower down
- **Converts to Polar Bear position** — from the squat, lean chin and forearms onto pillows in front while buttocks extend upward; this can help the baby shift position during labour

## 5. Pelvic Floor Exercises

- **Among the most important prenatal toning** — these exercises (sometimes called Kegels) strengthen the figure-eight muscle network around the vaginal and anal region used during Birth Breathing
- **Additional benefits beyond birth** — faster postpartum recovery, prevention of age-related urinary problems, enhanced lovemaking, better bladder/bowel confidence as pregnancy advances
- **Technique** — in a sitting position, constrict the lowest anal and vaginal muscles and progressively tighten upward (count one to ten, tightening with each number); hold for several seconds, then release slowly
- **Self-check** — stopping urine mid-flow confirms you are engaging the correct muscles, but do this only once to verify — repeating it can cause a urinary tract infection
- **Frequency** — five to ten repetitions per session, several times a day; these can be done anywhere — at work, at home, while driving or walking

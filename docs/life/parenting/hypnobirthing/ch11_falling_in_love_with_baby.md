## 1. Pre-Birth Bonding and Why It Matters

- **Start bonding before birth** — connecting socially and emotionally with your unborn baby as soon as you know you are pregnant lays the foundation for a lifelong relationship and tells the baby he is welcome and wanted
- **Both parents influence the pre-born baby** — reciprocal nurturing between parents sends a strong message of security to the baby; dads getting involved in nurturing both mother and baby is essential, not optional
- **Babies are cognisant in the womb** — Dr. Thomas Verny's research in foetology showed that the womb is the baby's first classroom, where he develops emotionally, physically, spiritually, and socially
- **Babies remember birth** — Dr. David Chamberlain's research found that babies are active participants in birth and carry the imprint of that experience throughout their lives; an undrugged newborn's alert, knowing gaze confirms this awareness
- **Measurable benefits** — a University of Salzburg study found that parents who interacted with their pre-born babies had easier pregnancies and birthings, approached birth with relaxed confidence, and later displayed softer, more balanced caregiving. Their babies had fewer premature births, fewer low-birthweight cases, better socialisation, and overall better health

## 2. What the Baby Perceives in the Womb

- **Emotional crossover** — just as everything the mother ingests crosses the placenta, so do emotions; love, play, and music reinforce the baby's sense of security, while screaming, loud noises, and emotional upsets cause the baby's pulse rate to rise abruptly
- **Sensory responsiveness** — pre-born babies react to vibrations, stroking, tapping, rubbing, squeezing, conversation, voices, music, light, heat, cold, pressing, teasing, loud noises, TV sounds, and humour
- **Music and singing** — babies exposed to soft music and singing in utero were calmer, happier, and better adjusted after birth; Dr. Michael Lazarev emphasises that if you listen to your unborn baby, he will let you know what sounds he prefers

## 3. Practical Pre-Birth Parenting Activities

- **Daily relaxation practice** — learn and practise relaxation techniques daily; the baby is listening to the music and calming suggestions at the same time as the mother
- **Physical play** — sway, dance, rub, pat, squeeze, and press gently on the belly to interact with the baby through movement and touch
- **Talk and read** — carry on conversations, say affirmations, read stories with animated voices, and play children's tapes so the baby becomes familiar with the parents' voices
- **Soothing sounds** — play ocean, bird, wind, soft piano, guitar, flute, harp, and nature sounds to help the baby develop a wider awareness
- **Keepsakes for later** — write letters or keep a journal for the baby, take pregnancy photographs, record messages, videotape the birth with a birthing-day message, and involve siblings in preparing baby's room

## 4. The "Be the Baby" Exercise

- **Perspective shift** — imagine yourself as the baby developing in the womb, experiencing the surroundings, absorbing the emotions and moods of those around you
- **Self-assessment questions** — reflect on how welcome, loved, and secure the baby feels; what kind of pace, atmosphere, and nourishment you are providing; whether the environment is free of smoke, alcohol, and drugs; and whether the baby can expect guidance rather than punishment
- **Identify changes** — after reflecting on the questions, determine what adjustments you can make to improve the baby's environment and emotional experience before birth

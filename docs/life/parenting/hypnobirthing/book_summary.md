## 1. The Thesis

Marie Mongan argues that birthing, when a pregnancy is normal and healthy, is not an emergency and was never designed to be painful. The pain that Western cultures assume to be inherent in labour is, in her account, the product of fear — absorbed from generations of war-story conditioning, medical over-management, and religious doctrine — which tenses the very muscles that need to yield. The **Mongan Method** frames birth preparation as the systematic removal of that fear through education about birthing physiology, daily conditioning in self-hypnosis and deep relaxation, specific breathing techniques, careful nutrition and toning, thoughtful selection of provider and setting, and a partnership with an informed **birthing companion**. The promise is not that pain will be abolished but that a mother approaching her **birthing** without fear in a supportive environment is usually able to experience it calmly, comfortably, and joyfully. Throughout, Mongan reframes the vocabulary of labour: tightenings of the uterus are **surges**, not contractions; the event is a birthing, not a delivery; the person by the mother's side is a birthing companion, not a coach. Words act on the body through the mind, so the right words remove the mental scripts of alarm before they can be triggered.

## 2. Origins of the Method

HypnoBirthing began in January 1990 in Concord, New Hampshire, when Mongan's daughter Maura gave birth to her grandson Kyle in a dimmed hospital room with soft music, no epidural, and staff who had never seen a labour proceed so calmly. Mongan had developed the programme for Maura after compiling twenty years of her own reading and practice into a small guide; the success of Maura's birth and two other "pioneer mothers" that year turned it into a formal programme that, from the outset, introduced advanced hypnosis techniques into the birthing classroom.

Mongan's path had long pointed this way. A schoolgirl research paper led her to medical textbooks that treated birth almost entirely as pathology, one even warning doctors to beware "the educated, sophisticated woman who may harbour a notion that she knows how to bring a baby into the world." A 1950 Life Magazine cover story on Grace New Haven Hospital's adoption of Dick-Read's theories, showing a calm mother who had given birth "with no fear, little pain," was the first published evidence she found matching her intuition. Her own first two births were traumatic — planned relaxation overridden by nurses forcing ether on her while the babies were extracted with forceps — while her third and fourth, after she advocated fiercely, were calm and quick. Her farm cat Squatter provided another reference point: choosing a dark nesting box, purring through labour, and birthing five kittens unaided, but instantly shutting down her labour and fleeing when dogs fought in the yard, returning only when safe. For Mongan this embodied two core claims: undisturbed mammalian birthing is gentle and self-directed, and fear shuts labour down.

Early growth was organic — nurses, hypnotherapists, and midwives who had seen the work asked to be trained — and a 1990s NBC Dateline feature produced roughly five thousand enquiries in weeks. The programme spread through a network of certified practitioners and eventually reached over forty-eight countries. The UK edition was shaped by Mongan's exposure to the British midwifery model, which she describes as significantly more mother-and-baby friendly than the American obstetric model.

## 3. A Brief History of Birth and Fear

To show that fear of birth is historically constructed rather than innate, the book traces a long arc. In the pre-Christian ancient world, birth was treated as the highest manifestation of nature, and scholar Helen Wessels found no evidence that ancient women considered childbirth a curse. Grecian medicine inherited this view: Hippocrates warned against "meddlesome interference" and established formal midwifery; Aristotle recommended deep relaxation during birthing; Soranus, in the last century B.C., compiled their work and mentioned pain only in abnormal, complicated births.

From the late second century onwards, church-influenced governance swept Europe, destroying the old birthing rites, executing midwives as witches, and inserting the notion of the "Curse of Eve" into scriptural translations. Women were deemed undeserving of assistance because they had conceived in "carnal sin"; the only attendant permitted in many places was the goat gelder, who cut the baby out while the mother was left to die. For roughly fourteen centuries the association of birth with agony and death was entrenched. Queen Victoria's insistence on anaesthesia for her births in 1853 and 1857 later made it standard in obstetrics and drew upper-class women into hospitals, while mid-twentieth-century obstetrics, following Joseph DeLee, concluded that since hospital births rarely looked normal, every birth should be treated as if complicated. Most of the deaths women suffered in that era, Mongan notes, came not from childbirth itself but from child-bed fever in filthy wards.

Grantly Dick-Read's counter-thesis, developed from the 1920s, is the intellectual foundation of HypnoBirthing: he proposed that pain was not inherent in birthing, and that when fear made a mother taut her body went taut and created the pain. His papers were dismissed unread and his licence threatened. Fernand Lamaze later taught focus and breathing to distract from pain, but Lamaze still assumed pain was present and taught women to push the baby to crowning. Robert Bradley championed Dick-Read's relaxation approach. Mongan concludes that fear, not the physiology of birth, was always the problem — and its answer was "hiding in plain sight."

## 4. Fear, the Uterus, and the Autonomic Nervous System

The mechanics of birthing follow the design of the uterus itself. It has three muscle layers, two of which matter for birth: an outer layer of vertical muscles running up the back, and an inner layer of horizontal circular muscles thickest just above the cervix. During a **surge**, the vertical muscles tighten and draw upward, pulling the relaxed circular muscles back; the cervix progressively thins and opens, and the baby is nudged down in a wavelike motion. When the mother is relaxed, the two sets work in harmony and many HypnoBirthing mothers report the tightening as their only sensation.

The Autonomic Nervous System is the body's involuntary communication network, and Mongan gives its two branches memorable names: the sympathetic branch she calls the **Emergency Room** and the parasympathetic branch the **Healing Room**. The Emergency Room triggers fight, flight or freeze and should occupy only two to five percent of a life; the Healing Room maintains harmony and balance and should occupy ninety-five to ninety-eight. Perceived threats trigger the Emergency Room as strongly as real ones, so the fearful birth stories a mother absorbs are processed as actual danger.

When a mother enters labour with unresolved fear, her body releases the stressor hormone catecholamine. Since fighting or fleeing is impossible, the body freezes: blood is redirected away from the uterus, and nurses and midwives, Mongan reports, describe seeing the uterus of a frightened mother turn white from lack of blood. Starved of oxygen, the lower circular muscles lock instead of yielding; the upper vertical muscles keep pushing against the locked cervix; the result is real pain and often a stalled labour labelled "failure to progress" (FTP) — initials that also abbreviate Dick-Read's Fear–Tension–Pain Syndrome. The corrective chemistry runs in reverse: relaxation speeds the release of endorphins, which cannot co-exist with catecholamine; when endorphins flow, oxytocin is released in the right amounts, birthing muscles function, and discomfort is minimised. The more a mother practises, the faster her body can reach this state.

Mongan devotes a full chapter to releasing fears before labour because unresolved worries easily surface during birthing. Following Dr. Louis Mehl-Madronna's study on turning breech babies with hypnosis, she catalogues common sources: the mother's own birth story, others' birth stories, previous labours, confidence as a parent, support network, relationship security, finances, and — particularly importantly — any personal history of physical or sexual abuse, which is deeply body-associated and can cause the body to shut down or resist during labour. For stubborn fears she recommends private fear-release hypnotherapy sessions.

## 5. The Power of the Mind

Four Laws of the Mind underpin the programme. The **Law of Psycho-Physical Response** holds that every thought or emotion produces a corresponding physiological response; the body is the "robot" that acts out whatever the mind perceives as real (Krazner's Robot Theory). This, Mongan says, is the most important law for birthing. The **Law of Repetition** holds that a thought expressed often enough is accepted as reality, which is why daily practice of affirmations and the **Rainbow Relaxation** recording is essential. The **Law of Harmonious Attraction** describes an "echo" or "boomerang" by which the energy of what one puts out returns in kind. The **Law of Motivation** captures how a highly motivated body can override ordinary pain signals — a football player with a sprained ankle feels nothing while trying to win the game.

Self-hypnosis is not stage magic. All hypnosis is self-hypnosis; no one can make a person act against their morals under it. Everyone already enters light hypnotic states daily while absorbed in work or reacting to a film. During labour, a mother in self-hypnosis will hear conversations, remains fully in control, and is aware of surges but experiences them comfortably. The American Medical Association recognised hypnosis as a beneficial therapy in 1957.

Language is part of this conditioning. Words trigger thoughts, repeated thoughts become feelings, feelings become beliefs, and beliefs shape experience. The book offers a lexicon that replaces medicalised terms with HypnoBirthing terms throughout: uterine surges or waves for contractions, birth companion for coach, birthing for labouring, birth path for birth canal, birthing time for due date, pressure or sensation or tightening for pain, membranes release for water breaking, birth breathing for pushing, special circumstances for complications, uterine seal for mucous plug, birth show for bloody show, thinning and opening for effacing and dilating, nearly complete for transition, pre-labour warm-ups for Braxton-Hicks, resting labour for stalled labour, practise labour for false labour. The point is not affectation but to remove the mental script of alarm each word carries.

Mongan contrasts two self-images. The Dependent Model is uninformed, submissive, passive, vulnerable, helpless. The Whole-Person Model is knowledgeable, powerful, involved, trusting, self-sufficient, confident. Without conscious awareness, some mothers construct high-maintenance pregnancies or dramatic births because the attention rewards the dependent self-image. Her argument is that you birth the way you live.

## 6. Bonding Before Birth

The womb, in the book's telling, is the baby's first classroom. Dr. Thomas Verny's research in foetology is cited for the claim that babies develop emotionally, physically, spiritually, and socially in utero, and Dr. David Chamberlain's *Babies Remember Birth* for the claim that children and adults can recall events from birth and the womb. The book asks parents to treat pre-birth bonding as a social and emotional relationship already under way.

Everything the mother ingests crosses the placenta; so do her emotions. Love, play, and music reinforce the baby's sense of security, while screaming and emotional upsets cause the baby's pulse to rise abruptly. A University of Salzburg study is cited for the finding that parents who interacted with their pre-born babies had easier pregnancies and births, fewer premature deliveries, fewer low-birthweight cases, and children with better socialisation. Mongan recommends daily relaxation practice, gentle physical play with the belly, talking and reading aloud, and keeping letters and recordings as keepsakes. The "Be the Baby" exercise asks the parents to imagine themselves as the baby in the womb and adjust the environment and emotional climate accordingly.

## 7. Daily Practice: The Six Techniques and the Routines

The programme rests on six component techniques: education, relaxation, breathing, visualisation, deepening, and affirmations. Daily practice is non-negotiable. A skier would never compete without conditioning, and a mother cannot attend classes and hope relaxation will occur on its own when labour begins; the time invested can cut both the duration and the effort of labour, because the mind forms a pattern it can respond to automatically when birthing starts.

The practice environment matters: same time and place each day, a softly-lit spot, warmth, an empty bladder, and a HypnoBirthing audio track — Stephen Halpern's *Comfort Zone* is recommended because it matches the **Rainbow Relaxation**. The two recommended positions are Nesting (pillows under head, arms, knees) and the Lateral (left-side-lying) position; lying flat on the back in later pregnancy is avoided because the weight of the baby can compress the inferior vena cava. Facial relaxation is the doorway in: because the lower jaw is connected to the vaginal opening, relaxing the jaw relaxes the vaginal area. Once mastered — eyelids closing without forcing, relaxation spreading through face and jaws, tongue tip placed where upper teeth meet the palate, shoulders drooping — it is an instant entry point usable at any moment during labour.

Beyond these foundations, the book offers interchangeable relaxation techniques. Progressive Relaxation assigns each body region a number and sweeps relaxation through each zone on an exhale, targeting a body as limp as a soft doll. Disappearing Letters is faster: the mother mentally recites "AAA-BBB-CCC-D…" while visualising the letters rolling by, and by the first or second C most find the remaining letters erasing as the body goes limp. **Light Touch Massage** is a partner technique: the companion uses the back of the fingers to lightly stroke the mother's skin, which stimulates the erector pili and releases endorphins — and since endorphins and catecholamine cannot co-exist, creating endorphins blocks the stress hormones that cause tension. The strokes follow a V-pattern up from the base of the tailbone or a horizontal figure-eight across the back. Nipple stimulation as part of Light Touch is delayed until the very end of the term because it also produces oxytocin. Anchoring sets a lasting imprint through association with a gesture, sound, image, or touch: a shoulder-press anchor is used for birthing, with the companion pressing a hand on the mother's shoulder during practice while instructing her that she will immediately relax twice as deeply, until the response becomes automatic.

## 8. The Three Breaths

The programme uses exactly three breathing techniques, each named for when it is used and each covering a phase of labour: **Calm Breathing**, **Surge Breathing**, and **Birth Breathing**.

- **Calm Breathing** — Used for visualisation practice, for resuming relaxation between surges, and any time tension arises. Technique: eyes closed, tongue tip on the palate; inhale through the nose to a count of four, feeling the stomach rise; pause; exhale through the nose to a count of eight, directing breath energy down to the back of the throat while the shoulders droop.
- **Surge Breathing** — Used throughout the entire thinning and opening phase, with every surge, until it is time to breathe the baby down. It assists the vertical muscles as they draw the horizontal muscles up, so breath works with the body rather than against it; mothers often find surges last less time. Technique: lie in a lateral position, hands across the top of the abdomen; using only the abdominal muscles, inhale to a rapid count of twenty while expanding the abdomen into the cupped fingers, pause, and exhale at the same pace.
- **Birth Breathing** — Replaces Surge Breathing once the cervix has opened enough for the baby to pass. It assists the **Natural Expulsive Reflex (NER)** — the body's own pulsations that move the baby down — providing a gentle nudge, not force. Technique: mouth softly closed, breath through the nose only; breathe only downward and only during a surge; direct breath energy to the lower back of the throat and let that thrust move down behind the baby in the shape of a "J," with the vaginal outlet staying open between breaths; use Calm Breathing between surges. The best place to condition Birth Breathing is on the toilet during daily bowel movements. Many mothers report needing only two or three Birth Breaths for full emergence.

The book is pointed about why it rejects forced pushing. When unanaesthetised birth returned via Lamaze, practitioners who had never seen natural descent assumed the baby had to be actively pushed out, and the "purple pushing" protocol became entrenched. Physiologically, forced pushing creates stress that causes the sphincters of the vagina to close ahead of the descending baby and limits oxygen to the baby, often causing the heart-rate decelerations that trigger the very interventions the family hoped to avoid. Mongan points out the paradox that women with epidurals are often allowed to "labour down," while mothers who planned a natural birth are still expected to forcefully push.

## 9. Visualisation and Ultra-Deepening

Breathing and relaxation are the daily essentials; visualisation techniques are labour-day tools. The exception is **Rainbow Relaxation**, the cornerstone exercise, practised every day. Using Calm Breathing to enter relaxation, the mother pictures herself on a bed of strawberry-coloured mist, progressing through each colour of the rainbow (strawberry, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo) and ending with white, letting each colour sweep from head to feet. If she drifts off, she is not actually asleep; the subconscious continues to process the session. Any uncomfortable images can be substituted, because all hypnosis is self-hypnosis. The companion practises with her frequently, reciting the sequence while stroking her hand and arm, both to condition her response to his voice and to familiarise the baby with it.

An alternative Birth Companion's Reading, adapted from Henry Leo Bolduc, combines imagery of ocean waves, endorphin release, and the moment of holding the baby after birth. Two further visualisations support specific parts of labour: the Opening Blossom envisions the perineum opening like the petals of a rose, and Blue Satin Ribbons reimagines the lower circular fibres of the cervix as soft blue ribbons yielding to the vertical muscles. The Arm-Wrist Relaxation Test offers visible proof that conditioning is working: the mother pictures helium-filled balloons tied to her right wrist, and despite her efforts to hold it down the arm rises six to ten inches; each time, fewer balloons are needed.

Ultra-deepening techniques produce the almost amnesiac state in which the mother goes within to her birthing body and her baby. **Glove Relaxation** is the one Mongan considers among the most effective directives during labour: the mother imagines a soft silver glove of natural endorphins slipped onto her right hand, the fingers tingling, the hand going numb as though plunged into icy slush. The companion strokes her hand and arm; the numbness spreads up until it feels like wood or leather, and she can then mentally place that hand on any part of her body — particularly the lower pelvic area — to transfer the numbness there.

The **Depthometer** is an inverted-thermometer visualisation: a flexible thermometer inside the body, bulb above the forehead, tube extending to the toes, full of a clear fluid of natural relaxation. As she counts down from forty to zero, the fluid flows down one number at a time, with relaxation doubling at each decade mark. At the bottom she reaches the "control centre" of her inner mind, where the Sensory Gate Control Valve is located — visualised as a round valve labelled "HypnoBirthing Valve" on a control panel. Turning the indicator switch to "off" (at the eight o'clock position) shuts off sensation; the switch is always returned to off and self-hypnosis released before moving about. Time distortion is folded into this state: the mother suggests that every five minutes will feel like one, and her companion later escalates this to every twenty minutes feeling like five.

## 10. Physical Preparation

Two chapters on nutrition and toning round out the pregnancy-long preparation. Water is the single most important daily habit (eight 200 ml glasses, with urine the colour of light lemonade as the self-test). The basic nutritional formula is to eat vegetables, consume seventy-five to one hundred grams of protein a day, choose natural fats over processed oils, and "wipe out whites" (no white bread, rice, sugar, flour, or starches unless protein). The Clean 15 and Dirty Dozen help parents prioritise organic for produce whose skins absorb chemicals. Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, processed meats, raw fish, and refined foods are avoided. Perfectionism is counterproductive — stress harms pregnancy.

Exercise is folded into daily activities. Walking is among the best prenatal exercises, slowed in later months so blood is not diverted from the uterus. Posture is aligned by an imaginary string running from earlobe through shoulder and hipbone to behind the ankle, avoiding the "pregnancy waddle" and the slouching that encourages the baby into a less favourable position. An exercise ball keeps the mother upright; the pelvic rock or tilt relieves back strain; inner-thigh toning prepares for the unusual positions used during Birth Breathing.

Two named positions stand out. The **Leaping Frog** is a forward squat Mongan attributes to Virgin Islands midwives: it widens the pelvic opening, relaxes perineal tissues, uses gravity, increases oxygen to the baby, shortens the birth path, and lets the mother see the birth. Because Western women do not squat routinely, it is practised in advance with the companion's support. From the squat, leaning chin and forearms onto pillows while the buttocks extend upward converts to the **Polar Bear** — not a birthing position but a recovery position used when the baby is in a less-than-optimal orientation, allowing the baby to back away and rotate. The Rebozo technique, developed by midwife Guadalupe Trueba, uses a long scarf placed under the mother's abdomen and lifted upward while she is in Polar Bear, lifting the baby out of position so it can re-enter in a better orientation.

Pelvic floor exercises (sometimes called Kegels) strengthen the figure-eight muscle network around the vaginal and anal region used during Birth Breathing. Perineal massage starts six to eight weeks before the estimated due date, in daily sessions of at least five minutes, using virgin olive, sweet, almond or apricot oil. With thumb or fingers oiled to the second knuckle, the mother presses downward on the area between vagina and rectum and slides upward along the sides in a U-shaped sling motion, supported by the rosebud visualisation. The goal is a soft perineal rim that gently unfolds and lets the baby slip past.

## 11. Choosing the Team and Setting

Choosing the right provider and facility is one of the highest-leverage decisions parents make: "You can't grow orchids in the Arctic." Parents should think of themselves as hiring managers interviewing candidates for the most important job of the birthing day; if a mother leaves a visit feeling less than enthusiastic, her inner knowing is sending a message.

The book surveys UK midwifery options — community midwives in groups of six to eight, midwife-led NHS birth centres, independent midwives hired privately, and the Domino scheme — and describes midwives as guardians of normal birth for low-risk women. Obstetricians are trained surgeons specialising in complications and are the appropriate choice for high-risk pregnancies. For mothers seeking vaginal birth after caesarean (VBAC), HypnoBirthing is especially favourable because its gentle breathing techniques avoid forced pushing. Sample questions are provided: how often they perform caesareans and why, whether they will postpone induction until forty-two weeks, how long they will wait after membrane release for spontaneous labour, whether they are comfortable with a mother breathing the baby down. A doula works behind the scenes, liaising with staff and interpreting the situation, freeing the birthing companion to focus entirely on the mother.

Mongan lays out four management models. **Active Management of Labour (AML)** originated in late 1960s–70s Ireland to reduce caesareans by preventing long labours, but has had the opposite effect; it is characterised by scheduled induction, early and frequent augmentation, mandatory IV, continuous electronic foetal monitoring, total food restriction, routine epidural, coached forced pushing, routine episiotomy, and immediate cord clamping. **Standard or Conventional Management** mixes AML with some instinctive-physiological practices. **Management for Instinctive Birthing** is typical in free-standing birth centres and home births: managed only when needed, with parents and provider working as a team. **Parental Self-Management** (Pure or Unassisted birth) is the fourth.

Setting matters as much as provider. Some hospitals have homelike midwifery-led units with the same interventionist protocols underneath; a smaller number have allocated entire floors to natural birth, with no monitors in the room and staff selected to support unmedicated birth. Free-standing birth centres serve only healthy women, carry less high-tech apparatus, and have safety outcomes comparable to hospitals. For homebirth, a 1976 Stanford-affiliated study by Dr. Lewis Mehl-Madronna of 2,092 births found identical morbidity outcomes for home and hospital births, with 5% of homebirth mothers receiving medication versus 75% in hospital. Tours should happen early, because a facility may teach gentle birthing in its childbirth classes yet impose routine intervention on arrival.

## 12. When Baby is Breech or Late

The normal turning window is between weeks thirty-two and thirty-seven, when the baby rotates head-down into vertex position, drawn by gravity. The turn often happens in sleep and goes unnoticed. Fear and tension can delay it: if the uterus stays taut, the baby lacks space to complete the rotation. When breech persists, the options are to help the baby turn, to birth vaginally in breech position, or to resort to surgical birth; caesarean should not be the first avenue.

Mehl-Madronna's study of two hundred women with breech babies at thirty-six-plus weeks is cited for the power of hypnotherapy to help the turn. Against a baseline spontaneous turn rate of about twelve percent after week thirty-seven, eighty-one of the hundred women who received hypnotherapy saw their babies turn to vertex, compared with twenty-six of the hundred who did not. Sessions guide the mother into deep relaxation while she visualises her baby turning and the uterus becoming pliable. The estimated ten hours per mother turned out to average four, and half the successful turns required only one session.

Going past the estimated due date is not, in itself, a cause for intervention. The EDD — "The Guess Date," as one doctor calls it — is an estimate, and many women are given as many as three different due dates. Only about five percent of babies arrive on the EDD; normal gestation is 265–300 days from the first day of the last period, averaging 282 days, and first-time mothers average 41.3 weeks. A mother is not technically post-date until 42 weeks.

When gentle initiation is needed, Mongan prefers "hugs before drugs." Hot and spicy foods stimulate the digestive system, which stirs the birthing muscles. Lovemaking is powerful: semen contains prostaglandin that softens the cervix, and nipple or clitoral stimulation triggers oxytocin. Walking, warm baths, fear-release sessions, and professional acupressure and acupuncture are also offered; these points must never be stimulated during pregnancy except for induction. If artificial induction becomes necessary, minimal dosage is requested and the Pitocin or Syntocinon is stopped once the body has established its own rhythm. Before consenting, parents should ask for their Bishop's Score, which rates cervical readiness on five factors — position, consistency, effacement, dilation, station — each scored 0 to 3; a low score means a long labour with higher surgical risk.

## 13. Labour: The Unfolding

The HypnoBirthing map of labour is deliberately simple. Labour runs from when the cervix begins to thin and open until the baby is born, and the programme recognises only two phases: the Thinning and Opening Phase and the Birthing Phase. Early signals are release of membranes, release of the **uterine seal** (the "birth show"), surges up to thirty minutes apart, a feeling of constipation, and surges forming a pattern. Pre-labour warm-ups — what other methods call Braxton-Hicks — appear earlier as erratic tightenings that produce pressure but not pain.

When membranes release, the mother checks the fluid is clear and without putrid odour, calls her care provider, and states her wish to remain at home until labour is under way. Infection risk is overstated: infection is possible but rare, because bacteria must be actively introduced. Accepting a vaginal exam is often the first step toward induction. Labour most likely starts on its own within twenty-four hours. Early labour is settled into with gentle breathing down on each surge and the rosebud visualisation; because the calm approach inhibits catecholamine, digestion continues normally, so the mother snacks and drinks. When she signals readiness, the companion strokes her arm and recites cues from the Birth Companion's Prompt Card.

Once labour advances, the mother's role is to "do nothing" — to stay loose and limp while the body does its work. Labour is a continuum, not a sequence of fragments, and dilation numbers should be ignored; HypnoBirthing mothers often jump from four centimetres to seven or eight between exams. Hallmarks of progress include alternating feelings of overheating and cold, a spot of blood on the sanitary pad when the mother gets up to empty her bladder, brief digestive reactions (hiccupping, burping, nausea), and the "urge to escape" — a sudden "I don't think I want to do this any longer," which Mongan calls the most exciting hallmark because it means the baby's birth is imminent. Clutching or curling into a foetal position creates counterproductive tension; a 1997 University of Texas Southwestern Medical Centre study is cited for finding no significant difference in labour length between women who walked and those who rested. Water birth is an extraordinarily good complement.

Slow or resting labour is normal: labour commonly accelerates, slows, rests, and accelerates again, and the worst response is to panic and accept augmentation. After a calm nap, many mothers resume active labour. The birth ball, bath or birth pool, warm shower, humour, and nipple stimulation in privacy can all help restart it. Electronic Foetal Monitors, introduced in the 1980s for women with complications, are identified as a particular drawback for low-risk families because caregivers fixate on tracings and intervene to "fix" a labour, after which the baby often ends up worse off from the fixing itself.

Near the end of the thinning and opening phase, surges become closer and stronger. When the body signals downward fullness, the mother switches from Surge Breathing to Birth Breathing. Time distortion sets in, an amnesiac dreamlike state takes over, and awareness narrows to the baby and the birthing body. The Pelvic Station measures descent: negative numbers mean the head is above the pelvic midsection, zero means engaged, positive numbers mean descending toward birth. From this point on, discomfort arises only if the mother slips out of the relaxed state.

## 14. Crowning and Emergence

In the birthing phase, body and baby work together through the **Natural Expulsive Reflex (NER)** to move the baby down; the mother assists with Birth Breathing rather than directed pushing. Several positions are available: the toilet (an instinctive release position, moved from as crowning approaches); the lateral; a slanted "J" just above the tailbone; a companion-supported squat; a birthing stool; a companion-supported upright; the Leaping Frog; hands-and-knees (which lets the baby back up and reorient); and the birth ball.

Birth Breathing is the reverse of Slow Breathing — a short deep breath in and a J-shaped breath down behind the baby — and the mother visualises the vagina opening like the petals of a rose as the baby reaches the perineal rim. Mothers report needing only three or four Birth Breaths to bring the baby past the perineum. Gradual thinning of the perineal rim by the natural pressure of the baby's head is what prevents the need for episiotomy; the unfolding may appear slow, but is accomplished more swiftly with Birth Breathing than by any other means. The appearance of the baby's head should be announced quietly, because loud cheering frightens a baby who hears every sound; birthing is not an athletic event. Once the top of the head is visible, only one or two surges are usually needed to gently birth the head, and the perineum stretches with elastic-like flexibility if the mother stays relaxed. Some mothers who have thoroughly mastered the deepening techniques find their body propels the baby through the birth path on its own, without conscious Birth Breathing.

## 15. The First Hour and Beyond

Immediately after emergence the priority is that the parents receive the baby. If everything is fine, suctioning and other routines wait until the baby has been completely birthed. The vernix caseosa, the cheesy covering on the newborn's skin, is treated as a gift from nature that absorbs naturally. Skin-to-skin contact takes priority over cord cutting: the baby is placed on the mother's bare chest, and Kangaroo Mother Care studies from Australia are cited for the finding that the mother's body heat adjusts to the newborn's needs. Cord clamping is delayed until pulsation stops, because cutting while the cord is still pulsating abruptly severs the baby's blood and oxygen supply. The placenta is allowed to release naturally, usually within five to thirty minutes, and the cord is never pulled to extract it.

Breastfeeding begins immediately. Dr. Lennart Righard's 1990 Danish study is cited for the finding that newborns whose mothers were not medicated during labour could crawl to the breast and self-attach, while medicated babies could not manage this even with assistance. Suckling releases oxytocin, which helps detach the placenta and shrinks the uterus. Colostrum is a natural laxative that clears meconium and delivers antibodies; the WHO ranks infant nutrition sources as mother's own milk directly from the breast first, then expressed milk, donor milk, and finally formula. Discomfort while breastfeeding signals a problem to solve, and lactation consultants and groups such as La Leche League should be identified before the birth.

The **afterglow** is the immediate post-birth period and the **babymoon** extends it into the early weeks at home. The birthing room should have the reverence of a place of worship, voices subdued and soft lighting restored. Only the mother and her partner should hold the baby initially, because exposure to the scent and energy of others is overwhelming for a newborn still adjusting to life outside the womb. Most hospitals offer at least one hour of uninterrupted family time, but the book considers this inadequate — in some cultures parents and baby seclude themselves for two or three weeks — so parents are encouraged to treat the early period as a honeymoon for the new family and to limit visitors to those bringing a meal, doing laundry, shopping, or cleaning.

## 16. Birth Preferences as a Partnership

The programme's closing artefact is the birth preference sheet, supplied by the practitioner as a template. It is completed before touring the facility, attached to the prenatal record, and copied to hospital admissions, the midwife, and clinic staff. The preferences acknowledge explicitly that they may change as labour unfolds and that full cooperation is given if a genuine special circumstance arises, after explanation and discussion. The framing is partnership: the cover letter thanks providers for being chosen and expresses trust that they will help attain a joyous and satisfying natural birth. Preferences are organised by phase — onset of labour, admission, thinning and opening, descent, birth, and for baby — with specific items such as intermittent monitoring, discussing "comfort level" rather than "pain level," dim lights and soft music, freedom to walk and choose positions, few or no vaginal exams, breathing the baby down with prompts only from the birthing companion, delayed cord cutting, the baby crawling to the breast and self-attaching, and the baby remaining with parents at all times.

## 17. Key Takeaways

- Pain in normal birth is largely the product of fear acting on the autonomic nervous system; relaxation releases endorphins, which cannot co-exist with catecholamine and which let the uterus work as designed.
- The Mongan Method's vocabulary is itself a tool: surges instead of contractions, birthing instead of labouring, pressure instead of pain, birth companion instead of coach, special circumstances instead of complications.
- Daily conditioning is non-negotiable. The six techniques (education, relaxation, breathing, visualisation, deepening, affirmations) only work when practised, with Rainbow Relaxation daily and facial relaxation as an instant trigger.
- Three breaths, one per phase: Calm Breathing for relaxation and between surges, Surge Breathing with every surge during thinning and opening, Birth Breathing to assist the Natural Expulsive Reflex during descent. Forced pushing is rejected.
- The body knows how to birth. The mother's role is to "do nothing" — stay loose and limp, ignore dilation numbers, trust the continuum of labour, and welcome slow or resting labour as normal.
- Provider, setting, and doula are chosen early and matched against one of four management models (Active Management, Standard, Instinctive Birthing, Parental Self-Management) so that philosophy and facility align with the family's intent.
- Post-birth, skin-to-skin contact, delayed cord clamping, unmedicated self-attachment to the breast, a quiet afterglow, and a protected babymoon are continuations of the birth, not afterthoughts.
- Birth preferences are a partnership document: specific in their requests, explicit about flexibility when genuine medical need arises, and shared with every provider who may attend.
- The programme does not promise a birth free of pain or unanticipated incidents; it offers women the opportunity to step into birthing without fear or stress and, in most cases, to experience it calmly and joyfully.

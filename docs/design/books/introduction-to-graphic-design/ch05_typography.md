## 1. What Typography Is

- **Typography** — the visual expression of the written word. Letters animate to form alphabets, then words, sentences, paragraphs, and stories. Works whenever it conveys a message, regardless of whether type is written with a magic marker, dots and dashes, or elegant letterforms.
- **Form vs. meaning** — designers revel in the interplay between the pure form (shape) of type and the meaning it conveys. Quotation marks, commas, single words, captions, and lists are all raw material for layout.
- **Typesetting** — the visual arrangement of text. Originally referred to physically setting metal type; today it is primarily digital and refers to working with large amounts of text.
- **Typographer** — someone who designs typefaces, either commissioned by a client or initiating new typeface designs independently.
- **Type foundries** — own the rights to typeface designs and sell digital versions to the public; cost varies based on designer and whether an extended family is included.

## 2. Anatomy of Letterforms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **x-height** | Height of lowercase letters, measured with a lowercase x |
| **baseline** | Line on which the base of each capital letter rests |
| **cap height** | Height from the baseline to the top of capital letters |
| **meanline** | Line establishing the height of the body of lowercase letters |
| **ascenders** | Lowercase strokes that rise above the meanline |
| **descenders** | Lowercase strokes that fall below the baseline |
| **stress** | Axis on which a letter is drawn; usually measured on the lowercase o |
| **terminal** | End of a stroke that lacks a serif |
| **serif** | Small element added to the end of a stroke |
| **sans serif** | Typefaces without serifs |

## 3. Type Classification and Characteristics

- **Type classification** — system for dividing typefaces into categories and subcategories based on appearance, including weight, stroke, and the way each part of a letter ends. Major classifications include humanist and old style (15th–16th c., calligraphic), transitional (sharper serifs, more vertical axis), serif (18th–19th c., vertical axis with high stroke contrast), and sans serif (early 20th c. modernism).
- **Serif vs. sans serif** — serifs are small elements added to terminals; sans serif typefaces lack them. Both are widely used today with numerous subcategories.
- **Shape and form diagnostics** — ask whether letters are straight or rounded, upright or skewed, geometric or handwritten; whether the lowercase o is round or oval; whether the uppercase R is wide or narrow with bowls and stems that flare or stay tight; whether serifs are tapered, flat, or decorative.
- **Decoration** — produces expressive typefaces used for headings and display. Look for shadows, shading, stencil effects, reversed-out or outlined letters. Avoid for body copy because complexity interferes with readability.
- **Terminals** — shapes at the ends of letters. Serif typefaces have more complex terminals than sans serif; can be vertical, oblique, hard-edged, flared, pointed, rounded (teardrop), or reference calligraphy.

## 4. Measuring and Sizing Type

- **Point system** — letterforms are measured by width using points (pt). 12 points = 1 pica. Picas are commonly used in book/newspaper/multipage layout because they avoid fractions.
- **Weight** — thickness or thinness of a typeface, determined by ratio of stroke thickness to character height. Lighter weights for text type; bolder weights (bold, black) for headings and display.

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Light / lightweight** | Less weight than roman or medium |
| **Roman / Book weight** | Mid-weight; designed for setting large amounts of text |
| **Black** | Heavier than bold |
| **Italics** | Slanted version with letters specifically redesigned for the slant — used for emphasis |
| **Obliques** | Simply slanted version of the typeface |
| **Condensed** | Reduced character width (also extra condensed, compressed) |
| **Extended** | Wider characters than the medium or roman version |

- **Type family** — collection of different weights and iterations of a typeface. Univers has nearly two dozen styles; the family is the unit a designer pairs across light/heavy versions.
- **Optimal scale for body text** — most readable body text is 9 to 11 point; exact size depends on the typeface. 12-point default for term papers is usually too big for design pieces. Sans serif faces tend to appear larger than serif at the same size.
- **Display type vs. text type** — display (also called display text) is larger type used for headings/titles; should be visibly bigger than body text — the eye has trouble noticing differences smaller than two points. Text type can be used as display if letterforms are clearly visible enlarged.
- **Digital type scale is variable** — depends on user's screen and viewing distance; users may also resize. Establishing initial visual hierarchy is at least as important on screen as in print.

## 5. Typographic Hierarchy

- **Typographic hierarchy** — the arrangement of elements in a graduated series, from most prominent to least prominent. Foundation for most other design decisions.
- **Fewer variables, more power** — make clear distinctions using the fewest variables; too much styling obscures hierarchy.
- **Read text aloud as a test** — use inflection and volume to identify which words deserve emphasis; map speech patterns to visual styling.
- **Variables for revealing hierarchy**:

| Variable | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Ordering** | Decide what is first, second, third before applying any visual styling |
| **Space** | Adding space above/below a line gives it prominence |
| **Weight** | Changing words/lines to bold or black gives greater prominence |
| **Placement / thresholds** | Indenting or shifting text to one side groups information and creates emphasis |
| **Scale** | Enlarging a single word or line further emphasizes content |
| **Color** | Adding color to a word or line further differentiates it |

## 6. Alignment Systems

- **Four basic alignments** — flush left, flush right, centered, justified. Choice depends on content type and format.

| System | Behavior | Best Use |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **Flush left** | Vertically aligned to left edge; right edge is the **rag** (a feathered rag is preferable to a tight, even rag) | Most readable; default for left-to-right languages |
| **Flush right** | Vertically aligned on right edge; ragged left | Small chunks like captions, sidebars, margin notes; not ideal for large blocks |
| **Centered** | Ragged on both sides, symmetrical around center | Invitations, title/masthead pages, packaging, pull quotes; difficult for long paragraphs (no consistent threshold) |
| **Justified** | Smooth straight edge on both sides | Standard for books, magazines, newspapers; risks **rivers** of white space, requires manual tracking and force-hyphenation |

## 7. Spacing: Kerning, Tracking, Leading

- **Kerning** — space between two characters. Type designers set default kerning, but it doesn't work in every situation. Optical correctness trumps numerical correctness; letter combinations with y, o, e, a, w are particularly problematic. Display sizes nearly always need additional hand kerning. *Practice tip:* rotate text 180 degrees so letters appear as shapes.
- **Tracking** — space between groups of letters, lines, or paragraphs. Used to fix words that don't fit, eliminate widows/orphans, or fix typefaces that feel too crowded or open. Generally correct when imperceptible; +2 or −2 is undetectable in body text.
- **Leading** — space between lines of type, measured in points. Term dates from metal-type spacers. Default in most software is two points larger than body text (e.g., 10 on 12). Fonts with high ascenders/low descenders need more leading. For small body text (8 or 8.5 pt), add 1–2 extra points to increase readability. Notation: "8 on 12" means 8-point text with 12 points of leading.

## 8. Line Length and Readability

- **Line length** — number of words or characters on a line. Readers scan groups of three or four words rather than every word.
- **Optimal length** — 9 to 12 words per line is ideal; small text up to 12–13, large text 9–10. By character count, 45 to 75 characters (including spaces) is optimal. Justified text benefits from longer lines (12–15 words) to reduce hyphenation and rivers.
- **Failure modes** — long lines cause fatigue, slower reading, lost place; very short lines force excessive hyphenation and produce a choppy rag.

## 9. Typographic Niceties

- **Typographic niceties** — small styling decisions that make text clearer and easier to read. Designers maintain a list of "typographic sins" — unsightly orphans/widows, unhung quotes, rivers, un-kerned quotation marks at paragraph ends.
- **Hyphenation** — marked syllabic division of words when they don't fit on a line. Acceptable when a syllable has 3+ letters; avoid hyphenating words with 2-letter or "ly"-ending syllables; never have more than two consecutive hyphens; never hyphenate the first two letters of a word.
- **Hung punctuation** — quotation marks and bullets placed *outside* the text block (rather than within) maintain clean, smooth alignment and look more elegant.
- **Orphan** — a single word by itself on a line. Avoid unless the word covers at least one-third of the column width; fix using kerning, tracking, or hyphenation to coax it up or pull a word down.
- **Widow** — a single line at the beginning or end of a column. Avoid by breaking the paragraph differently to keep the widow with company.
- **Ligatures** — unique typographic characters produced when two or more letters are joined. Only serif typefaces include them; created for problematic combinations like double f or f+i. Reduce visual tension and increase readability.
- **Dashes** — punctuate breaks/pauses and connections.
  - **Em dash** (—) — as wide as the letter M; indicates a break in thought (replaces colon/semicolon). Created with shift+option+hyphen.
  - **En dash** (–) — as wide as a capital N; indicates a connection between things or names (replaces hyphen). Created with option+hyphen.

## 10. Orientation and Type with Color

- **Orientation: rotate, don't stack** — when vertical type is needed (signage, book spines), rotate horizontal text 90 degrees clockwise. Stacking individual letters produces no consistent alignment, awkward spacing, and "ransom note" results.
- **Angled type** should still start on a horizontal baseline before rotation; this is considered flush-left even after angling.
- **Type and color** — text color depends on typeface mass, scale, and hierarchy. Letterforms with less mass need more contrast. Light colors (pale yellow, pale green) won't read on white; switch background to brown/blue. Blocky typefaces (Rockwell, Univers black) work with very light or saturated hues; delicate faces (Bodoni, Frutiger 45 light) do not.
- **White-on-black bump** — white body text on black often appears smaller; increase point size by 0.5 to 1 point for visual consistency.

## 11. Choosing Typefaces

- **Free fonts caveat** — "you get what you pay for" still applies. Free fonts often need extra kerning/tracking to look consistent and tend toward expressive display rather than body use.
- **Body copy** — also called body text or running text; multiple lines of text set in paragraph format. Main vehicle for content; needs the most readable face.
- **Designer-staple typefaces** the chapter highlights:

| Typeface | Type | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Baskerville** | Serif | John Baskerville, 1724; thick/thin contrast |
| **Bodoni** | Serif | Giambattista Bodoni, 1798; un-bracketed serifs, expressive caps for headings |
| **Caslon** (Adobe) | Serif | Carol Twombly's revival of William Caslon (1722); suited to 6–14 pt text |
| **Garamond** | Serif | Claude Garamond, used as early as 1530; among the most legible for large text |
| **Georgia** | Serif | Matthew Carter, 1993, for Microsoft; designed for low-resolution screens |
| **Futura** | Geometric sans | Paul Renner, 1927, Bauer; capitals trace to ancient Greek lettering |
| **Frutiger** | Sans | Adrian Frutiger, 1970; originally Roissy for Charles de Gaulle Airport signage |
| **Gill Sans** | Sans | Eric Gill, 1928, Monotype; bundled with Microsoft Office and iOS |
| **Helvetica** | Sans | Eduard Hoffmann/Max Miedinger, 1957 (originally Neue Haas Grotesk) |
| **Optima** | Sans | Hermann Zapf, 1950s; Roman-inspired with flared terminals, no true serifs |
| **Univers** | Sans | Adrian Frutiger, 1954; based on 1898 Akzidenz Grotesk; 44 weights/styles |
| **Verdana** | Sans | David Berlow & David Jonathan Ross with Matthew Carter, 1996; built for onscreen display |

- **Display type** — designed to work well at large sizes; classifications label faces "display" or "text" but bold/black versions of common faces are often used as display. Pair a display face with a more readable text face; serif/sans serif combinations work well — choose two noticeably different fonts rather than two similar ones.
- **Hand-drawn type** — if you want type to look hand-drawn, draw it yourself; faking it produces predictable results.

## 12. Type as Form

- **Type as image** — letters/words altered so formal qualities override direct meaning replace imagery. Letters can be enlarged, split, cropped, or rotated until origin is unrecognizable. Avoid skewing or distorting the original shape; start with cleaner faces rather than already-decorative ones.
- **Type as texture** — words, lines, or paragraphs layered or distorted to make reading impossible. Used in backgrounds or shapes; serif textures (Baskerville) read very differently from sans (Univers, Helvetica). Textured type must deliberately obscure meaning so it doesn't compete with hierarchy; leave space between textural and regular text.
- **Rules** — designer term for lines, thick or thin. Used with type/image to separate small chunks of text or highlight a single line. Place rules above or below type, not between two lines (a rule between can crash through descenders); rules should clearly relate to the text they apply to.

## 1. The Design Process

- **Design process** — a repeatable series of actions used to develop design projects, beginning with the **brief** and ending with **production** and dissemination of the final product. Professional studios often customize it; the underlying steps stay consistent.
- **Five-stage cycle** the book uses:

| Stage | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| **Brief / Assignment** | Specifies what to accomplish, audience, time, budget, sometimes assets |
| **Research / Strategy** | Investigate client, audience, context, prior work; develop a communications strategy |
| **Visual Design Development** | Brainstorming, ideation, sketching, layout iterations |
| **Revision** | Refine after feedback |
| **Implementation / Production** | Final digital or physical output |

- **Components are modular** — the cycle can be applied as a whole or pulled apart and reused. Time per phase scales with project size and deadline.
- **Brief** — written document outlining the specific expectations of a design project. Usually written by the client; can be initiated by a design company. Student briefs mimic professional ones but rarely include a budget.
- **Strategy** — plan of action designed to achieve specific goals and objectives.
- **Production** — digital or physical realization; may involve printing or coding depending on the type of design.

## 2. Research and Strategy

- **Research is the only way to make sure you're solving the correct problem** — it tells you about the competition, prevents re-making what already exists, builds a complete picture of the audience, and reveals the environment where the end result will be seen.
- **Time allocation** — on larger long-term projects, research can take at least a third of the designer's time.
- **Failure mode example** — designing a poster for a visiting designer using your favorite typeface, only to discover the visiting designer is famous for typefaces made from found objects. The mismatch is avoidable with a quick search.
- **Practical starting point** — Google or another search engine for quick searches about content/subject; check what other designers have done with similar projects while being careful to avoid mimicking or copying their ideas.

## 3. Concepts: What They Are and Where They Come From

- **Concept** — the idea behind a design. Concepts emerge *after* research, based on the specifics of content and the goals of the assignment, often rooted in visual/verbal associations.
- **Concepts can be overt or subtle** — an ad pun is overt; a book cover whose layout mimics the verticality of buildings described inside is subtle. Audiences may appreciate the connection without identifying the concept.
- **When concept matters most** — advertisements, logos, and new-product packaging benefit from strong concepts. Instructional diagrams may be better served by simple text and drawings.
- **Inspiration** — visual or mental stimulation that leads to a new idea or solution. Nonlinear, unpredictable; comes to different people at different times. Even at rest, the mind continues to work on problems posed earlier in the day.
- **Ideation** — generating ideas and expressing them through diagrams or visual explorations.
- **Visual explorations** — different ways of using visual material to communicate a message; designers often produce many versions when developing a layout.
- **Cliché** — an idea, visual, or thought used so often it loses originality. Common in design but should be avoided. *Test:* ask whether the cliché is effective, what fresh approach would do, and what the viewer should feel/learn — answers to these questions form a roadmap for concept development.
- **Reasons not to start a design digitally** — concepts are unlikely to be original or experimental; details get attention too early; software tools influence ideas more than concept does; layouts feel stilted and predictable.

## 4. Brainstorming Techniques

- **Brainstorming** — structured activity using lists, diagrams, and sketches to develop creative solutions; can be both visual and verbal.
- **Free association** — write down facts about the project, then list words that come to mind unedited. Don't worry about whether ideas lead to a solution; resist editing. Spend 10–15 minutes; quantity over quality. Map words back to the assignment afterwards and turn the strongest ones into thumbnail sketches.
- **Quantity is a hallmark of good brainstorming** — most output won't end up in final layouts; that's expected. The more words, the better the chance a unique concept emerges.
- **Take a break before evaluating** — wait at least a day before judging sketches; if rushed, step away for a few hours. Concepts that survive evaluation are like ingredients — a single concept can be applied to different formats and deliverables.
- **Using connections** — five-to-ten-minute exercise sketching how each principle applies to the project's three goals. One minute per sketch; don't draw full layouts.

| Principle | What it does |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Connection** | Shows association or linkage; can be **literal** (two items or elements are linked) or **contradictory** (something does the opposite of what one expects). Distortion and exaggeration can also enhance visual connections |
| **Space / depth** | Activates the 2D plane; iterate by exaggerating, distorting, or highlighting space |
| **Surprise and play** | Taps the viewer's emotions; most effective combined with narrative; can occur in one area or as a substituted element |

- **Ideas with actions** — generate concepts by applying verbs to the content; sketch each in under a minute, ideally with pen rather than pencil. Verbs: *Magnify, Distort, Exaggerate, Repeat, Reduce, Simplify, Reverse, Rearrange.*
- **Ways of framing the design problem** — diagnostic questions: What will the viewer get from the design? Who is the target audience? Does it have a purpose? Should the tone be informational or persuasive? What would *you* want to see if you were the user?

## 5. Storytelling Devices

- **Visual narrative** comes from juxtaposing words and images that don't obviously go together — the viewer's tendency to make sense of mismatches creates meaning. Useful for storytelling, but can lead to content overload if associations grow too complex.
- **Literary devices** that translate to design:

| Device | Definition | Distinguishing feature |
|--------|------------|------------------------|
| **Metaphor** | One idea/visual stands for something else | Indirect; relies on two elements together to create a third, less literal meaning (e.g., lighted window + closed door = "life") |
| **Simile** | Pairing of two dissimilar things in overt comparison | Direct comparison ("the young woman is like the rose") |
| **Pun** | Word play using dual meanings or similar-sounding words | An image may stand in for part of the word pairing |
| **Personification / anthropomorphism** | Human nature applied to inanimate objects or animals | Common in advertising mascots (book examples: GEICO gecko, AFLAC duck) |

## 6. Matching Design to Audience

- **Audience mismatch is a common student failure mode** — the desire to make unique, "cool" work can override consumer preferences and the realities of a target age group; the project fails regardless of how cool it looks. Letting go of an idea that doesn't serve the brief is part of the process; keep it as a reference for future projects.
- **Define the audience narrowly** — "everybody" is too broad. Ask demographic questions (age, income, transport, brand loyalty) plus stylistic ones (will a particular embellishment appeal? does the audience have time to read or are visuals primary?).
- **Audience-driven calibrations**:

| Audience attribute | Visual implication |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Older adults | Larger type for readability |
| Children | Bright colors and characters |
| Teenagers | Lively-looking graphics |
| Time-poor / busy environment | Lead with imagery; reduce reading load |

- **It's easier to design for people you have something in common with** — practice designing for unfamiliar audiences early; ask classmates and instructors during critiques whether visuals match the intended audience.

## 7. Revision and Experimentation

- **Most first ideas are not the final solution** — exploring multiple ideas leads to better quality work and prevents repeating the same visual solution; even when an initial idea wins, refinement is necessary for placement, scale, typography.
- **Revision** — redoing or changing a design, usually after receiving feedback on an initial version.
- **Industry mirror** — design companies typically present at least three versions to a client; even after a concept is chosen, the designer continues to create variations of the layout before arriving at the final.
- **Making better work faster** — practiced revision speeds future work; advanced students should produce a fully realized layout in the same time it once took to develop initial ideas. School is a good place to practice the multi-project juggling required of professionals.

## 8. Presenting and Critiquing Work

- **Match presentation media to final media** — show digital work on screen (websites projected, motion graphics on a monitor); print work as printed or 3D mock-ups. Preliminary stages may show sketches; later stages require fully realized printed or 3D versions.
- **Craft** — neatness; how well a 2D or 3D item is displayed or constructed (including on screen). Attention to detail markedly improves how design work is received in critique.
- **Presenting work-in-progress checklist**:
  - **Sketches** — detach from notebook, trim uneven edges, go over pencil with pen/marker so drawings read from a distance.
  - **Printouts of in-progress work** — trim white edges with X-Acto unless white space is part of the design.
  - **Printouts of final work** — print on bright-white paper; mount on Foamcore where required; paste/mount before trimming.
  - **3D objects and multipage documents** — fully construct mock-ups; use the booklet feature in InDesign or print double-sided where signatures are needed; large-scale work can be digitally inserted into a real-space photo.
- **Critique** — written or verbal evaluation of a design project; usually includes recommendations for improvement. Exists to *help you improve your work* and mimics the professional environment.
- **Receiving critique** — negative feedback is hardest after long hours on a piece. Even great ideas may not suit specific content or audiences. A logo can succeed at large size but fail on a business card; a visual might be too complex to coexist with typography. *Unrealized versions of a project are never a waste of time* — keep castoffs for future assignments.
- **Tips for giving feedback**:
  1. Be specific. Limit positive comments to no more than three; cite imagery, color choice, type rather than "I like it."
  2. Critique the work, not the person.
  3. Anchor criticism in the project's stated goals and target audience; avoid praising interesting visuals if they fail the brief.
  4. Evaluate hierarchy — has the designer styled the most important information so it's seen first? If not, say so immediately. *Great-looking visuals cannot save a project that fails to communicate content.*
- **Evaluation and assessment** — even after a grade or sign-off, evaluate what could have been done better. A good concept goes a long way, but a project can still suffer from poor craft or production problems.
- **Student work groups** — meet at regular intervals with classmates working on similar projects; get used to giving constructive criticism; weight feedback that aligns with what the instructor says.

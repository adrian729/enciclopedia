## 1. Visual Arrangement and Organization

- **Visual arrangement and organization** — every designer must learn to organize text and images in space so the design's concept is expressed and the content makes sense to the viewer. The layout process is both rational and intuitive.
- **Hierarchy as a blueprint** — hierarchy provides the blueprint for initial decisions about placement, scale, and proximity; more specific stylistic decisions come from sketching and brainstorming.
- **Same elements, many solutions** — the same visual elements can produce a variety of layouts; experimentation and visual exploration are required to identify the successful arrangements.

## 2. Creating a Layout

- **Layout sequence** — review project specs, target audience, format, and viewing context first; decide what is more and less important (hierarchy); produce several handmade sketches showing emphasis; only then begin the digital layout.
- **Avoid equal weight** — never let two elements carry the same visual weight unless they are genuinely equally important.
- **Sketch before software** — sketches let ideas move quickly and yield better visual decisions than starting at a blank screen; nearly every professional designer produces preliminary sketches.

## 3. Emphasis

- **Emphasis** — deciding which elements are more and less important *before* layout begins; this is hierarchy applied to a composition. Iterate emphasis through scale, color, placement, and proximity, then assess which version works best.
- **Emphasis is relative** — how much one item stands out depends on what surrounds it and where it sits. A single large image with small supporting type carries most of the visual weight; the same image surrounded by large display type loses prominence.
- **Common arrangement problems to avoid**:

| Problem | Why it fails |
|---------|--------------|
| **Stepping** type or imagery | Content appears scattered |
| Elements too close to each other or to a compositional edge | Creates visual tension |
| Multiple alignment systems crammed without breathing room | Causes visual confusion between text aspects |
| Equal visual weight between image and text | Obscures hierarchy and makes the composition less interesting |

- **Multiple alignment systems are allowed** — when listing dates or supporting information, two or more alignment systems can coexist if each piece of text is distinct and there is enough white space between them.
- **Scale and placement** — by experimenting with scale and placement, the same content yields different layouts depending on whether imagery or text is dominant. Once the largest/most important element is placed, use visual association to connect larger elements to smaller ones.

## 4. Emphasizing With Color

- **Color as emphasis** — color and value are as important as scale and placement; color affects hierarchy and provides emphasis. Make sure color supports, rather than detracts from, the overall hierarchy.
- **Black-and-white test** — to test whether color is improving a layout, eliminate color completely and re-evaluate. Some designs are more effective in black and white.
- **Less color, more noticeable hues** — the less color present in a composition, the more noticeable the individual hues will be.
- **Warm-on-cool emphasis** — warm colors stand out against cooler tones; adding a warm color to the foreground provides emphasis and creates a sense of depth on a flat page.
- **Value can replace hue** — even when no color is present, value can change the hierarchy of a composition.
- **Color across sections** — color can also differentiate particular sections or pages in a multipage document, not just emphasize a single area.

## 5. Flow

- **Flow** — the direction the designer wants the viewer's eye to move around the design. Visual variables like placement, scale, color, and proximity entice the viewer to look at elements in a particular order.
- **Western reading model** — readers of Western alphabets read left to right and top to bottom; layout flow can be modeled on this principle.
- **Avoid breaking flow** — avoid layouts where elements direct the eye off the edge of the page, where supporting information has greater importance than the heading, or where layered images and text obscure meaning.
- **Consistent flow lines / thresholds across panels** — placing text chunks at the same height across panels (e.g., across the panels of an accordion-fold brochure) provides a system that makes content easy to follow from one panel to the next.

## 6. Margins

- **Margins** — guides indicating where text and other visuals should be placed; the space between the "live area" of a design and the edge of the composition. Without margins, nothing keeps text from sliding to the page edge. Images and textural elements may break the margin and extend to the edge.
- **Margin proportion affects perception** — too-tight margins look crowded; overly generous margins make content feel lost in the space.
- **Thumb rule** — in print, avoid placing content beyond where the thumbs would comfortably touch and overlap the page if you held the finished object.
- **Bottom margin convention** — wider bottom margins produce more balanced, harmonious-looking layouts; this is more common than equal top/bottom.
- **Tight vs. generous margins by deliverable**:

| Deliverable | Margin tendency |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Novels, magazines, text-heavy projects | Tighter margins — fit more content, fewer pages |
| Brochures, art books, textbooks | More generous margins |
| Apps (small screens) | Tighter margins |
| Online newspapers (text/image heavy) | Smaller / tighter margins |
| Online fashion magazines, personal websites | More generous margins |

- **Margins on screen** — websites typically have more generous left/right margins than top/bottom; size depends on the viewing device's proportions. Grids (also called frames or wireframes) keep content areas consistent across screens.

## 7. Gutters

- **Gutter** — the space between columns of text or between two pages of a book. The space between two pages is also called the **fold** or the **spine**, and disrupts the flow of content.
- **Gutter width matters** — narrow gutters between columns make text feel crowded; gutters too wide break the visual connection and the reader may stop continuing across columns.
- **Bindings that don't open flat** — when the fold obscures part of the page, avoid extending content beyond interior margins on each side; add extra space to interior margins so content isn't lost in the gutter.

## 8. Multipage Documents

- **Goal of multipage layout** — motivate the viewer to keep reading and turn the page (or scroll/swipe). Maintain clear hierarchy, create readable text blocks (scale, line length, columns), integrate imagery, and provide visual continuity.
- **Verso and recto** — left and right pages of a spread. In right-to-left languages (Japanese, Arabic, Hebrew), the verso is on the right and the recto on the left.
- **Spreads** — two pages sitting next to each other. Most print-based multipage documents are designed as spreads, not single pages. Set up facing pages in InDesign or multiple art boards in Illustrator from the start.
- **Tips for working in spreads**:
  1. Layouts should be designed as spreads rather than single pages.
  2. The goal is to present content clearly, keep readers' interest, and motivate them to turn the page.
  3. Layout should use a flow that encourages the eye to move left to right and top to bottom.
- **Multipage documents on-screen** — connected content uses scrolling, swiping, or click navigation (gestures). Maintain hierarchy and consistent styling across all pages or screens. Conventions for on-screen layout continue to evolve, so what is considered correct may change before a student finishes school.

## 9. Grids

- **Grids** — an underlying structure of horizontal and vertical thresholds (or rectangles) used to organize typographic and pictorial elements. They specify where content should appear across multiple pages and items in a series.
- **Where grids are used** — books, magazines, web pages, annual reports, brochures, timetables, posters, catalogues, advertising campaigns, application designs.
- **Grids as a tool, not absolutes** — grids are used to produce both highly structured and extremely experimental work; limits come from how the designer chooses to use the grid, not from the grid itself. The designer can break the grid with call-outs, imagery, or other elements.
- **Symmetrical vs. asymmetrical**:

| Type | Behavior | Best for |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Symmetrical** | Modules / alignment points mirror each other on each side of the spread or page | Content-heavy, multipage documents (books, magazines) where pages should share the same margins and feel continuous |
| **Asymmetrical** | A quadrant appears in different positions on each side | Single-page formats (posters, websites); web design where pages are seen one after another; cases where content varies considerably side to side or where a specific area is reserved for particular content (like images) |

## 10. Types of Grids

- **Wireframes** — used in screen-based designs; structural models showing functionality, navigation, and separate areas for content on websites and applications. Like other grids, they indicate hierarchy and where to place text and image elements.
- **Constructional grids** — use elements in a composition and their placement as the basis for axis lines. Unlike most grid systems, constructional grids are organic and created *during* layout, not before. Some designers don't consider them grids at all; the value is in using consistent thresholds and alignment points to balance compositions.
- **Column grids** — based on dividing a page into separate columns with gutters between. Accommodate body text, marginalia, headings, footers, and images. Column widths can be consistent or one column may be wider/narrower for captions, notes, or navigation. Adding horizontal axis or flow lines extends the grid's function.
- **Modular grids** — use horizontal and vertical alignment markers to divide space into equally sized quadrants or cells; gutters separate cells. Cells can hold individual chunks or be combined for larger zones.
  - **Module size matters** — too-small modules give too many alignment options and obscure the grid's purpose; too-large modules don't provide enough placement areas, slowing work and producing layouts lacking continuity.
- **Combining grid systems** — the same project may use a two-column grid on some pages and a three-column grid on others; experimentation determines which yields the best results.
- **Considerations before creating a grid**:
  - Where will the content be seen (website, poster, book)?
  - Are multiple columns needed? If so, consider a module or column grid.
  - Looser or tighter system? For long documents on a tight schedule, a more structured grid is faster.
  - Should the grid be based on an aspect of the content (e.g., a book about trees deriving its grid proportion from a leaf)?
  - Are the standard systems appropriate, or is a non-standard grid needed?
- **Software workflow** — set page size, margin size, and column width in the document setup window. In InDesign, grids are typically created on the master page(s) and applied to other pages. In Photoshop or Illustrator, create a separate layer for the grid or work with guides toggled on and off.

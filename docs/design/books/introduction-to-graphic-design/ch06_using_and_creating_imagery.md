## 1. Why Imagery Matters

- **People "see" before they read** — viewers grasp visual content faster than text, making imagery a crucial communication tool. Imagery exists as form in space and must fit appropriate hierarchy with other elements.
- **Image function varies by context** — focal point, visual narrative, complement to text, background, decoration. Choice depends on context, audience expectations, and stylistic goals.
- **Production paths** — images can be made by hand (drawing, painting, collage), with a camera, or directly in software with a mouse or stylus.
- **Narrative** — the story. In design, narrative can be both textual and visual; imagery and visuals can complement text or work alone to create a visual narrative.

## 2. Representation and Abstraction

- **Representational images** — clearly recognizable; refer to life. A representational dog looks similar to a real dog. Mainstay of design and ubiquitous in advertising's persuasive direct associations (e.g., shampoo ad with high-resolution model). Best when direct meaning is required.
- **Abstraction or abstract images** — exist with some independence from the natural world and strict representation. Useful when type content dominates, when no obvious subject exists, or when a non-specific visual texture is needed.
- **Continuum, not binary** — meaning exists on a continuum between pictorial and abstract. As visuals become less pictorial, meaning becomes less literal and admits wider interpretation.
- **Choosing abstract** — try when no obvious visuals exist or when subtle communication is the goal. Designers may distort photos or use found materials (old magazines, fabric) and repurpose them.

## 3. Reductive Image Forms

- **Symbols** — graphic representations of ideas/concepts that exist without pictorially describing what they relate to. Nike's swoosh, Target's bull's-eye, McDonald's arches don't describe goods/services but become brand-associated. The same form can mean different things across cultures (swastika as Nazi fascism vs. Hindu/Buddhist well-being).
- **Icons** — visual forms representing a person, object, or idea, reduced to simplest form with no distracting detail. Used for logos, signage, app/web navigation.
- **Pictograms / pictographs** — representations of a letter, word, phrase, or physical concept; rely on viewer association. Restroom symbols are a classic case; pictograms transcend cultural and language barriers and are useful where literacy is uneven. Early writing developed from pictograms; Mandarin still preserves traces.

## 4. Semiotics

- **Semiotics** — the study of signs and symbols, and how meaning is created and communicated. Important to graphic design because designers always communicate to an audience and must anticipate associations.
- **Three stages of interpretation**:

| Stage | What happens |
|-------|--------------|
| **Perception** | The viewer sees or becomes aware of a sign, symbol, or visual |
| **Manipulation** | The viewer interprets the sign and considers how to respond |
| **Consummation** | The viewer responds to the visual |

- **Core assumption** — every sign or symbol has meaning. More than one meaning may attach to the same visual; interpretations are influenced by culture and prior experience.

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Denotation** | Direct or literal meaning, without the influence of personal feelings |
| **Connotation** | Associated or interpretive meaning, based on experience and ideas the viewer already possesses |

## 5. Modes and Style

- **Mode** — the media or technique used to produce an image. The same subject rendered as photograph, line drawing, or ornate historical illustration produces different reactions and interpretations.
- **Style** — appearance or distinctive visual character; an image may be drawn abstractly or representationally.
- **Choosing literal vs. interpretive** — for literal interpretation, use representational/pictorial imagery (photo, realistic illustration). For interpretive meaning, use obscured/abstracted visuals or those that contrast with text.
- **Combining modes** — pairing more than one mode (e.g., photograph + abstract texture) extends meaning and changes visual language. The grouping must have clear purpose.

## 6. Sourcing Imagery: Stock vs. Self-Initiated

- **Stock images** — sold by companies (usually online) for use in creative projects. Sites charge per-image fees or subscriptions; sell photographic, digital, illustration, vector, and video. Cater to every budget — students can afford low-cost or free imagery. Confirm with instructors whether stock is permitted vs. self-generated requirement.
- **Stock has improved** — historically perceived as staged or overly dramatic; today amateur and professional uploaders provide a wide range.
- **Self-initiated imagery** — visual material created directly by a designer or creative team (photos, collages, paintings, drawings, prints). Tailored to the project; particularly suited to student work because it costs nothing and fits exact needs.
- **Considerations when choosing image style** — what type of visual material best communicates the message (illustration vs. photography); intended audience preferences; production constraints (online vs. print, billboard resolution); single image vs. multiple; budget; copyright; whether imagery should dominate or play a subordinate role.

## 7. Creating Images by Hand

- **Analog** — art or design processes that originate with the human hand (painting on canvas, sculpting clay).
- **Drawing and illustration** — countless options. Realistic renderings complement text; abstract or cartoon styles can also work. Same subject rendered different ways shifts meaning. Develop multiple versions and test in composition.
- **Painting** — not commonly associated with design but effective for textures or backgrounds; can reference a specific historical moment (e.g., Van Gogh swirls). Mask off areas behind text so brush strokes don't reduce readability.
- **Printmaking** — woodcuts and linoleum cuts are simple; silkscreen, lithography, and etching require specialized equipment. Plexiglas monoprints, potato prints, and woodcuts use inexpensive materials. Useful when you want to retain evidence of the originator's hand; suits posters and book arts.
- **Collage** — combination of two or more visual elements (printed matter, photographs, found items) put together on a background. Same composition rules apply as other visuals.
- **Photomontages** — use two or more photographic images together to create a composite; can be produced by hand or in Photoshop.
- **Montages** — use separate elements to create a new and different image from the parts; similar to collage, but the requirement of an independent resulting image distinguishes it.

## 8. Producing Images Digitally

- **Digital** — art and design that uses technology in production or distribution. A digital drawing can be created with a stylus; a digital print may have been made by hand and reproduced via computer-based technique.
- **Input methods** — mouse/trackpad on a laptop, or a stylus on a tablet/phone with varied tips for line quality. Time and patience produce nearly the same results with a mouse.
- **Tracing workflow** — sketch by hand, scan, import to Illustrator, trace with the pen tool. Practice the pen tool because curves are counterintuitive (see Bezier curves). Paintbrush/pencil tools are easier but rarely match pen-tool line quality and can look "computer-generated."
- **Live Trace** (Illustrator) — converts a black-and-white scanned raster image to vector. Without skillful settings, results resemble Photoshop filters; many instructors prefer hand-input lines.
- **Manipulation** — change or alteration of a visual from its original form (color shifts, distorted line quality).
- **DPI (dots per inch)** — measurement of pixel density in computer graphics.
- **Pixilation** — digitized imagery enlarged or distorted until individual pixels show. Generally avoided.

## 9. Image Formats

| Type | What it is |
|------|------------|
| **Raster** | Dot-matrix system of pixels; scaling up reduces clarity. Used for photos and fine-art illustration. Edited in Photoshop |
| **Vector** | Mathematical expressions of geometric shapes (lines, points, polygons); infinitely scalable. Used for logos, type, items needing crispness |

- **Common file formats**:
  - **AI** — Adobe Illustrator Artwork; vector content in Illustrator.
  - **EPS** — Encapsulated PostScript; vector save format before printing.
  - **GIF** — compressed raster/bitmap; small files often used for animation; lower image quality.
  - **INDD** — InDesign Document; multipage layout. Printers rarely accept live INDD; export to PDF.
  - **JPG** — compression format; appropriate for screen, not print production.
  - **PDF** — Portable Document Format; the right file to send a project to a commercial printer. Save an editable original alongside.
  - **PNG** — raster with lossless compression; common in screen-based design.
  - **PSD** — Photoshop Document; multi-layered, effect-based; not for print unless transparent background needed.
  - **TIFF** — Tagged Image File Format; raster bitmap; used in photo manipulation and print. Can be saved repeatedly without quality loss but is resolution-dependent.

## 10. Photography in Design

- **Photographs as carriers of truth** — photos uniquely carry information ("a picture is worth a thousand words"). They have ethical dilemmas: even rudimentary darkrooms allow alteration, and digital retouching is now routine. Designers should be cautious not to unintentionally change meaning or play into stereotypes/clichés.
- **Custom photography** — almost unrivaled at quickly communicating ideas and tone. Professional designers hire photographers; you can shoot yourself with a digital camera, smartphone, or disposable. Even great photographers take dozens of shots before identifying the best.
- **Custom-photography workflow** — shoot from multiple angles for live scenes; alter light source, backdrop, props for staged shoots; always shoot at high resolution (you can reduce but not increase); crop and adjust contrast/color before final selection.
- **Working with three-dimensional objects** — building or finding 3D props (toys, cardboard, clay sculptures) and photographing them is a low-cost way to create custom visuals. Use a three-point light system (one overhead, two at 45 degrees), shoot at highest resolution, then edit in Photoshop.
- **2D scanning input** — scan flat artwork at 300 dpi or higher (72 dpi for screen-only output). Alternatively photograph outdoors in natural light or with a copy stand.

## 11. Working with Images: Cropping, Layering, Manipulation

- **Cropping** — alter visual material to fit composition and balance with type, shape, color. Used to create more dynamic compositions, focus on part of an image, or repeat parts. Can combine multiple visuals or distort scale.
- **Cut-outs** — images cropped using a shape outline or compositional line; shapes and type can act as masks. Effective for incorporating low-quality or off-style visuals.
- **Cropping tips** — keep an original; tighter cropping is usually more dynamic; don't think only in rectangles (textures, patterns, photos can crop to shapes or letterforms); avoid awkward cuts like the middle of a face unless intentional; consider nontraditional crops where images are cut and rearranged or paired with dissimilar imagery.
- **Layering and transparency** — software lets one element sit above another and move independently or linked. Transparency reduces upper-layer opacity so layers below show through.
- **Blending modes** — Photoshop/Illustrator combine layers using preset effects. Watch for muddy or ambiguous results; layered effects often look better on screen than print.
- **Digital manipulation tips**:
  1. Start with high-resolution, good-quality images.
  2. Decide alterations before opening the file.
  3. Enhance an existing image rather than apply a "cool looking" effect.
  4. Avoid filters that imitate art-making — start from a real painting or drawing instead.
  5. Avoid Photoshop-tutorial illusions; tutorials teach software but not always good design.
  6. Ask whether the manipulation is obvious; if so, reduce filters/blending modes.
  7. Evaluate whether the result fits other elements, audience, and message.

## 12. Copyright and Legal Issues

- **Copyright** — legal right giving the originator exclusive rights to use or distribute work in a region for a set time. Varies by country.
- **Permissions or photo release** — legal release/waiver signed by a model (photography) or by the originator/owner of artwork granting permission to publish or reproduce. Required for client work and commercial use.
- **Duration** — creator usually retains copyright for 70 years after death; in some cases trusts, collectors, or museums hold rights indefinitely. Reproduction fees typically scale with prints and context.
- **Who owns images**:

| Source | Owner |
|--------|-------|
| **Fine art photography** | The photographer holds the copyright |
| **Commercial photography (work for hire)** | The client owns the image |
| **Fine art / personal illustration or artwork** | The creator holds copyright |
| **Commissioned illustration** | The company owns copyright unless otherwise stipulated |
| **Design / photography / artwork created as part of one's job** | The company owns copyright |
| **Student work produced while studying** | The creator owns copyright; some schools stipulate reproduction rights for publicity |

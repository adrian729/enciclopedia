## 1. Form and Space: Core Vocabulary

- **Form** — any element in a design (images, type, dots, lines). Synonymous with *figure* and *element*; the front/foreground of a composition is made up of forms.
- **Formal elements** — the basic building blocks placed in physical or virtual space: **points, lines, patterns, shapes**. Combined infinitely, paired with type/imagery, and modified by visual variables to set mood, denote emphasis, and organize space.
- **Space** — the area or context in which visual elements are placed. As each form is added, a language of visual association builds; placement decisions affect emphasis and variety.
- **Visual understanding** — viewers inherently look for meaning in visual material; even a circle and a line on a page trigger associations. This tendency is why graphic design works as communication.
- **Concept, content, context** — the three principles that govern form/space decisions:

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Concept** | The expression of an idea — connecting meaning to formal elements via cognition, experimentation, interpretation |
| **Content** | The underlying message or text the form communicates (informational or motivational) |
| **Context** | The external/virtual environment where the design will be seen |

- **Structure** — the underlying organization (overt or subtle) that determines how form exists in space. Grids are one example; pairing of value, photo style, or any unifying principle also provides structure.

## 2. The Compositional Container

- **Live area, composition, format** — three terms for the space in which design occurs:

| Term | Primary use |
|------|-------------|
| **Live area** | Screen-based design (websites, apps, motion graphics) |
| **Composition / compositional space** | Print design, also general use |
| **Format** | Proportions, size, or orientation of a composition or live area |

- **Ground (background)** — like negative space; an area without elements or form.
- **Bleed** — the area outside the trim of a page or live space; "to bleed" an element means it extends beyond the edge. Originally print terminology, now used in screen design too.
- **Cropping** — removing parts of an image or element to improve framing of a single element or the whole composition.
- **Space and volume** — pages, websites, and apps are 2D, but shading, shadow, texture, cropping, tonal gradation, and linear perspective can simulate depth. Larger items appear closer; smaller items recede; darker elements appear behind lighter ones.
- **Sizing the container matters** — large space swallows small elements; large forms overpower small space. Too much space feels empty; too little feels overcrowded. Multipage documents use repeated lines/circles/squares for continuity; packaging uses shapes/lines as framing devices.

## 3. Basic Formal Elements

- **Point** — an exact position on a plane or in space; massless in pure form, becomes a **dot** when marked. Adding a second/third dot begins a story; order, placement, or color alters hierarchy.
- **Line** — the space between two points; length but no breadth. Connectors, so they convey movement, separate space, provide emphasis, and frame other elements. Implied lines form when elements are placed in a row, or by a string of words.
  - Thick lines have more visual weight; very thick lines may read as shapes or planes.
  - Horizontal lines are restful; angular lines create tension; squiggly lines suggest movement.
  - Repeated/rotated lines create textures and surface patterns.
- **Plane** — a flat, two-dimensional space; regular or irregular. Dots and lines become planes when enlarged relative to the surrounding space. When sides are indicated, a plane becomes a **shape**.
- **Shape** — a flat object defined by lines within 2D space. Lines provide the boundary (visible or invisible, smooth or rough).

| Shape attribute | Distinction |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Geometric** | Hard, regular edges — usually man-made objects |
| **Organic** | Irregular edges — usually natural world |
| **Active vs. passive** | Vertical rectangles are more active; horizontal rectangles are more passive |

- **Bezier curves** — mathematical-equation curves in vector software (Illustrator, Photoshop) drawn with the pen tool; infinitely scalable. Used for letterforms, type on a path, complex shapes, and illustrations; also map time-based motion in animation.
- **Digital form-making** — software re-creates hand gestures via line, pencil, or pen tool; gestural quality of a digital mark depends on which tool is used.

## 4. Mode, Texture, and Pattern

- **Mode** — the visual style used to render form: expressive techniques (painting, drawing, photography) or simple lines/shapes. Visual style affects both explicit and associated meaning. Common modes: **line art, drawing, photo**.
- **Customized vs. ready-made forms** — handmade/one-time-use forms have specificity; ready-mades (fonts, clip art, stock images, illustrations) suit less precise visual associations.
- **Texture** — appearance or feel of a surface; hard/smooth, handmade/digital. Adds tactile quality to 2D space, suggests handmade feel, or implies physical depth in virtual environments.
- **Print texture considerations** — paper stock, ink absorption, finishes (aqueous coating, embossing) all change the result. Glossy paper for magazines (*Time*, *Newsweek*, *Vogue*) and packaging; matte paper for art books, textbooks, invitations. Texture should not interfere with readability.
- **Pattern** — repeated/rotated forms create alternative surface patterns that organize or activate space.

## 5. Figure-Ground and Negative Space

- **Figure-ground** — the relationship between form (positive space) and the background (negative space, ground, white space). Form and ground are equally important and interdependent.
- **Negative space** — the space *around* visual elements; effective use is one of the best ways to create emphasis without overcrowding.
- **Variation between figure and ground creates dynamics** — equal visual weight produces static, one-dimensional compositions. Too much form mimics a crowded room (no individual stands out); too little form leaves elements lost.
- **Figure-ground reversal** — deliberately swapping emphasis between form and ground; can produce optical illusions where the eye shifts between two readings.

## 6. Hierarchy, Balance, and Symmetry

- **Hierarchy** — visual logic used to achieve clarity of message; describes how elements relate and how prominent each is. Determined by visual weight and placement; meaning shifts with ordering and use of scale, proximity, color, value. Lacking hierarchy causes confusion or uneasiness.
- **Balance** — based on gravity (think of a seesaw); visually heavy objects pull attention. Designers usually pursue **visual** balance rather than absolute balance, since equilibrium has many valid solutions. Variables: placement, proximity, weight, scale, color, value, texture.
- **Symmetry vs. asymmetry**:

| System | Character | Historical association |
|--------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Symmetrical** | Pure equilibrium; right/left (sometimes top/bottom) mirror across a central axis. Ordered, balanced, somewhat static | Standard pre-20th-century method; title pages, poetry, wedding invitations |
| **Asymmetrical** | Off-center placement without a mirroring counterpart; designer develops overall visual balance with focal points | Emerged from the modern movement at the **Bauhaus** in 1920s–30s Germany |

- **Asymmetry communicates complex messages more easily** than symmetry, which is harder to use successfully (best kept simple, with few elements).

## 7. Scale and Proportion

- **Scale** — size of an object, or graduated ordering of separate elements; relative — perceived size depends on surrounding objects and the live area. Used as a verb ("to scale up/down"). When applied to relational sizing of elements, it overlaps with hierarchy.
- **Static** — lacking movement or action; in design, generally undesirable. Identical or near-identical scaling across all elements produces dull, static compositions; differentiate sizes clearly.
- **Problems with digital scaling** — distorting proportions when resizing produces unreadable type and unclear images. Hold shift / use **constrained proportions** to maintain ratio. Resolution matters: too low pixelates; over 72 dpi for screen wastes load time.

## 8. Activating Space: Movement, Rhythm, Proximity

- **Active / activating** — literal or implied movement; activating part of a design means considering all areas of the page and balancing space so elements maintain hierarchy. Active design is generally preferred over static.
- **Movement** — changing object location in physical/virtual space, or guiding the eye around a page. Literal (running horses) or conceptual (graduated abstract shapes). Methods in 2D: **sequencing, layering, bleeding, overlapping, clustering, aligning**.
- **Rhythm** — uniform pattern or succession of strong and weak elements; visual rather than auditory. Achieved by progression, sequencing, repetition, or subtle variation in scale/color/tone/texture.
- **Syncopation** — interruption of a regular flow, used to create accents and emphasis (auditory analogy: pauses and volume changes in speech).
- **Rhythm is essential in motion graphics and multipage documents** — pacing must be designed; in motion graphics, scaling images/text to mimic audio volume/timing creates structure.
- **Proximity** — relative distance between objects in space or time. Close placement of dissimilar elements creates a focal point through contrast; isolation of an element via surrounding negative space denotes importance.

## 9. Contrast, Visual Weight, and Tension

- **Contrast** — difference between two things; greater difference = greater contrast. Achieved via value, scale, color, style, or juxtaposing dissimilar/contradictory elements. Figure-ground relationships are the most basic form. Too little contrast = static/boring; too much intense contrast = obscured hierarchy.
- **Visual weight** — degree of emphasis each element has; dominant elements are more noticeable, subordinate elements less so. Big shapes, bright colors, and high-value-contrast elements have more weight.
- **Common ways of creating emphasis** — value, hue, contrast, scale, variation or repetition, proximity.
- **Visual tension** — placement that draws the eye to a particular area or imbalances the composition. Often unwanted (elements too close to each other or to edges); occasionally positive when it complements content or provides a needed focal point. No absolute measurement — evaluate whether spaces between elements feel accidental.

## 10. Form and Meaning

- **Seeing combines perception and interpretation** — literal meaning, memory, and contextual associations. Formal elements have both literal and associated meaning; viewers decode messages by evaluating all elements.
- **Designer's job** — ensure content and form are complementary and enhance correct decoding rather than interfere with it. Decisions should be rooted in meaning, audience, and context, not style or decoration alone.
- **Less is more** — design combines structure and freedom. Filling space with form to look "more designed" is tempting but usually wrong; using the **fewest elements possible** to convey a message is the aim, especially when learning. Form-filled, over-the-top layouts can succeed but are not the default.

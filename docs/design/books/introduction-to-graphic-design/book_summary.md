## 1. Thesis and Scope

Aaris Sherin's *Introduction to Graphic Design: A Guide to Thinking, Process & Style* presents graphic design as a professional, client-based activity distinct from fine art. Where fine art expresses a personal vision, graphic design — also called **communication design** or **visual communication design** — combines text, imagery, and other visual elements to communicate a message or create an experience for a defined audience. Successful design occurs when that message or experience is optimally expressed.

The book's central argument is that design is conceptual before it is technical. Software literacy is necessary but never sufficient; the durable skills are concept development, audience awareness, typographic judgment, and the ability to compose visual elements in space. Across nine chapters, Sherin builds from the field's scope and the designer's responsibility, through the design process, into the formal vocabulary (form, color, type, image), composition (layout and grids), and production (printing, binding, file preparation), closing with the designer's lifelong way of seeing.

## 2. What Graphic Design Is

The terms **graphic design**, **communication design**, and **visual communication design** are used interchangeably; the older terms **graphic artist** and **commercial artist** predate the field's current name. Output has broadened — historically two-dimensional (books, magazines, posters, fliers), today three-dimensional (packaging, signage, installations) and digital (apps, websites, kiosks, virtual reality) — but the underlying principles of arrangement, structure, and expression have not changed.

Sherin distinguishes several specializations: **advertising design** (visuals and environments for promotion), **interactive design** (often abbreviated *IxD*; products and services a person interacts with), **interface design**, **web design**, **screen-based design**, **motion graphics** (combining video, still images, type, and audio), **print production**, and **industrial design** (also called *product design*; mass-produced objects and systems). Boundaries between print and screen have blurred — packaging and environmental signage are physical but not always printed, and brand identities live across both — so many companies now seek designers who can move between media. Whatever the specialty, every designer needs the formal principles of design, typography, and strong visual and spatial skills; software fluency comes second.

### 2.1. The Designer's Dual Responsibility

A designer is an intermediary between **client** (who pays) and **end user / audience** (who experiences the work). The book traces a historical shift in how this dual responsibility has been understood. Early industrial-era designers worked mainly for clients, exploiting consumer aspirations to drive purchases; users' real needs were largely absent from measures of success. *First Things First*, the 1964 manifesto initially written by Ken Garland and signed by twenty-two visual communicators, challenged designers to put their skills to "worthwhile use" rather than purely commercial interests. In the 1970s, industrial designer Victor Papanek's *Design for the Real World* argued that designers had an obligation to work for the greater good, not just their clients' financial well-being; his follow-up *The Green Imperative* extended the argument to environmental responsibility, asking whether designers, architects, and engineers can be held personally and legally liable for environmental harm caused by what they make. Papanek's ideas were initially denounced; today the underlying principle is broadly accepted, and **sustainable design** — eco-friendly papers, printing, and materials, plus working for like-minded organizations — is its concrete expression.

### 2.2. Skills, Concept, and Software

Sherin is emphatic that rules and software are scaffolding, not substitutes for thinking. Well-known designers break fundamental rules successfully, but the aim of most visual communication is to help users navigate visual material; learn the rules first to know when and how to break them. *Concept* — the idea behind a design — is central, and software is downstream of it. Designers need Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator, and InDesign, but knowing how to use them does not make one a graphic designer. Software platforms change throughout a typical undergraduate's degree; design thinking does not. Students who struggle with software usually catch up; students who struggle with concept development and typography stay disadvantaged into their careers. **Design thinking** — a range of cognitive problem-solving processes popularized by Tim Brown's 2009 book *Change by Design* — names this conceptual core.

The book closes its first chapter with practical career notes: roles cluster into owner/founder, managerial, creative/upper-level design, entry level, and support level positions; **freelancing** (independent designers paid hourly or per-job) and **contract work** (non-permanent employment, often through a third-party agency) are common; internships are a strong on-ramp, often worth taking even unpaid if responsibilities are clearly stated. Roles concentrate in cities, but the internet, file sharing, and online portfolios let designers work and find clients regardless of location.

## 3. The Design Process

Every piece of communication design is the result of a creative process — a repeatable series of steps used to solve conceptual and practical problems. Professional studios customize the process, but the underlying steps stay consistent. Sherin organizes them into a five-stage cycle:

1. **Brief / assignment** — the written document outlining the project's specific expectations: what to accomplish, target audience, time, budget, and any provided assets. Briefs are usually written by the client; school assignments mimic them but rarely include a budget.
2. **Research / strategy** — investigating the client, audience, context, and prior work, then developing a communications **strategy** (a plan of action designed to achieve specific goals).
3. **Visual design development** — brainstorming, **ideation** (generating ideas through diagrams or visual explorations), sketching, and laying out compositions on the computer.
4. **Revision** — redoing or changing the design after feedback.
5. **Implementation / production** — final digital or physical realization, which may involve printing or coding.

The components are modular: the cycle can be applied as a whole or pulled apart and reused. Time per phase scales with project size and deadline.

### 3.1. Research and Strategy

Research is the only way to make sure the designer is solving the correct problem. It informs decisions about the competition, prevents re-making what has already been done, builds a complete picture of the audience, and reveals the environment where the end result will be seen. On larger long-term projects, research can take at least a third of the designer's time. Sherin gives a vivid failure-mode example: designing a poster for a visiting designer using one's favorite typeface, only to discover the visiting designer is famous for typefaces made from found objects. The mismatch is avoidable with a quick search. Practical starting points are search engines and reviewing what other designers have done in the same category, while taking care to avoid mimicking or copying their ideas.

### 3.2. Concepts, Brainstorming, and Storytelling

A **concept** is the idea behind a design, developed *after* research, based on the specifics of content and the goals of the assignment, and often rooted in visual or verbal associations. Concepts can be overt (an advertising pun) or subtle (a book cover whose layout mimics the verticality of buildings described in the text). Audiences may appreciate the connection without being able to identify the concept. Advertisements, logos, and new-product packaging benefit most from strong concepts; instructional diagrams may be better served by simple text and drawings.

**Inspiration** — visual or mental stimulation that leads to a new idea — is nonlinear and unpredictable. Even at rest, the mind continues to work on problems posed earlier in the day. To jump-start ideas, Sherin recommends **brainstorming**, a structured activity using lists, diagrams, and sketches. The simplest method is **free association**: write down facts about the project, then list words that come to mind unedited for ten to fifteen minutes; map words back to the assignment afterward and turn the strongest ones into thumbnail sketches. Quantity is a hallmark of good brainstorming — most output won't end up in final layouts, but the more words produced, the greater the chance a unique concept will emerge. Take a break before evaluating; concepts that survive are like ingredients, applicable to different formats and deliverables. A complementary exercise, "ideas with actions," generates concepts by applying verbs (*magnify, distort, exaggerate, repeat, reduce, simplify, reverse, rearrange*) to the content; sketches should take less than a minute, ideally drawn with pen rather than pencil.

A persistent risk is the **cliché** — an idea or visual used so often it loses originality. Clichés feel safe but rarely produce memorable work. Sherin also warns against starting a design digitally: concepts are unlikely to be original, details get attention too early, software tools influence ideas more than concept does, and layouts feel stilted and predictable.

Visual narrative emerges from juxtaposing words and images that don't obviously belong together; the viewer's tendency to make sense of mismatches creates meaning. Several literary devices translate to design: **metaphors** rely on two elements together to create a third, less literal meaning (a lighted window and a closed door together standing for "life"); **similes** make overt comparisons ("the young woman is like the rose"); **puns** exploit dual meanings or similar-sounding words; and **personification** (also called anthropomorphism) applies human nature to objects or animals — common in advertising mascots like the GEICO gecko or the AFLAC duck.

### 3.3. Matching Output to Audience

Audience mismatch is a common student failure mode. The desire to make unique, "cool" work can override the realities of consumer preferences and the attributes of the target age group; the project fails regardless of how cool it looks. The fix is to define the audience narrowly — "everybody" is too broad — and ask both demographic questions (age, income, transport, brand loyalty) and stylistic ones (will a particular embellishment appeal? does the audience have time to read or do visuals carry the message?). Older adults may need larger type; children favor bright colors and characters; teenagers respond to lively-looking graphics; busy environments call for imagery over text. Designing for unfamiliar audiences should be practiced early; classmates and instructors can help confirm whether visuals match the intended target.

### 3.4. Revision, Critique, and Presentation

Most first ideas are not the final solution. Exploring multiple ideas leads to better-quality work and prevents repeating the same visual solution; even when an initial idea wins, refinement is necessary for placement, scale, and typography. Design companies typically present at least three versions to a client, and even after a concept is chosen, the designer continues to create variations of the layout before arriving at the final.

**Critique** — a written or verbal evaluation of a design project, usually with recommendations for improvement — exists to help the designer improve the work and mimics the professional environment. Negative feedback is hardest after long hours on a piece, but unrealized versions are never wasted: castoffs may serve future assignments. Sherin's tips for giving feedback include being specific (cite imagery, color choice, type rather than "I like it"), critiquing the work rather than the person, anchoring criticism in the project's stated goals and audience, and evaluating hierarchy directly — has the designer styled the most important information so it is seen first? *Great-looking visuals cannot save a project that fails to communicate content.*

When presenting work, the medium of presentation should match the medium of the final piece. Digital work is shown digitally (websites projected, motion graphics on a monitor); print work is shown printed or as three-dimensional mock-ups. **Craft** — neatness; how well a two- or three-dimensional item is displayed or constructed — markedly improves how design work is received in critique.

## 4. Form and Space

The third chapter introduces the formal vocabulary that the rest of the book builds on. **Form** is any element in a design — images, type, dots, lines — synonymous with *figure* and *element*. **Space** is the area or context in which visual elements are placed. Three principles govern decisions about how form and space relate: **concept** (the expression of an idea), **content** (the underlying message the form communicates), and **context** (the external or virtual environment where the design will be seen). **Structure** — the underlying organization (overt or subtle) determining how form exists in space — is what gives a composition coherence; grids are one example, but pairing of value, photo style, or any unifying principle can also provide it.

The compositional container has its own vocabulary: **live area** (screen design), **composition** or **compositional space** (print), and **format** (proportions, size, or orientation). The **ground** is the area without elements; **bleed** is the area outside the trim of a page (or the act of extending an element past the edge); **cropping** removes parts of an image to improve framing.

Design's basic building blocks — **points**, **lines**, **shapes**, **planes** — combine infinitely with type and imagery and are modified by visual variables to set mood, denote emphasis, and organize space. A point becomes a **dot** when marked; a second or third dot begins a story. A line is the space between two points, conveying movement, separating space, providing emphasis, and framing other elements; horizontal lines feel restful, angular lines create tension, squiggly lines suggest movement. **Shapes** are flat objects defined by lines, distinguished as **geometric** (hard edges; usually man-made) or **organic** (irregular edges; usually natural). Vertical rectangles are more active; horizontal rectangles more passive. Software re-creates hand gestures via line, pencil, or pen tool; **Bezier curves** drawn with the pen tool in graphics software like Illustrator and Photoshop are infinitely scalable.

**Mode** is the visual style used to render form — line art, drawing, photography, or simple lines and shapes — and visual style affects both explicit and associated meaning. **Texture** adds tactile quality to two-dimensional space; **pattern** organizes or activates space through repetition. **Figure-ground** is the relationship between form (positive space) and background (negative space, ground, white space); both are equally important, and effective use of negative space is one of the best ways to create emphasis without overcrowding.

### 4.1. Hierarchy, Balance, and Symmetry

**Hierarchy** is the visual logic that achieves clarity of message — how elements relate and how prominent each is. It is determined by visual weight and placement; meaning shifts with ordering, scale, proximity, color, and value. A composition lacking hierarchy causes confusion or uneasiness. **Balance**, by contrast, is based on gravity (think of a seesaw): visually heavy objects pull attention. Designers usually pursue *visual* balance rather than absolute balance. **Symmetry** offers pure equilibrium — right and left mirror across a central axis — and was the standard pre-twentieth-century method for title pages, poetry, and wedding invitations. **Asymmetry** is off-center placement without a mirroring counterpart; it emerged from the modern movement at the **Bauhaus** in 1920s–30s Germany. Asymmetry communicates complex messages more easily than symmetry, which is best kept simple, with few elements.

### 4.2. Activating Space

**Active design** is generally preferred over **static** design. **Movement** — changing object location in physical or virtual space, or guiding the eye around a page — can be literal (running horses) or conceptual (graduated abstract shapes); two-dimensional methods include sequencing, layering, bleeding, overlapping, clustering, and aligning. **Rhythm** is a uniform pattern or succession of strong and weak elements, achieved by progression, sequencing, repetition, or subtle variation. **Syncopation** interrupts a regular flow to create accents and emphasis. Rhythm is essential in motion graphics and multipage documents, where pacing must be designed. **Proximity** — the relative distance between objects in space or time — uses close placement of dissimilar elements to create a focal point through contrast, while isolation via surrounding negative space denotes importance.

**Contrast** is the difference between two things, achieved via value, scale, color, style, or juxtaposing dissimilar elements; figure-ground relationships are the most basic form. Too little contrast produces a static composition; too much obscures hierarchy. **Visual weight** is the degree of emphasis each element carries; **visual tension** is placement that draws the eye to a particular area or imbalances the composition — usually unwanted, occasionally a needed focal point.

### 4.3. Less Is More

Sherin closes with a directive: design combines structure and freedom, and using the *fewest elements possible* to convey a message is the aim, especially when learning. Filling space with form to look "more designed" is tempting but usually wrong. Form-filled, over-the-top layouts can succeed but are not the default.

## 5. Working with Color

**Color** is what the eye perceives when light reflects off surfaces, produced by varying wavelengths. The hue seen depends on the surface's reflectivity. Combining multiple pigments in ink or paint produces black (the mixture reflects so little light that black is perceived); on screens, color is light emitted from each quadrant of the display.

Color carries **subjective associative meaning** influenced by place, age, gender, personal experience, religion, and ethnicity. Brides wear white in Europe and the Americas but bright or intense colors in Asian countries (red signifies happiness in traditional Chinese bridal ceremonies); white accompanies funerals in some countries while black or gray indicate mourning in others. Designers must check cultural connotations: what works in New York may not work in Bangkok or Mexico City.

### 5.1. Systems of Primary Colors

Three primary systems coexist for different situations, and design software allows switching between modes:

| System | Primaries | Use case |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Additive (RGB)** | Red, green, blue | Screen design — mimics how light stimulates the eye |
| **Subtractive (CMYK)** | Cyan, magenta, yellow, black | Print design — combinations produce printed hues |
| **Artists' primary colors** | Red, blue, yellow | Paint and other art-making; basis of color theory and color-wheel relationships |

The **visible spectrum** — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, blue violet, violet — is a limited range, and the eye discerns roughly ten million colors, all combinations of primaries.

### 5.2. Color Wheel and Schemes

The **color wheel** is a diagram showing relationships between hues; it serves as a starting point for student palettes. The book uses the subtractive (artists') wheel as the basis. **Primary hues** (red, yellow, blue) are pure; **secondary hues** (orange, green, violet) are equal mixes of two primaries; **tertiary hues** sit between primary and secondary, with one primary dominating. The yellow-orange-red half of the wheel is warm; the green-blue-violet half is cool.

Core terminology: **hue** is another word for color; **saturation** (also called *chroma*) is intensity, strength, or purity without added black or white; **brightness** (also called *value*) is relative lightness or darkness; a **tint** is a color plus white, a **shade** is a color plus black. **Temperature** describes warmth or coolness — warm colors (red, yellow, orange undertones) feel active and dynamic; cool colors (blue, green undertones) feel dependable and calming. Warm colors advance, cool colors recede; even within grays, a gray with blue or red mixed in gains depth, while pure black-white grays appear flat.

**Color schemes** are pairings located by relative position on the wheel. **Complementary** schemes (opposites such as red/green or blue/orange) produce the greatest contrast and may visually vibrate when adjacent. **Split complementary** uses a primary plus the two secondaries adjacent to its complement, which is less harsh. **Analogous** schemes (a primary plus two adjacent hues) feel harmonious because the wavelengths are similar. **Triads** are three hues equidistant on the wheel; **tetrads** combine four complementary or split-complementary hues, with one dominant and the others subordinate to avoid confusion. **Monotone** or **monochromatic** palettes use a single hue with its tints and shades — harmonious and less expensive to print.

### 5.3. Color Theory and Perception

**Color theory** is the practical analysis of how hues relate and what happens when two or more are mixed in different proportions. Research suggests the eye prefers pairings that produce a neutral, supported by the **afterimage** phenomenon: staring at a color and then looking away produces its complement, possibly because retinal receptors fatigue and produce a complement of equal value and intensity. The brain's search for that balance is one rationale for including complements in a palette.

Perception is relative — colors are rarely seen in isolation, and the same hue is affected by proximity to other hues and by physical context. A white square on black appears larger than a black square on white; a gray square looks lighter on white than on black.

The Bauhaus produced the most influential modern theorists. **Johannes Itten**, master instructor at Bauhaus Weimar in the 1920s, outlined three approaches to color in *The Elements of Color*: visually (**impression**), emotionally (**expression**), and symbolically (**construction**); he believed designers must study hues in different situations to build sensitivity. **Joseph Albers**, Itten's student and later a teacher at Black Mountain College and Yale, wrote *Interactions of Color* (1963), studying how hues change next to others and arguing perception is subjective — fifty students asked to picture red would name fifty different reds. Both held that recognizing successful color pairings is a learnable skill improved by practice.

### 5.4. Choosing a Palette

A **color palette** is a limited set of colors chosen for a composition or set of deliverables, usually including **dominant** tones (used most), **subordinate** tones, and **accent** colors used sparingly. Sherin's three-color method picks a dominant hue that matches the message or audience, chooses a subordinate to complement or contrast, and then adds an accent — a tint or shade of one of the others, a neutral, or a wholly new hue. The palette should match the client's identity: saturated palettes feel lively; muted or neutral feel subdued. A bank wants secure, conservative tones; a financial firm rarely uses fun or frivolous colors. Younger, nimble brands take risks — Snapchat's bright fluorescent yellow, Twitter's light blue.

Starting a layout in black and white can be useful when color choice feels overwhelming: it lets the designer set placement and hierarchy first, then add one or two colors at a time. One-color palettes are cheaper to print and develop hierarchy easily; black-and-white offers optimum contrast and stands out in busy environments. Two-color pairings add variation while staying simple; image-based palettes sample tones from a photograph or fine-art reference for visual tie-in.

When printing black in CMYK, moving the slider to **K (key)** alone is right for one-color jobs and black text, but large areas can look mottled because paper shows through; **rich black** — a custom swatch at 60% cyan, 60% magenta, 40% yellow, and 100% black — produces a pleasing solid black for large areas.

Color repetition develops visual unity and connects elements; color can also override size — small bright shapes can appear larger than bigger muted ones. Color must support the intended hierarchy, not disrupt it. The least legible combinations (red on blue, orange on blue, yellow on orange, green on orange) appear to vibrate; with dark backgrounds use lighter or brighter colors on top, and vice versa.

## 6. Typography

**Typography** is the visual expression of the written word. Sherin defines it broadly: it works whenever it conveys a message, whether type is written with a magic marker, dots and dashes, or elegant letterforms. **Typesetting** — the visual arrangement of text — originally referred to physically setting metal type and now refers primarily to digital work with large amounts of text. A **typographer** designs typefaces; **type foundries** own the rights to typeface designs and sell digital versions to the public.

### 6.1. Anatomy and Classification

Letterforms have a precise vocabulary: the **x-height** (height of lowercase letters), **baseline** (line on which capital letters rest), **cap height** (baseline to top of capitals), **meanline** (top of the lowercase body), **ascenders** (strokes rising above the meanline), **descenders** (strokes falling below the baseline), **stress** (the axis on which a letter is drawn), **terminal** (end of a stroke without a serif), **serif** (small element added to the end of a stroke), and **sans serif** (typefaces without serifs).

**Type classification** divides typefaces into categories based on appearance, including weight, stroke, and the way each part of a letter ends. Major classifications include **humanist** and **old style** (fifteenth–sixteenth century, calligraphic), **transitional** (sharper serifs, more vertical axis), serif faces of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries (vertical axis with high stroke contrast), and **sans serif** types from early-twentieth-century modernism. Decoration produces expressive typefaces used for headings and display; complexity interferes with body-copy readability.

### 6.2. Hierarchy, Alignment, and Spacing

Type is measured using **points** (12 points = 1 pica); picas are common in book, newspaper, and multipage layout because they avoid fractions. **Weight** — thickness or thinness, determined by the ratio of stroke thickness to character height — varies from light through roman or book weight (mid-weight, designed for large amounts of text) to bold and black. **Italics** are slanted versions with letters specifically redesigned for the slant; **obliques** are simply slanted versions of the typeface. Most readable body text is 9 to 11 point; the 12-point default for term papers is usually too big for design pieces. Sans serif faces tend to appear larger than serif at the same size. **Display type** is larger type used for headings or titles; the eye has trouble noticing differences smaller than two points, so display must be visibly bigger than body text. Digital scale is variable — depending on screen and viewing distance, and users may resize — so initial visual hierarchy matters at least as much on screen as in print.

**Typographic hierarchy** is the arrangement of elements in a graduated series, from most prominent to least prominent. Sherin recommends making clear distinctions using the fewest variables; too much styling obscures hierarchy. Reading text aloud reveals which words deserve emphasis. The standard variables are ordering (decide what is first, second, third before any styling), space, weight, placement and thresholds, scale, and color.

The four basic alignments are flush left, flush right, centered, and justified. **Flush left** — vertically aligned to the left edge with a ragged right — is the most readable and is the default for left-to-right languages; a feathered rag is preferable to a tight, even rag. **Flush right** suits small chunks like captions and sidebars but is poor for long blocks. **Centered** alignment works for invitations, title pages, packaging, and pull quotes but is difficult for long paragraphs because there is no consistent threshold for the eye to return to. **Justified** alignment — smooth on both sides — is the standard for books, magazines, and newspapers; it risks **rivers** of white space and requires manual tracking and forced hyphenation.

Three forms of spacing matter most. **Kerning** is the space between two characters; type designers set defaults, but optical correctness trumps numerical correctness, and combinations involving y, o, e, a, and w are particularly problematic. Display sizes nearly always need additional hand kerning. **Tracking** is the space between groups of letters, lines, or paragraphs — used to fix words that don't fit, eliminate widows or orphans, or fix faces that feel too crowded or open; it is generally correct when imperceptible. **Leading** is the space between lines of type, measured in points (the term dates from metal-type spacers); the default is two points larger than body text (e.g., "10 on 12"), and small body text or fonts with high ascenders need more.

Line length affects readability. Readers scan groups of three or four words rather than every word; nine to twelve words per line is ideal, or 45 to 75 characters including spaces. Long lines cause fatigue and lost place; very short lines force excessive hyphenation and a choppy rag.

### 6.3. Typographic Niceties

**Typographic niceties** are small styling decisions that make text clearer and easier to read; designers maintain a list of "typographic sins" — unsightly orphans and widows, unhung quotes, rivers, un-kerned quotation marks at paragraph ends. **Hyphenation** is the marked syllabic division of words; a hyphenated syllable should have at least three letters, and there should never be more than two consecutive hyphens. **Hung punctuation** places quotation marks and bullets outside the text block to maintain clean alignment. An **orphan** is a single word alone on a line; a **widow** is a single line at the beginning or end of a column. Both are fixed by tracking, kerning, hyphenation, or paragraph breaking.

**Ligatures** are unique typographic characters produced when two or more letters are joined, created for problematic combinations like double f or f+i; only serif typefaces include them. **Em dashes** indicate a break in thought (replacing colon or semicolon); **en dashes** indicate a connection between things or names (replacing hyphen).

When vertical type is needed (signage, book spines), rotate horizontal text 90 degrees clockwise rather than stacking individual letters, which produces awkward spacing and ransom-note results. Type color depends on typeface mass, scale, and hierarchy: light colors won't read on white (switch the background to brown or blue); blocky typefaces work with very light or saturated hues, while delicate faces do not. White body text on black often appears smaller — increase point size by 0.5 to 1 point for visual consistency.

### 6.4. Choosing Typefaces

Free fonts often need extra kerning and tracking and tend toward expressive display rather than body use. **Body copy** — also called body text or running text — is the main vehicle for content and needs the most readable face. The chapter highlights designer staples such as **Baskerville** (John Baskerville, 1724; thick-thin contrast), **Bodoni** (Giambattista Bodoni, 1798; un-bracketed serifs), **Garamond** (Claude Garamond, used as early as 1530; among the most legible for large text), **Georgia** (Matthew Carter, 1993, designed for low-resolution screens), **Futura** (Paul Renner, 1927), **Frutiger** (Adrian Frutiger, 1970; originally Roissy for Charles de Gaulle Airport signage), **Helvetica** (Eduard Hoffmann at Haas Type Corporation commissioned Max Miedinger to design it, 1957, originally Neue Haas Grotesk), **Optima** (Herman Zapf, 1950s), **Univers** (Frutiger, 1954), and **Verdana** (David Berlow and David Jonathan Ross with Matthew Carter, 1996; built for onscreen display).

When pairing display and text faces, the safest approach is a clearly different second face — serif and sans serif together, two noticeably different fonts rather than two similar ones. Hand-drawn type should be drawn by hand; faking it produces predictable results.

Type can also act as image. Letters or words altered so their formal qualities override direct meaning can replace imagery; Sherin advises starting with cleaner typefaces rather than already-decorative ones. Type as **texture** uses words, lines, or paragraphs layered or distorted to make reading impossible — used for backgrounds or shapes — and must deliberately obscure meaning so it does not compete with hierarchy. **Rules** — designer term for lines, thick or thin — separate small chunks of text or highlight a single line; place rules above or below type, not between two lines, so a rule does not crash through descenders.

## 7. Using and Creating Imagery

People see before they read; imagery is one of design's most powerful tools. An image in a layout can serve as focal point, visual narrative, complement to text, background, or decoration; the choice depends on context, audience expectations, and stylistic goals. Images can be made by hand, with a camera, or directly in software with a mouse or stylus. **Narrative** in design — the story — can be both textual and visual, and imagery can complement text or stand alone.

### 7.1. Representation, Abstraction, and Reductive Forms

Visual material exists on a continuum between **representational** and **abstract**. A representational image is clearly recognizable and refers to life — a representational dog looks similar to a real dog. Representational imagery is the mainstay of design and is ubiquitous in advertising's persuasive direct associations. **Abstract images** exist with some independence from the natural world; useful when type content dominates, when no obvious subject exists, or when a non-specific visual texture is needed. As visuals become less pictorial, meaning becomes less literal and admits wider interpretation.

Three reductive image forms compress meaning into compact marks. **Symbols** are graphic representations of ideas or concepts that exist without pictorially describing what they relate to — Nike's swoosh, Target's bull's-eye, McDonald's arches do not describe goods or services but become brand-associated; the same form can mean different things across cultures (the swastika as Nazi fascism versus Hindu and Buddhist well-being). **Icons** are visual forms representing a person, object, or idea reduced to simplest form, used for logos, signage, and app and web navigation. **Pictograms** (also called *pictographs*) represent a letter, word, phrase, or physical concept and rely on viewer association — restroom symbols are the classic case; pictograms transcend cultural and language barriers and are useful where literacy is uneven. Early writing developed from pictograms, and Mandarin still preserves traces.

### 7.2. Semiotics, Modes, and Style

**Semiotics** is the study of signs and symbols and how meaning is created and communicated; it is important to graphic design because designers always communicate to an audience and must anticipate associations. The book describes three stages of interpretation: **perception** (the viewer becomes aware of the sign), **manipulation** (the viewer interprets and considers how to respond), and **consummation** (the viewer responds). Two terms describe layers of meaning: **denotation** is the direct or literal meaning without personal feeling, and **connotation** is the associated or interpretive meaning based on experience and ideas the viewer already holds.

**Mode** is the media or technique used to produce an image — a photograph, line drawing, or ornate historical illustration of the same subject produces different reactions and interpretations. **Style** is appearance or distinctive visual character. For literal interpretation, use representational imagery (photograph, realistic illustration); for interpretive meaning, use obscured or abstracted visuals or those that contrast with text. Combining modes — photograph plus abstract texture, for instance — extends meaning, but the grouping must have clear purpose.

### 7.3. Sourcing, Manipulating, and Owning Images

**Stock images** are sold by companies (usually online) for use in creative projects; sites charge per-image fees or subscriptions and sell photographic, illustration, vector, and video material. Stock has improved over time, but designers should always confirm with instructors whether stock is permitted versus a self-generated requirement. **Self-initiated imagery** — visual material created directly by the designer or team (photos, collages, paintings, drawings, prints) — is tailored to the project and particularly suited to student work because it costs nothing.

Hand-made approaches include **drawing and illustration**, **painting** (effective for textures or backgrounds, sometimes referencing a specific moment such as Van Gogh swirls; mask off areas behind text so brush strokes don't reduce readability), **printmaking** (woodcuts, linoleum cuts, silkscreen, lithography, and etching), **collage** (combining visual elements on a background), **photomontage** (two or more photographic images combined into a composite), and **montage** (separate elements combined into a new and different image).

Digital production includes mouse, trackpad, or stylus input; tracing a hand sketch in Illustrator with the pen tool; and Live Trace conversion of black-and-white scans to vector. Many instructors prefer hand-input lines to Live Trace because skill-poor settings produce filter-like results. Image formats divide into **raster** (dot-matrix pixels, used for photos; scaling up reduces clarity; edited in Photoshop) and **vector** (mathematical expressions of geometric shapes; infinitely scalable; used for logos, type, and items needing crispness). Common file formats include AI (Illustrator), EPS, GIF, INDD (InDesign — rarely accepted by printers; export to PDF), JPG (compressed; fine for screen, not print), PDF (the right file to send a commercial printer), PNG (raster with lossless compression for screen), PSD (multi-layered Photoshop), and TIFF (raster bitmap for photo manipulation and print).

Custom photography is almost unrivaled at quickly communicating ideas and tone; even great photographers take dozens of shots before identifying the best. **Cropping** alters visual material to fit composition and balance; tighter crops are usually more dynamic, and crops need not be rectangular — textures, patterns, and photos can be cropped to shapes or letterforms. Layering and transparency let elements stack and interact; **blending modes** in Photoshop or Illustrator combine layers using preset effects, but layered effects often look better on screen than print.

**Copyright** gives the originator exclusive rights to use or distribute work in a region for a set time; a creator usually retains copyright for seventy years after death. **Permissions** or **photo releases** are required for client and commercial work. Ownership varies by context: fine-art photographers hold copyright on their work, but commercial work-for-hire belongs to the client; commissioned illustration belongs to the company unless otherwise stipulated; design or photography produced as part of one's job belongs to the company; student work belongs to the student, though some schools stipulate reproduction rights for publicity.

## 8. Layout and Arrangement

Every designer must learn to organize text and images in space so that the design's concept is expressed and the content makes sense to the viewer. The layout process is both rational and intuitive. Hierarchy provides the blueprint for initial decisions about placement, scale, and proximity; more specific stylistic decisions come from sketching and brainstorming. The same elements can produce a variety of layouts, and experimentation is required to identify the successful arrangements. Sherin recommends sketching by hand before opening software — sketches let ideas move quickly and yield better visual decisions than starting at a blank screen.

### 8.1. Emphasis and Flow

**Emphasis** is the act of deciding which elements are more and less important *before* layout begins; iterate emphasis through scale, color, placement, and proximity. Emphasis is relative — how much one item stands out depends on what surrounds it. Common arrangement problems to avoid include stepping type or imagery (content appears scattered), placing elements too close to each other or to a compositional edge (visual tension), cramming multiple alignment systems without breathing room (visual confusion), and equal visual weight between image and text (obscured hierarchy). Multiple alignment systems can coexist when each piece of text is distinct and there is enough white space between them.

Color and value are as important as scale and placement; color affects hierarchy and provides emphasis. To test whether color is improving a layout, eliminate color completely and re-evaluate — some designs are more effective in black and white. The less color present in a composition, the more noticeable the individual hues will be. Warm colors stand out against cooler tones; adding a warm color to the foreground provides emphasis and creates a sense of depth on a flat page.

**Flow** is the direction the designer wants the viewer's eye to move around the design. Visual variables such as placement, scale, color, and proximity entice the viewer to look at elements in a particular order. Readers of Western alphabets read left to right and top to bottom; layout flow can be modeled on this principle. Avoid layouts where elements direct the eye off the edge of the page, where supporting information has greater importance than the heading, or where layered images and text obscure meaning. Across panels of an accordion-fold brochure, placing text chunks at the same height across panels provides a system that makes content easy to follow.

### 8.2. Margins, Gutters, and Spreads

**Margins** are guides indicating where text and other visuals should be placed — the space between the live area of a design and the edge of the composition. Without margins, nothing keeps text from sliding to the page edge. Too-tight margins look crowded; overly generous margins make content feel lost. A practical rule is to avoid placing content where the thumbs would touch the page when the finished object is held. Wider bottom margins produce more balanced layouts than equal top and bottom. Text-heavy projects (novels, magazines) tend toward tighter margins; brochures and art books toward generous ones; apps tighter, online fashion magazines more generous.

The **gutter** is the space between columns of text or between two pages of a book; the space between two pages is also called the **fold** or **spine** and disrupts the flow of content. Narrow gutters between columns make text feel crowded; gutters that are too wide break the visual connection. When a binding does not open flat, add extra space to interior margins so content is not lost in the gutter.

In multipage documents, the goal is to motivate the viewer to keep reading and turn the page. **Verso** and **recto** are the left and right pages of a spread (reversed in right-to-left languages such as Japanese, Arabic, and Hebrew). Most print-based multipage documents are designed as **spreads** — two pages sitting next to each other — rather than as single pages; set up facing pages in InDesign or multiple art boards in Illustrator from the start.

### 8.3. Grids

A **grid** is an underlying structure of horizontal and vertical thresholds (or rectangles) used to organize typographic and pictorial elements, specifying where content should appear across multiple pages and items in a series. Grids are used everywhere from books and magazines to web pages, brochures, posters, and application designs. They are a tool, not an absolute — used to produce both highly structured and extremely experimental work; the designer can break the grid with call-outs, imagery, or other elements.

Symmetrical grids mirror modules and alignment points across the spread, suiting content-heavy multipage documents like books and magazines. Asymmetrical grids place a quadrant in different positions on each side, suiting single-page formats (posters, websites), web design where pages are seen one after another, or cases where content varies considerably side to side.

Sherin describes several grid types. **Wireframes** are used in screen-based designs as structural models showing functionality, navigation, and content areas. **Constructional grids** use elements in a composition and their placement as the basis for axis lines; unlike most systems, they are organic and created during layout. **Column grids** divide a page into columns with gutters; column widths can be consistent or one column may be narrower for captions. **Modular grids** use horizontal and vertical alignment markers to divide space into equally sized cells; cells can hold individual chunks or be combined for larger zones. Module size matters — too-small modules give too many alignment options and obscure the grid's purpose, while too-large modules don't provide enough placement areas. Combining systems is allowed: the same project may use a two-column grid on some pages and a three-column grid on others.

## 9. Context and Production

**Production basics** are the materials and processes that transform ideas and software files into finished design projects. First-year students rarely send files to a commercial printer, but they do create finished comps for critique and portfolio.

### 9.1. Comps, Printing, and File Preparation

A **comp** — short for *comprehensive* — is a visual layout of a design solution shown digitally or as a two- or three-dimensional mock-up. In professional practice, comps are shown to clients before final production; the client may accept or request revisions. For complicated or oversized projects, a digital mock-up that drops the layout into a photograph of an actual environment (a poster on a bus stop, signage on a building exterior) is often acceptable.

Two main printing systems dominate. **Digital printing** divides into **ink-jet** (sprays wet ink onto specially coated papers; ideal for large-format pieces, photography, and image-dominant work; bright fairly true colors; relatively slow) and **laser** (adheres dry-pigment ink using heat; fast, inexpensive; ideal for comps and multipage student projects, with better-quality units offering duplexing or booklet printing; limited paper sizes such as 8.5×11 and 11×17 in the US; colors more muted or darker than on screen). **Offset lithography** transfers an inked image from a metal CMYK plate to a rubber blanket and then to paper; it is the choice for professional jobs of more than 2,000 copies or very high-quality prints, and it supports custom colors, metallic and fluorescent inks, and finishes — though plate-separation overhead makes it expensive for school projects.

Resolution depends on output. Screen targets 72 dpi; most print production projects need 300 dpi at 100% of the intended size. **Pixilation** occurs when a bitmap image is too low-quality or has been enlarged until pixels show. For print, TIFF is preferred for Photoshop bitmap imagery; EPS or PDF for vector files (Illustrator line art, illustrations, logotypes); PSD when transparency is required. Final imagery should be saved in CMYK and flattened before placement into layout programs unless transparency is required. PDFs are usually printed via Adobe Acrobat and embed font information so non-system fonts don't need to be installed; color profiles between Acrobat and other programs may differ, so check with the service provider when exact color matching is critical.

InDesign files only *link* to images and fonts; **File > Package** collects the layout, linked images, graphics, and fonts into a single folder before moving the project or sending it to print. A printing checklist verifies printer and lab specs, confirms all files are linked, flattens layers (while preserving an editable version), confirms color profiles match the printer's, checks that imagery is not pixilated or distorted, and confirms fonts are installed (not needed when printing from PDF).

### 9.2. Binding, Folds, and Mounting

**Binding** physically collects and assembles pages into a book, brochure, or other multipage form using glue, thread, wire, or plastic combs. The book describes several methods. **Perfect binding** (also called *adhesive binding*) stacks same-size pages, applies adhesive along one edge, and adds a heavy-weight cover; widely used for paperbacks and magazines; it needs at least about twenty sheets to adhere properly. **Saddle stitch** prints both sides of a spread on the same sheet, gathers the sheets, folds them in half, and staples along the fold; it is cheap and doable by hand with long-arm staplers. **Spiral binding** punches holes along the edge and threads wire or plastic combs through; lies completely flat when open. **Sewn bindings** drive thread through folded pages and are used for textbooks and art books.

For shorter pieces, folds replace bindings. Common folds include the four-page simple fold (single fold yielding four panels), the four-page short fold (asymmetric fold with two larger and two smaller panels), the **accordion fold** (paper folded at least twice with each fold opposite the previous), the **barrel fold** (outer edges folded inward toward the center), and the **French fold** (folded first horizontally, then vertically). **Scoring** (also called *creasing*) compresses paper along a fold line and is required for card stock and heavy-weight paper.

**Trimming** cuts edges off a layout or crops it. **Trimmed flush** matches the edge of the page to the edge of the design; **crop marks** indicate where to cut when color or imagery doesn't reach the edge. A metal ruler with a cork back, a self-healing surface, and sharp X-Acto or matt-knife blades are essentials; multiple cuts along the length are safer than forcing the blade through thick material. Mounting flush requires mounting before trimming so no extra paper hangs over; assignments requiring a black or white border should generally use at least 1.5 inches.

Adhesives have trade-offs. Rubber cement excels short-term but is toxic and impermanent; spray mount works at large scale but requires a spray booth or well-ventilated room; glue stick is quick and cheap but less strong; double-sided tape is convenient but only adheres where it sits; PVA glue (polyvinyl acetate, also known as wood glue or white glue) gives excellent adhesion and dries clear but can warp paper if too much is applied; StudioTac (paper mounting adhesive) is non-water-based and pressure-activated but expensive.

### 9.3. Design as a Working Process

Design is creative *and* practical: professional designers also handle project management — time management, file naming, saving, backing up, binding, and finishing. Sherin advises avoiding the term *final* in file names and using a rational numbered system based on project name and version (e.g., `Intro_Project_1_1.1.psd`, `Intro_Project_1_1.2.psd`). **Save As** copies the current document and saves a new version under a different name or location; better to have many well-organized versions than few. Every design student should own at least two portable hard drives (one for transport, one as backup); cloud services like Google Drive and Dropbox provide limited free storage, and reminders to back up at the end of every semester prevent loss.

## 10. The Designer's Way of Seeing

The book closes by reframing design as a way of life. Design is conceptual *and* practical — it occurs when great ideas are visualized using type, images, and other materials. The book is a beginning, not a complete education; advanced classes will add new skills and new ways of delivering content. Becoming a designer means learning to be a sponge for the visual world, until badly kerned letters become annoying, environmental colors invite use in projects, and textures and patterns read as raw material rather than backdrop. Continued practice connects the designer to a community of people committed to solving practical problems and exploring new visual territory.

## 11. Key Takeaways

- **Design is a client-based discipline distinguished from fine art by intent.** It serves a message to a defined audience under cost, production, and contextual constraints; constraints are what make design.
- **The designer has dual responsibility — to client and to audience.** *First Things First* (Garland, 1964) and Victor Papanek's *Design for the Real World* shifted the field toward broader social and environmental obligation; sustainable design is its concrete expression today.
- **Concept beats software.** Software changes throughout an undergraduate's degree; design thinking does not. Students who struggle with concept development and typography are at lasting disadvantage.
- **The design process is repeatable but modular.** Brief → research/strategy → visual design development → revision → production. Research is the only way to make sure the designer is solving the correct problem.
- **Avoid clichés and don't start digitally.** Concepts are unlikely to be original or experimental at the screen; sketching by hand and brainstorming in quantity produces stronger ideas.
- **Match output to audience.** "Cool" cannot rescue a project that fails the brief; let go of ideas that don't serve the work and keep them for future use.
- **Hierarchy is the foundation.** Whether organizing form in space, choosing type weights, or laying out a spread, hierarchy determines whether the message is decoded or lost.
- **Less is more.** Use the fewest elements possible to convey a message; over-designed layouts can succeed but are not the default.
- **Color, type, and image are interlocking systems.** RGB for screen, CMYK for print, artists' primaries for theory; type as form, texture, and image; representational, abstract, and reductive imagery — each reinforces the others when chosen intentionally.
- **Production is part of design.** Comps, printing methods, binding, folds, file packaging, and adhesives all influence whether the final piece succeeds; designers also manage time, file naming, and backups as part of the work.
- **Critique is for improvement.** Specific, work-focused feedback anchored in stated goals and audience is more useful than praise; great-looking visuals cannot save a project that fails to communicate content.
- **Design is a way of seeing.** Practice changes how the designer perceives type, color, and form in everyday life; the discipline is a continuing practice, not a static skill set.

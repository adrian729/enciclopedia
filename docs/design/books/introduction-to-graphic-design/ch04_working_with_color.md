## 1. What Color Is

- **Color** — perceived by the eye when light reflects off surfaces; produced by varying wavelengths of light. The hue seen depends on the surface's reflectivity and which wavelengths it produces. Ambient light and pigment intensity affect hues, shades, and tints.
- **White contains all colors** — combining multiple pigments in ink/paint produces black (the mixture reflects so little light that black is perceived); on screens, color is light emitted from each quadrant of the display.

## 2. Subjective and Cultural Meaning

- **Subjective associative meaning** — colors symbolize ideas and evoke meaning influenced by place, age, gender, personal experience, religion, and ethnicity. Brides wear white in Europe/the Americas but bright/intense colors in Asian countries (red signifies happiness in traditional Chinese bridal ceremonies). White accompanies funerals in some countries; black/gray indicate mourning in others.
- **Designers must check cultural connotations** — what works in New York may not work in Bangkok or Mexico City.

## 3. Systems of Primary Colors

- **Three primary systems** exist for different situations; design software allows switching between modes:

| System | Primaries | Use case |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Additive (RGB)** | Red, green, blue | Screen design — mimics how light stimulates the eye |
| **Subtractive (CMYK)** | Cyan, magenta, yellow, black | Print design — combinations produce printed hues |
| **Artists' primary colors** | Red, blue, yellow | Paint and other art-making; basis of color theory and color-wheel relationships |

- **Visible spectrum** — limited range of colors distinguishable by the human eye: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, blue violet, violet. The eye discerns approximately **10 million colors**, all from combinations of primaries.

## 4. Core Color Terminology

- **Hue** — another word for color; used interchangeably. Different hues are seen depending on the wavelength reflected.
- **Saturation (chroma)** — intensity, strength, or purity of a color without addition of black or white. Saturated hues are purer.
- **Brightness (value)** — relative degree of lightness or darkness. White lightens, black darkens.
- **Tint vs. shade**:

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Tint** | Variation of a color plus white |
| **Shade** | Variation of a color plus black |

- **Temperature** — perceived warmth or coolness:

| Family | Undertones |
|--------|------------|
| **Warm colors** | Red, yellow, orange undertones — active, dynamic |
| **Cool colors** | Blue and green undertones — dependable, calming |

- **Warm colors advance, cool colors recede** — even within grays: a gray with blue/red mixed in gains depth; pure black/white grays appear flat. Warmer colors look like they sit in front of cooler ones.

## 5. The Color Wheel and Hue Categories

- **Color wheel** — diagram developed to help designers/artists understand relationships between hues; a starting point for student palettes. The book uses the subtractive (artists') wheel as classification basis.
- **Hue families on the wheel**:

| Category | Definition |
|----------|------------|
| **Primary hues** | Red, yellow, blue — pure hues, unrelated to each other; mixing them creates any color |
| **Secondary hues** | Orange, green, violet — equal amounts of two primaries |
| **Tertiary hues** | Located between primary and secondary; one primary dominates the mix |

- **Warm/cool indicated on wheel** — yellow/orange/red half is warm; green/blue/violet half is cool.

## 6. Harmonies and Color Schemes

- **Color schemes** are pairings located by relative position on the wheel:

| Scheme | Definition | Effect |
|--------|------------|--------|
| **Complementary** | Opposite on wheel (e.g., red/green, blue/orange) | Greatest contrast; combined cancel into a neutral; placed adjacent may visually vibrate |
| **Split complementary** | A primary plus the two secondaries adjacent to its complement | Less harsh than direct complements |
| **Analogous** | Primary plus two adjacent hues | Harmonious — similar wavelengths |
| **Triad** | Three hues equidistant on the wheel (e.g., the three primaries) | Triadic combination |
| **Tetrad** | Four complementary or split-complementary hues | Use one dominant, others subordinate, to avoid confusion |
| **Monotone / Monochromatic** | Single hue plus its tints and shades | Harmonious; less expensive to print |

- **Complementary warning** — can seem loud/garish next to each other; avoid where the message conflicts with such a palette.

## 7. Value, Neutrals, and Color Families

- **Neutrals** — black, white, gray, and muted tones of brown or beige; don't appear on the color wheel and are often overlooked. Can stand alone or extend a palette. Like other colors they have warm/cool variants. Versatile (masculine or feminine), often associated with subdued subjects but also used for edgier ones.
- **Value** — relative lightness/darkness of a color. Hue is stronger than value on its own; color added to gray makes that shape stand out. Greater value difference = more contrast. Value indicates hierarchy: equal-value shapes have equal importance; altering one value makes it dominant or subordinate. Value depends on ambient light — indoors looks darker, outdoors looks brighter.
- **Light colors** — pale shades, yellows, hues with much white added. Good as accents/backgrounds; balance darker shades. More feminine than saturated versions; common in baby clothing and children's accessories. Tricky on type — avoid for text unless against a much darker background.
- **Dark colors** — hues containing black; dramatic, useful as background tones. Pair dark colors with lighter or more saturated ones; two dark colors of similar value are hard to distinguish.
- **Bright colors** — pure pigment, no black/white added (also fluorescents). Strong attention-grabbers; limit how many saturated/super-bright colors appear together — supercharged combinations irritate the eye and harm hierarchy. Often paired with black or brown to offset.

- **Color blindness** — inability to see certain colors; affects approximately 8 percent of the population, most often men. **Red-green color blindness** is the most common. Designers should consider whether color-coded references rely on distinctions a colorblind viewer can't make.

## 8. Color Theory and Perception

- **Color theory** — practical analysis of how hues relate and what happens when two or more are mixed in different proportions. Provides guidance for pairings and the real-world effects of color on objects/environments.
- **Eye seeks equilibrium** — research shows the eye prefers pairings that produce a neutral. Based on the **afterimage** phenomenon: staring at a color and looking away produces its complement, possibly because retinal receptors fatigue and produce a complement of equal value/intensity. The brain looks for the balance of a hue paired with its opposite — a rationale for including complements in a palette.
- **Perception is relative** — colors are rarely seen in isolation. A single color is affected by proximity to and interaction with other hues and by the physical context.
- **Spatial orientation effect** — a white square on black appears larger than a black square on white; a gray square looks lighter on white than on black. Colors change based on the surrounding hue.
- **Bauhaus color theorists**:

| Teacher | Contribution |
|---------|--------------|
| **Johannes Itten** | Master instructor at Bauhaus, Weimar (1920s); *The Elements of Color* outlined three approaches to color solutions: visually (**impression**), emotionally (**expression**), symbolically (**construction**). Believed designers must study hues in different situations to build sensitivity |
| **Joseph Albers** | Itten's student, later at Black Mountain College and Yale; *Interactions of Color* (1963). Studied how hues change next to others; argued perception is subjective — fifty students asked to picture red would name fifty different reds |

- **Both Itten and Albers held** that recognizing successful color pairings is a learnable skill improved by practice.

## 9. Choosing a Color Palette

- **Color palette** — limited set of colors chosen for a composition or set of deliverables; usually includes **dominant** tones (used most), **subordinate** tones, and **accent** colors (used sparingly).
- **Dominant / Subordinate / Accent system** — a three-color method: pick a dominant hue (matches message or audience), choose a subordinate to complement or contrast, then pick an accent (tint/shade of one of the others, a neutral, or a wholly new hue). Test by swapping subordinate or altering saturation.
- **Match palette to client identity** — saturated palettes feel lively; muted/neutral feel subdued. A bank wants secure/conservative tones (less saturated dominant, brighter accent); a financial firm rarely uses fun/frivolous colors. Younger, nimble brands take risks (Snapchat's bright/fluorescent yellow; Twitter's light blue).
- **Starting in black and white** — useful when color choice feels overwhelming; lets you set placement and hierarchy first, then add one or two colors at a time.
- **Palette types**:

| Type | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **One-color palettes** | Cheaper to print; develop and maintain hierarchy easily; black-and-white offers optimum contrast and stands out in busy environments |
| **Two-color pairings** | Allow variation while staying simple; identify dominant and subordinate; consider paper/screen color (not all colors show on non-white backgrounds) |
| **Image-based palettes** | Sample tones from a photograph or fine-art reference; visual tie-in between imagery and rest of design |

- **Custom colors** — almost always work better than default software swatches; create a custom swatch and test more/less intense versions.
- **Online tools** — Adobe Kuler (color.adobe.com), Adobe Capture (free app, iPhone/Android), Color Scheme Designer (paletton.com); Pantone swatch books for print.
- **Printing black** — two methods in CMYK:
  - Move slider to **K (key)** alone — for one-color jobs and black text; large areas can look mottled because paper shows through.
  - **Rich black** — new swatch at 60% cyan, 60% magenta, 40% yellow, 100% black; pleasing solid black for large areas.

## 10. Color, Contrast, and Hierarchy

- **Color repetition** — repeating one or more hues develops dynamic visual space, creates unity, and connects elements. Works best when the palette is limited; too many repeated colors look disorganized.
- **Color and hierarchy** — color can override size: small bright/light shapes can appear larger than bigger muted ones. Color must support intended hierarchy or it disrupts the message. Bright/white/intense colors stand out; dull/dark colors recede.
- **Contrast and color** — contrast between elements determines whether they remain distinct. Two tones of yellow with only slight value difference blend or look unintentional. Least legible combinations: red on blue, orange on blue, yellow on orange, green on orange — these appear to vibrate. With dark backgrounds use lighter/brighter colors on top, and vice versa.
- **Too much contrast can also confuse** — contrast must support, not fight, the inherent hierarchy of the text.

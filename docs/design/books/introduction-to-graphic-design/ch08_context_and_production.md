## 1. Production Basics

- **Production basics** — the materials and processes that transform ideas and software files into finished design projects. First-year students rarely send files to a commercial printer, but they do create finished comps for critique and portfolio.
- **Scope** — types of printing, common binding and finishing methods, and the tools needed for mock-ups. Students working only on screen-based assignments may skip ahead to presentation and feedback.

## 2. The Comp

- **Comp** — abbreviation of *comprehensive*; a visual layout of a design solution shown in digital form or as a 2D/3D mock-up. In professional practice, comps are shown to clients before the final design goes into production; the client may accept as-is or request revisions.
- **Student vs. professional use** — for students, producing a comp is usually the last step before completing the assignment; comps are shown in the same format as the final (printed spreads, 3D packaging, on-screen apps).
- **Photo mock-up** — for complicated or oversized projects, a digital mock-up where the layout is dropped into a photograph of an actual environment (e.g., poster on a bus stop, signage on a building exterior) is often acceptable.

## 3. Types of Printing

- **Two main printing systems** — most projects are printed using **digital printing** (ink-jet and laser) or **offset lithography**. Cost, quantity, and quality determine which to use.
- **Digital vs. offset comparison**:

| Method | How it works | Best use | Trade-offs |
|--------|--------------|----------|------------|
| **Ink-jet printing** | Sprays wet ink onto specially coated papers | Large-format pieces (signage, banners), photography, posters, image-dominant work | Bright fairly true colors; relatively slow, so not ideal for many copies |
| **Laser printing** | Adheres dry-pigment ink to paper using heat | Comps, multipage student projects (better-quality units offer duplexing/booklet printing); fast, inexpensive | Limited paper sizes (commonly 8.5×11 and 11×17 in the US); colors tend to be more muted/darker than on-screen; not for large format |
| **Offset lithography** | Inked image transfers from a metal CMYK plate to a rubber blanket and then to paper | Professional jobs needing more than 2,000 copies or very high-quality prints; supports custom colors, metallic and fluorescent inks, finishes | Plate-separation overhead makes it expensive for school projects |

- **CMYK separation** — for offset, the file is separated into cyan, magenta, yellow, and black; each color gets its own light-sensitive metal plate.
- **Embossing / debossing** — printing process using a special plate to create raised or recessed images on paper or another substrate. Specialty finishes typically cost more.
- **Duplex printing** — another word for double-sided printing.

## 4. Image Resolution and File Preparation

- **Resolution depends on output** — billboards, websites, annual reports, and books all require different resolutions. Larger formats need bigger images.
- **Standard targets** — screen resolution is **72 dpi**; most print production projects need **300 dpi at 100% of the intended size**.
- **Pixilation** — occurs when an image is too low quality or has been enlarged until it becomes blurry or individual pixels are visible; photographs, scans, and other bitmap images are subject to pixilation.
- **File formats for print**:
  - **TIFF** (tagged image file format) — preferred for Photoshop bitmap imagery (loss-less).
  - **EPS** (encapsulated post script) or **PDF** — for vector files (Illustrator line art, illustrations, logotypes); infinitely scalable.
  - **PSD** — acceptable when a transparent background is required; merge all layers except the transparent background.
  - Final imagery should be saved in CMYK and flattened before placement into layout programs like InDesign, unless it has a transparent background.
- **Printing PDFs** — PDFs are usually printed via Adobe Acrobat rather than the original software. They embed font information so non-system fonts don't need to be installed on the printing computer; color profiles between Acrobat and other programs may differ, so check with the service provider when exact color matching is critical.
- **Packaging InDesign files** — InDesign files only *link* to images and fonts. Use **File > Package** to collect the layout, linked images, graphics, and fonts into a single folder before moving the project to another computer or sending to print.
- **Saving PDFs**:

| Source software | Command |
|-----------------|---------|
| Illustrator | File > Save As |
| Photoshop | File > Save As |
| Microsoft Word | File > Save As |
| InDesign | File > Adobe PDF Presets, then choose quality |

- **Printing checklist** — verify printer/lab specs; confirm all files are properly linked (in InDesign, Illustrator, Quark Express); flatten layers before printing but save an editable version; check color profiles match the printer's; confirm imagery is not pixilated, stretched, or distorted; verify all fonts are installed (not needed if printing from PDF); choose single- or double-sided.

## 5. Binding

- **Binding** — physically collecting and assembling pages into a book, brochure, or other multipage form using glue, thread/string, wire, or plastic combs/spirals. Each technique produces a unique appearance and difficulty level.
- **Comparison of binding methods**:

| Method | How it works | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| **Perfect binding** (also adhesive binding) | Same-size pages stacked, adhesive applied along one edge; heavy-weight cover added | Used widely by publishing for paperbacks and magazines; uniform rectangular look; opens flat or nearly flat; needs at least ~20 sheets to adhere properly |
| **Saddle stitch** | Both sides of a spread printed on the same sheet; sheets gathered, folded in half, stapled along the fold | Cheap, doable by hand with long-arm staplers (or manually with awl/needle); good for student projects |
| **Spiral binding** | Holes punched along the edge; wire or plastic combs/loops threaded through | Lies completely flat when open; quality is not always high; imagery may look odd where holes are punched |
| **Metal spiral** | Common variant of wire binding | Inexpensive; small manual machines widely available |
| **Loop binding** | Wire binding variant where a metal loop sticks out along the spine | — |
| **Sewn bindings** | Pages collected, folded in half, thread driven through the fold | Used for textbooks and art books; an awl can pre-punch holes; result resembles the saddle-stitched format |

- **Common punching patterns** — square, rectangular, and round, depending on the binding machine.

## 6. Folds

- **Folds vs. binding** — brochures and shorter deliverables use folds rather than bindings. Each fold can be produced by hand using a ruler and a bone fold.
- **Scoring** (also called creasing) — compresses paper along a fold line; required for card stock and heavy-weight paper. Drag the pointed end of a bone fold along the line using a ruler edge, then fold along that mark.
- **Common fold styles**:

| Fold | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Four-page simple fold** | Single fold in the middle of a sheet → 4 panels |
| **Four-page short fold** | Asymmetric placement of the fold → two larger and two smaller panels |
| **Accordion fold** | Paper folded at least twice, each fold bending opposite the previous; equal panels if intervals are regular |
| **Barrel fold** | One or two outer edges folded inward toward the center; flaps inside used as panels |
| **French fold** | Folded first horizontally, then vertically; smaller trim size, fully opens to one large panel |

## 7. Trimming and Mounting

- **Trimming** — cutting edges off a layout or cropping it. Both printed and handmade projects are trimmed before mounting or critique.
- **Trimmed flush** — when white or colored edges are trimmed off so the edge of the page matches the edge of the design. **Crop marks** (small dashes in the corners) indicate where to make cuts when color or imagery doesn't reach the edge.
- **Tools** — a metal ruler with a cork back (cork prevents slipping; metal gives a clean cutting edge); cut only on a self-healing surface; replace dull X-Acto/matt-knife blades, especially when cutting board or Foamcore. Make multiple cuts along the length rather than forcing the blade through thick material.
- **Mounting flush** — to avoid a border, mount before trimming so no extra paper hangs over. Mount the design with adhesive, then trim with a ruler and X-Acto.
- **Mounting with a border** — for assignments requiring a black or white border, graphic design work is mounted directly onto matt board, illustration board, or Foamcore (no beveled window-frame). Generally a border of at least **1.5 inches** looks better than a smaller one. Use a T-square and triangle ruler for square cuts.

## 8. Glues, Adhesives, and Tools

- **Glue choice matters** — using the wrong adhesive can destroy paper or fail to last. The book describes the trade-offs of common options.
- **Adhesive comparison**:

| Adhesive | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| **Rubber cement** | Excellent short-term, non-water-based; good for lightweight paper | Toxic fumes (use in ventilated area); doesn't last indefinitely |
| **Spray mount** | Non-water-based; works well for large-scale projects, esp. mounting posters to Foamcore | Tricky on small areas; requires spray booth/well-ventilated room; sticky residue; "repositionable" version is too weak |
| **Glue stick** | Quick, nontoxic; no ventilation needed; cheap | Less strong than other adhesives; too much glue can buckle lightweight paper |
| **Double-sided tape** | Quick and easy; won't dry out or discolor (good brands) | Adhesion only where the tape is — not good for sticking large areas; common brands not strong enough for heavy paper to board/Foamcore |
| **PVA glue** (polyvinyl acetate / wood glue / white glue) | Excellent adhesion, dries clear, can be brushed on; nontoxic, ventilation not required | Slow drying; can warp/buckle paper if too much applied; multiple layers needed for book spines |
| **StudioTac / paper mounting adhesive** | Non-water-based sheets/rolls cut to exact size; pressure-activated; nontoxic; no mess | Expensive; limited sizes |

- **Spray booths and rooms** — adhesive sprays and rubber cement should only be used in well-ventilated areas or spray booths; freestanding semi-enclosed structures filter air or send it to an exhaust system.
- **Tools used in simple hand binding** — glue stick, double-sided tape, awl, X-Acto or matt knife, T-Square, bone fold, ruler, spray mount, PVA glue, Studio Tack / paper mounting adhesive, self-healing cutting surface.

## 9. Design as a Working Process

- **Design is creative and practical** — professional designers also handle project management: time management, file naming, saving, backing up, binding, and finishing.
- **Incremental goals and time allotment** — instructors often break assignments into specific tasks with target completion dates. Procrastination rarely yields positive results — last-minute designs look rushed and incomplete. Note when each part is due as the assignment is reviewed.
- **File naming and saving** — avoid the term *final* when naming files; use a rational numbered system based on project name and version (e.g., `Intro_Project_1_1.1.psd`, `Intro_Project_1_1.2.psd`). Save every few minutes; create separate folders per class and per project. Use **Save As** to preserve earlier versions; better to have more well-organized versions than fewer.
- **Save-as** — file-menu command that copies the current document and saves a new version in a different location or under a different name.
- **Backing up work** — every design student should own at least two portable hard drives (one for transport, one as backup). On Mac, Time Machine reminds when to back up. Cloud services like Google Drive and Dropbox provide limited free storage; large files (e.g., video) need hard drives or paid online storage. Set reminders; back up at the end of every semester and keep final versions for portfolio use.

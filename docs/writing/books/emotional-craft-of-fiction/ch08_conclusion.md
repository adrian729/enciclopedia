## 1. Trusting Your Own Feelings

- **Read vs. experience** — readers can read a novel or *experience* it: feel immersed, rapt, caught up in the spell as if what's happening to characters is happening to them. The experience is actually happening inside the reader's own mind and heart
- **Author must be immersed first** — to produce that effect, the author must be open, authentic, and free of fear, unrestrained by genre requirements, fan or critic expectations, or literary tribe — and willing to take positions and declare truths
- **Fiction persuades** — like a courtroom argument, the best story wins the judge and jury. Reach readers' hearts and they'll not only immerse but believe what the story says
- **Instincts of a true novelist** — they sense conflict, see hidden surprises, peer into strangers, mine family weirdness without shame, trust their gut about what is *right* in a story, and aren't afraid to start over when faking it
- **Story is personal** — personality, preferences, politics, and gender shape what story means; female authors may render female protagonists as wounded and journeying toward healing, male authors may give male protagonists hubris that needs humbling. No idea is wrong, just less effective when it isn't entirely the writer's own
- **Confidence over apology** — Virginia Woolf and Hemingway didn't blog about staying inspired or pitching anxiety; they wrote in the confidence that their stories were necessary. Lack of confidence shows in timid pages and low effect
- **Fiction's reach** — fiction engages imagination on a deeper level, stirs minds and hearts, and brings about change in ways few art forms can. Historical fiction, sagas, science fiction, crime, and coming-of-age have all served higher purposes than entertainment
- **Authors named for tackling their times** — Austen, Dickens, Hardy, James, Wharton, Fitzgerald, Hemingway, Hammett, Dreiser, Doctorow, Heller, Powell, Pynchon
- **Authors named for speculation as statement on human nature** — Verne, Huxley, Tolkien, Golding, Bradbury, Burgess, Dick, Vonnegut, Atwood, King
- **Authors named for the personal made universal** — Lawrence, McCullers, Tarkington, Conrad, Wright, Penn Warren, Jones, Fowles, Irving, McCarthy
- **Own your fanaticism** — protagonists, antagonists, *and writers* are fanatics. Own the side of yourself that could kill, that loves irrationally, that wears masks — that is what storytellers do
- **Human contradiction is the raw material** — we are irrational, careless, harmful, and quixotic, *and* grounded in decency, justice, and what is right. Flawed but good. That contradiction supplies everything needed to tell stories of human authenticity
- **Faith, not more credentials** — what the writer needs beyond craft, ideas, and support is *faith* in their mandate. Years, manuscripts, acceptance, stars, and movie deals are not prerequisites; humanity and goodness matter more
- **Writing as living** — writing a novel is itself an emotional journey akin to falling in love, hating, separating, reconciling, and arriving at deep abiding love. To write well, one must live fully and generously; emotional craft is ultimately *the gracious gift of your own loving heart*

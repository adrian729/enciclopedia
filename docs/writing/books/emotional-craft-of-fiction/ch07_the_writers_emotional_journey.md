## 1. The Writer Behind the Page

- **Disconnect between life and page** — writers often have astonishing personal stories yet produce manuscripts that feel small, or, in the opposite failure mode, manuscripts with high concepts and heroic characters that feel anything but genuine
- **Root cause is unworthiness** — many fiction writers do not feel worthy of their calling and assume great storytelling is reserved for native geniuses or prizewinners. Maass calls this baloney; what successful storytellers actually have is the *confidence* to pull off improbable stunts on the page
- **Cynicism shows through** — when writers approach craft from disappointment, anger, or paranoia about timing and connections, the prose feels self-conscious, defiant, and desperate. Cynical writing tries too hard
- **Spirit infects pages** — the spirit you bring to the writing desk either oppresses or excites; characters either inspire or leave us indifferent. *How you feel inside is how we will feel in reading.* Pages reveal the writer's inner self the way a handshake reveals a stranger's
- **Largeness of spirit is a practice** — generosity, faith in humanity, and a positive outlook are not innate gifts but choices cultivated through repetition

## 2. Positive Spirit

- **Stories send signals** — protagonists' behavior, speech, thought, and feeling set readers' expectations and orient them to the tale
- **Pathos vs. compassion** — *pathos* says *poor me!* and is rooted in despair and self-pity; *compassion* says *poor you!* and is rooted in hope. The same plot can transmit either, burdening readers with worry or inspiring them to believe
- **Negative protagonists exhaust readers** — too much negativity (blame, self-doubt, agonizing, waiting for lucky breaks) kills involvement. Positive people, by contrast, make the best of things, accept setbacks, forgive, persist, and stay interested even when negative things happen
- **Positive ≠ carefree** — Maass is not against despair, low points, or crushing plot turns. Characters still grapple and struggle; the question is whether their underlying spirit lifts the reader or weighs them down
- **Example — Ben H. Winters's *The Last Policeman*** — Detective Hank Palace investigates a suspicious suicide in a world six months from extinction by asteroid. He has every reason not to care, yet truth still matters to him. Maass calls this positivity: *some things are worth fighting for right up to the end*

> **Emotional Mastery 30: Positivity on the Page** — pick a low point for the protagonist, detail what makes its misery unique, then add perspective (why is it good to feel this bad? what truth is affirmed?), a vision of a better day ("a better day is coming, but that is not this day"), and an action that is comforting, creative, generous, or large; turn the writer's own mood around (breathe, run, affirm); blast past safe writing and surprise yourself; in the working scene, turn the protagonist's mood from helpless/trapped to capable/challenged, let him appreciate something, change something, shrug off setbacks, or refuse to take wins for granted.

## 3. The Emotional Mirror

- **Characters are the writer** — they spring from your makeup, experience, wants, fears, and beliefs; their conflicts, relationships, emotions, and values are rooted in yours
- **Self-avoidance limits characters** — *know thyself* is ancient, but humans hide, lie, project, repress, rationalize, and wear masks. Refusing to face yourself means not fully facing your characters
- **Inauthentic characters are kept boxed in** — stereotypes and cardboard cutouts result when authors keep characters contained to avoid feeling confused, embarrassed, defensive, inadequate, squirmy, or sad. Real characters take root when the writer gets real with themselves
- **Dostoyevsky** — *Lying to ourselves is more deeply ingrained than lying to others.* The remedy: ask the characters to help
- **Fiction is an *emotional mirror*** — it reflects the writer; what characters can reveal exceeds what the writer can introspect alone. Their freedom to speak is the writer's freedom to grow

> **Emotional Mastery 31: The Emotional Mirror** — imagine sitting alone with the protagonist in a quiet room and ask: tell me something true about myself; what are you dying to do that I'm not letting you?; what's your worst fear about what I'll put you through?; what am I missing about other characters (who has a secret, who's secretly working against you, who's better than they appear?); what do you want to say, confess, or forgive but haven't?; what is this story really about — to you?

## 4. Decency and Goodness in the Story World

- **The world sends signals too** — readers experience the setting both through the protagonist and as a community in their own right; many manuscripts feel like worlds where authority is malign, families are toxic, and friends ultimately can't help
- **People are basically good** — Maass's anecdote of Hurricane Sandy and the West Village blackout: ordinarily averted strangers shared stories, exchanged cards, asked after each other's safety. After 9/11 the same thing happened. *Innate goodness shines* the darker the times
- **Even dystopias have safe corners** — the darker the environment, the more goodness can stand out. Small towns can be small-minded, but they can also be warm and supportive — a function of the people in them
- **Example — John Grisham's *A Painted House*** — autobiographical novel set in 1952 Arkansas cotton country, a coming-of-age story for seven-year-old Luke Chandler whose family hires Mexican migrants and "hill people" at harvest. Grisham's nostalgic glow is built not just on biscuits and Cardinals games but on people: shopkeeper Pearl Watson sneaks Luke a forbidden Tootsie Roll on credit and welcomes a Mexican worker into her store with quiet decency. The world is anchored in *fundamental decorum and goodwill*
- **Comparable works** — the social-strata romance and tragedy in Grisham echo Steinbeck's *Of Mice and Men* and Robert Goolrick's *Heading Out to Wonderful*

> **Emotional Mastery 32: Decency and Goodness** — early in the story, give readers something nice about people of this time and place; introduce a generous, bighearted, or wise character early; have someone reach out to help someone else (food helps); set a scene at the equivalent of a Sunday picnic, country fair, or corner bar; identify the highest values of the story world and dramatize them.

## 5. Magnanimous Writing

- **What company are you?** — the question is not just the protagonist's temperament but the writer's own. Many manuscript protagonists are sour, snarky, bemused, self-pitying, or dull; spending four hundred pages with such people is a drag, even if endings are redemptive
- **Yearning, struggle, change still required** — the fix is not relentlessly chipper characters; it is the *spirit* in which yearning and need are rendered, one that invites readers in rather than makes them run
- **The writer's best self is *magnanimous*** — generous, curious, compassionate, insightful, large; humorous, ironic, tolerant, far-seeing, wise. To this self, story is not just a plot to wrestle but a celebration of human endurance, a forgiveness of our sins, a bountiful grace to bestow, a freedom to roam, a greathearted kindness, and a high-minded call to our better natures
- **You are what you write** — when the writer is cranky, the novel is cranky; when the writer shines, so does the novel. Magnanimity is a daily practice, not a personality trait

> **Emotional Mastery 33: Magnanimous Writing** — at any point in the story, find what's funny, ironic, or peculiar; at a point of pain, find what's beautiful or what the POV character can be grateful for; identify what's wonderful about the story world; give the protagonist a gift, a dream, or freedom; in pressure scenes, find what's excellent or exciting about the challenge; spot potential in a secondary character that others miss; find characters who can rise above, forgive, show humility, sacrifice, or admit wrong; on any page, identify someone who can act more noble, generous, or principled, and let the writer's own enjoyment shine through.

## 6. The Current: Hope

- **The current** — the deeper, seemingly mystical force that sweeps readers into a novel even when plot is on hold. It is not plot, scene dynamics, micro-tension, inner journey, setting, voice, or theme. Readers can sense it but can't name it
- **The current is hope** — a longing or ache for something unnamed and unobtainable that the writer causes readers to believe is real and possible. Hope is hard to deliberately stir, doesn't drive characters into action, and isn't really *part of the story* — it underlies it
- **Hope explains familiar puzzles** — why thrillers can pile on stakes without adding thrill, why beautifully rendered literary fiction can feel ice cold, why some dark mysteries depress while others nearly identical have us cheering
- **Hope in the horror cliché** — when Johnny pulls Susie into the creepy cabin, our anxiety isn't only Johnny's stupidity. It's *Susie's* voice — *Can't we go back to town?* — that hopes Johnny will make the right call. Hope is *Please, please don't die!* alongside *Look out!*
- **Where hope lives in fiction** — settings that present a *destiny* and a possibility of peace; characters who look inward with interest and outward with curiosity; a need to seek the good rather than avoid the bad; a rising curve of yearning rather than a series of setbacks; characters whose hearts are more generous than ours
- **Tool is the writer** — there is no technique to apply; the writer is the embodiment of hope and lets it flow into characters, outlook, and world
- **Wretched starts amplify hope** — characters can begin in utter hopelessness or as miserable human beings, which makes redemption more amazing. But care for a wretch is not automatic — readers need *signs* such a character is worth redeeming and that redemption will arrive
- **Example — Fredrik Backman's *A Man Called Ove*** — Ove is a 59-year-old grump emptied by his wife's death who repeatedly attempts suicide, each try interrupted by people needing his help. Hope enters not through Ove but through *the actions of others*: a pregnant Iranian neighbor insists he take in a stray cat; at his wife's grave, the cat reaches out and rests its head in his hand. *It's the selflessness of others that gets to us*
- **Example — Bryce Courtenay's *The Power of One*** — set in 1930s–40s South Africa, English-speaking Peekay is tortured at an Afrikaans boarding school. Rail worker and welterweight Hoppie Groenwald teaches him boxing under the maxim *first with your head, then with the heart* and demonstrates it by beating a much larger Jackhammer Smit in thirteen rounds. Watching small triumph over big, Peekay thinks: *I had hope. I was not powerless.* The kindness of strangers stirs hope
- **Heart is in the author** — *Heart is a quality inherent not in a manuscript but in its author. It is not a skill but a spirit.* Spirit can be cultivated and seeps into daily story choices

> **Emotional Mastery 34: Infusing Hope** — for fear stories, raise three hopes that the worst won't happen and three reasons survival matters more (make them personal); for romances, find three ways to make union matter more, beyond just the obstacles keeping the lovers apart; for principle stories (e.g., justice), name what the protagonist hopes for that no plot means can deliver and find three ways to elevate it over the plot goal; for journeys of healing, find three new ways to manifest the warmth remaining in a wounded heart; in any story, find people who can deliver a gift, forgive the unforgivable, humble themselves, know the right thing to say, change a life, grasp greater meaning, or die with grace — and add them.

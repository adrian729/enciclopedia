## 1. Engineering Emotion on Purpose

- **Big emotions are engineered by circumstances** — tears, rage, and terror don't arrive randomly; they show up when conditions line up (graduations, betrayals, a 3 a.m. pounding on the door). Knowing the conditions means a novelist can stage them deliberately
- **Attendant feelings amplify the primary feeling** — sorrow is most acute when happiness is ending, farewell is sharpened by gratitude, rage is worsened by helplessness, fear peaks in anticipation of the blow rather than at impact
- **Manipulation is the job, not a sin** — news, ads, politicians, and preachers all stir feelings; fiction may legitimately do the same. A writer can hope a story has emotional effect or craft it so the effect is guaranteed

## 2. High Moments

- **High moments** — story events that move hearts and stay remembered. Five durable types: forgiveness, sacrifice, betrayal, moral dilemma, and death
- **Forgiveness** — its emotional power lies in the change it forces inside the *forgiver*, not the wrongdoer. In Jason F. Wright's *The Wednesday Letters*, Jack and Laurel forgive Laurel's rapist and even welcome him as their pastor; Jack's parole-hearing letter dramatizes the shift from wanting to hate to recognizing the forgiver is the one who must change. Compare *The Kite Runner* (Hosseini), *China Dolls* (See), *Night Road* (Hannah), *Cry, the Beloved Country* (Paton)
- **Sacrifice — size doesn't matter, need does** — what makes sacrifice moving is not how big it is but how needed. In Richard Paul Evans's *The Walk*, waitress Ally kneads broken Alan Christoffersen's sore feet at a roadside diner (a scene Maass parallels to Mary washing Jesus's feet, Luke 7:38) and gives him gratitude in place of bitterness — a small sacrifice that lands as hard as Sydney Carton's death in *A Tale of Two Cities* or Robert Jordan's in *For Whom the Bell Tolls*
- **Betrayal — who and how, not what** — the act itself isn't what wounds; it's that the betrayer is someone trusted and the betrayal lands in the cruelest possible way. In Kristan Higgins's *Too Good to Be True*, Grace's fiancé Andrew leaves her for her adored younger sister Natalie — the "birthday present" sister — making the Judas kiss feel foul precisely because of the bond it breaks
- **Moral dilemma** — a choice between two outcomes whose consequences are equally excellent or equally excruciating, not just for the protagonist but for others. In Liane Moriarty's *The Husband's Secret*, Cecilia Fitzpatrick discovers her husband John-Paul murdered teenager Janie Crowley decades earlier; turning him in is legally right but destroys her daughters' father — and Moriarty piles up reasons on both sides until inaction itself triggers tragedy
- **Dilemmas work when stakes are high and personal** — law versus love, truth versus protection, honesty versus kindness. The morally right choice must be offset by an equally compelling personal one. When the character cannot win, the reader does
- **Death — make living beautiful first** — sorrow (not mere sadness) requires that the absent person be missed, which requires the reader loved being with them while alive. In John Green's *The Fault in Our Stars*, Hazel Grace and Augustus Waters are made impossibly vivid — sardonic, witty, in love — so their dying wrecks readers. To make death poignant, make life with that character the best thing about being alive

> **Emotional Mastery 22: Pushing High Moments Higher** — make the forgiver someone for whom this wrong is unforgivable and someone who must change more than anyone else. For sacrifice, work the recipient — make their need tremendous and tear down other avenues of help. For betrayal, make the betrayer central to the protagonist and surface the betrayal in the worst possible way. For dilemma, build both options until choice is impossible. For death, make us love the dying character more; if death pervades the story, fill it with joy and love.

## 3. Symbols

- **Symbols** — objects, gestures, places, or words that gather meaning through use. Not inherent — meaning is made, and anything can be made into one
- **Setup through pattern** — most symbols sneak up: an object recurs without being noticed at first, then strikes a recognizable note. Without context (as with diamond rings), a symbol has no effect
- **Big symbolic actions are most potent out of the blue** — unlike objects, which sneak up through pattern, a single grand symbolic action lands hardest when it arrives without setup. In KC McKinnon's *Candles on Bay Street*, half of Fort Kent walks down Bay Street holding candles to say goodbye to dying Dee Dee Michaud the night she has chosen to die — candles she sold them or taught them to make. "Cheesy" stops mattering when it works
- **Symbolic words don't sneak — a spotlight shines on them** — they land sharpest in ironic reversal: a term of affection turned into a slur, or a phrase coined on the fly and used against itself. In Graeme Simsion's *The Rosie Project*, Don Tillman tags Rosie "the most beautiful woman in the world" and seconds later tells her, "I hadn't really noticed" — a self-destructive irony that makes the reader cringe and root for him at once
- **Symbols are not limited to flags, roses, or rings** — anything meaningful to a character can reverse, recur, or be deployed to gain weight. Characters themselves can embody an idea or aspect of human nature

> **Emotional Mastery 23: Symbols** — burn down a place of heart-significance and rebuild it; damage and repair a key relationship; lose and refind a memory-laden object; reuse a special word in many ways; plant an object from the climactic scene earlier in the manuscript to build its symbolic value; enhance the gesture, place, or words around forgiveness by taking away what will later be given back; set up earlier the most significant demonstration of love, sign of loss, warmest welcome, ugliest contempt, or biggest celebration; and pick or create a character who embodies the opposite of the protagonist's belief — overdo it.

## 4. Story Worlds and Emotional Language

- **Story worlds we don't want to leave** — the longing to linger isn't produced by cobblestones, eccentric townspeople, or pretty settings. It comes from the reader's feelings, generated by what's happening inside the world, not what's on its surface
- **Build hope, then threaten it** — a big day (or big story) becomes big through the buildup and release of tension. Hope-driven tension (something good we want) outperforms fear-driven tension (something bad we dread) and applies equally to thrillers and romances
- **Hope must have heart value** — abstract hopes (world peace) move no one; specific, human, achievable ones (winning a smile from Conchita at the Barcelona café *Desde el Corazón*) do. Love is the highest heart value; establish what and whom the protagonist loves to anchor reader hope
- **Goodness makes a place lovable** — readers want to stay where the hero is liked, accepted, understood, and supported, and where fairness and generosity reign — or will reign once the protagonist wins. Hostile environments push readers out
- **Gems versus necklaces** — individual strong words are gems; arrangement is the necklace. Power comes from setting words in patterns: repetition, parallels, reversals, brevity, strong words at sentence-end, symmetry, contrast. Churchill's "victory at all costs, victory in spite of all terror, victory, however long and hard the road may be" is a diamond necklace; Pierce Brown's *Red Rising* uses the same rhetorical scaffolding (anastrophe, antithesis, chiasmus, litotes) to make miner Darrow's voice soar
- **Reordering syntax produces emotional response** — the rhythm itself, like a lullaby or backbeat, lulls readers into a receptive state. It is a free tool in the emotional toolbox

> **Emotional Mastery 24: Story Worlds We Don't Want to Leave** — decide whether the story's world is governed by goodness or hostility, then either name the protagonist's bedrock of goodness or show how she finds humor, comfort, or refuge inside a hostile place; create an early moment in which someone visibly cares for the protagonist (page two, if possible); bring in whom the protagonist loves as quickly as possible and show whether it is returned; let readers hold, smell, taste, or feel something non-human the protagonist loves, right away; and, apart from the plot goal, define a human, specific, achievable hope and signal it clearly.

> **Emotional Mastery 25: Emotional Language** — use word arrangement, not imagery, to mimic what is being described; take any long line of dialogue and make it eloquent (sermon, rant, whispered confession, poem); render an action through its effects (wreckage, reactions, long-term implications) in a montage; build a memory box of one character's images of another, in short bursts only; list everything the protagonist does *not* feel about something, then reverse it in one closing sentence; and write an "I am" list of metaphoric self-definitions for a character.

## 5. Change, Seasons, and Cascading Change

- **Change is the most powerful way to stir reader feeling** — it is universal, unavoidable, and individual. When characters change, readers are reminded of their own emotional earthquakes; what we feel for characters is really what we feel for ourselves
- **Resistance, not the change itself, produces the effect** — what changes in people is belief, behavior, or both, and the harder it is to make the change, the more emotional weight it carries when it arrives. Wrong belief and damaging behavior are traps because they are familiar; new ways require vulnerability, complexity, ambiguity, and risk
- **Squeeze the character between opposing modes** — yin/yang, helpless/reckless, Jeckyll/Hyde, pious/godless, righteous/resigned. Achieved change feels relieving, liberating, empowering — a turn from self-pity toward understanding
- **Change is the true ending of every story** — once a protagonist has transformed, the story is effectively over in a way plot resolution cannot match. In Patti Callahan Henry's *The Perfect Love Song*, hardened musician Jimmy Sullivan opens to Charlotte Carrington on a back porch when she observes that we "Forget the good parts because we are so busy forgetting the bad parts" — his heart opens "as if an earthquake had slipped the tectonic plates of his dismal childhood"
- **Seasons of the self** — characters need a sense of personal history and the phases inside it, not biographies but an awareness of *who I was* and *who I am becoming now*. Many manuscripts leave the protagonist's sense of self poorly defined; transitions between phases are felt by readers when they exist on the page
- **Crispin's arc as illustration** — in Avi's *Crispin: The Cross of Lead*, the thirteen-year-old peasant moves from believing his lowly place is fixed under "the great millstone" of time, to fearful self-direction on the run, to confronting the steward John Aycliffe with knowledge of his own birth, to a "newfound soul" that "lived in freedom." It is the inner transformation, not the plot's birth-secret, that compels
- **Newton's law for novelists: cascading change** — protagonists and other characters always change each other. Like Newton's cradle (*click-clack*), a moving protagonist transfers force on contact; every collision must produce reaction (stay, sway, topple, or be flattened). If characters don't change each other, the story is missing something
- **Cascading change in practice** — in Rosie Thomas's *The Illusionists*, magician Devil Wix changes everyone around him; Eliza alone changes him; the embittered dwarf Carlo Boldoni and the theatre sweeper Jakey are visibly altered by knowing Devil and, in a single drunken backstage exchange, by each other — a brief mutual nudge without a fix

> **Emotional Mastery 26: Making Change** — define the protagonist's new self at the end and work backward to detail the old self, including who validates that old self and what is good about being stuck; locate the first glimmer of the need to change as an event, not just an observation; add a mentor who sees the new self before the protagonist does and a devil who pulls toward the old; show what loss makes staying the same insupportable; stage the new self in the least expected, most temptation-rich moment; and enact the trigger of commitment symbolically.

> **Emotional Mastery 27: Seasons of the Self** — name the eras of the protagonist's life with their high and low moments and what was learned (or not); create a system by which the protagonist measures time and check the clock as the novel moves; at the opening, detail the phase being left behind and list the worrisome questions about the phase ahead; at any midpoint, stop and identify a forced change in self with what is good about it and what is good about staying the same; at the end, define the new self — what is familiar, what is entirely new, what one thing it now sees differently, and what one thing will never change.

> **Emotional Mastery 28: Cascading Change** — in the current scene, identify the two principal actors, the conflict, and the inner change in the POV character. Chart every character the protagonist interacts with and how each affects the protagonist's view of self, others, and the plot. Pick three of those characters and write down how each is changed in turn, with a consequence. Identify one unexpected ripple of the protagonist's overall journey and show it.

## 6. Feelings Without Names

- **Feelings without names** — emotional experiences that cannot be cleanly labeled (the fleeting beauty of life, the irony of it all, a nameless dread, the exquisite ache of inexpressible love). Evoking them is showing in its highest form; pure heart-to-heart connection
- **Pregnant pauses, "significant" looks, shrugs, snorts, and grunts don't work** — these devices are too pale to carry tacit feeling. Equally, evoking a feeling that has an obvious name produces no magic; wonder doesn't arise when the reader doesn't have to wonder
- **Project the inchoate onto something solid** — to convey what cannot be described, detail something concrete that fronts for the formless. The smaller and more specific the imagery, the more universal and expansive the unspoken feeling becomes
- **Light Between Oceans example** — in M.L. Stedman's novel, lighthouse keeper Tom Sherbourne, a Great War veteran, panics atop his lighthouse at "the nothingness of it all," then steadies himself by gripping concrete details: a loose hinge, brass fittings, oil, prisms, mercury. Stedman writes about hardware to write about shell-shock
- **All feelings have names — the art is making them seem too complex to quickly label** — "nameless" feelings are conflicting, layered, or complex, evoked through observation of something else rather than direct description

> **Emotional Mastery 29: Feelings Without Names** — find a point at which the protagonist is stuck, stymied, undecided, or overwhelmed; place something nearby for the protagonist to obsess over (positive, negative, or both) and detail its goods and bads, beauties and ugliness, with the focus kept off the protagonist; then ensure that nothing in the moment changes — discord unresolved, beauty overlooked, the protagonist helpless to do anything but notice.

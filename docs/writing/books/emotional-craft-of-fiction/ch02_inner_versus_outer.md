## 1. Three Modes of Emotional Response

- **Three primary paths** — there are three ways to produce an emotional response in readers, and all three are valid but each has pitfalls
- **Inner mode (telling)** — report what characters are feeling so effectively that readers feel it too, as though listening to a friend over coffee
- **Outer mode (showing)** — provoke in readers what characters may be feeling by implying their inner state through external action; like billiard balls, the action transfers impact without describing the feeling
- **Other mode** — cause readers to feel something the characters themselves do not feel; an emotional dialogue between author and reader in which the reader cannot escape the churn of her own feelings
- **Underlying cause matters** — to use any mode reliably, you have to know *why* it works when it works, so you can tell whether your current page will actually move a reader's heart

## 2. Outer Mode: Showing

- **Action is opportunity, not cause** — when outward actions stir us, it is not the actions that have stirred us but that we have stirred ourselves. The right question about showing is not *why* it works but *when* it works
- **When character emotion is highly painful, pull back** — Matthew Quick's *The Silver Linings Playbook* reports the actions of Pat Peoples, a mentally ill protagonist, without conveying his inner state. Humor and objective showing create a *safety zone* in which readers can process their own response to extreme emotional conditions
- **Subtext** — the unspoken emotional truth: a feeling we are not being told but is evident anyway. When the reader discerns it, it lands as a surprise
- **Subtext example** — in Gregory David Roberts's *The Mountain Shadow*, fugitive Lin abruptly turns on Naveen for mentioning his prison escape. Lin's feelings stay hidden, but the reader feels the jolt: *Watch out!* Small doses of subtext sum up to a tsunami-sized effect
- **Showing can present states of being, not just action** — Hemingway's "Now I Lay Me" opens with a sleepless, shell-shocked soldier listening to silkworms eat. Plain language and clunky compound sentences, no fancy words, but the situation is freighted with the terror of an out-of-body brush with death — and the reader feels compassion, quiet horror, helplessness
- **Hemingway's rule** — *Find what gave you the emotion; what the action was that gave you the excitement. Then write it down making it clear so the reader will see it too and have the same feeling as you had*
- **Emotional baggage is required** — Hemingway wrote about big stuff. Lying awake at night will not by itself cause readers to feel anything; only when a situation has heavy emotional baggage will readers pick it up and carry it
- **Imagery alone does not move readers** — in Anthony Doerr's *All the Light We Cannot See*, the museum scene is packed with sparkling imagery (gems, geology, curses), yet what causes feeling is a blind daughter's worry over her father, not the pretty images
- **Everyday realism rarely works** — pouring cereal or tidying the house yields little emotional mileage. When mundane action does work, it is because large emotions are already there

> **Emotional Mastery 1: Effective Showing** — pick a moment when the protagonist is moved or unsettled; list every emotion in the moment, obvious and hidden; brainstorm the biggest, most explosive or symbolic action or line he could deliver; add a setting detail only he would notice; then delete the emotions you listed and let actions and spoken words do the work.

## 3. Inner Mode: Telling

- **Cliché feelings fail** — *His guts twisted in fear*. *Her eyes shot daggers at him*. The daggers have dulled from overuse. Readers don't feel anything because they've read these lines too many times
- **Fresh, unexpected, but real** — what excites readers are feelings that are fresh and surprising, yet still real and true. Contrived feelings ring false and ignite nothing
- **The story situation is an emotional elephant** — at any moment a character has many possible feelings: surface and underneath, minimized, hidden, denied, embarrassing, vulnerable. Two characters in the same situation will never feel the same thing
- **Limited emotional menu is a common failure** — too many manuscripts offer only primary emotions because writers feel those are the safe, sellable ones. Others over-elaborate to the point of impenetrability. Both fail to connect
- **Go sideways to a secondary emotion** — in Bradbury's *Fahrenheit 451*, when Montag watches a woman about to set herself on fire among her books, a careless writer would lean on horror. Bradbury instead portrays Montag's *excitement* — the residual thrill of a fireman who once enjoyed his work. Because the chosen feeling is unexpected, the reader is forced to measure it against her own response
- **Du Maurier's deeper method** — in *My Cousin Rachel*, Philip's selfish misery on receiving news of his guardian's marriage is rendered through (1) an analogy (his memory of swimming to the safety of Ambrose's boat; sitting alone at Harrow), (2) alternative feelings he might feel (sympathy, gladness, happiness), (3) a moral judgment about the feeling (shame at his own lack of empathy), and (4) justification (it is the only possible feeling and here is why)
- **Length serves a purpose** — the deeper discourse creates a passage of perhaps fifteen seconds in which the reader's brain can process and arrive at her own emotional response, a response the author cannot know or dictate
- **Third-level emotions ignite primary feelings in the reader** — in workshops, Maass has writers ask a character "what else are you feeling?" twice to reach a third-level emotion, then develop it. Other readers report feeling the *primary* emotion (e.g., fear) the writer was originally trying to evoke. Tell readers what to feel and they won't; light an unexpected match and they ignite their own feelings

> **Emotional Mastery 2: Third-Level Emotions** — pick a moment where the protagonist feels strongly; ask "what else are you feeling?" twice to reach a third-level emotion; then work that layer four ways: (1) objectify it as an analogy, (2) make a moral judgment about it, (3) name a better alternative the character might feel, (4) justify why this is the only possible feeling. Add one setting detail only this character would see, and rewrite the moment around the third-level feeling.

## 4. Other Mode

- **Reader feeling does not come from characters** — readers' emotional experience comes from the readers themselves. Showing and telling are only part of the equation; other things on the page provoke the larger part
- **Control whether, not what** — you cannot control what each reader feels, but you can control whether they feel anything at all and how strongly. Treating emotional effect as accidental surrenders too much to chance
- **Readers read under their own influences** — temperament, history, biases, morality, likes, dislikes, peeves. They make judgments that don't agree with the author's
- **What entertainment consumers want, per research** — an experience, and a *positive* one: enjoyment, suspense, amusement, and *belief affirmation* (stories turning out as readers believe they should — more than just a happy ending)
- **Readers also want to be challenged** — entertainment delivers feelings of *competence* (mastering the story's challenges), *autonomy* (feeling unique in one's reading choices), and *relatedness* (adoration of characters, explained by *affective disposition theory*, treated in chapter five)
- **Chewing effect** — entertainment works best when it presents novelty, challenge, and aesthetic value, triggering *cognitive evaluation* — thinking, guessing, questioning, comparing the story to one's own experience. This chewing is medically necessary for human well-being
- **Memory pipeline** — what we read is first held in *sensory memory*, then moves to *working memory* (where the chewing happens), and the longer it stays there the more likely it is to be packaged by the *episode buffers* into *long-term memory*. Stories that make readers chew are remembered
- **Readers want to feel something about themselves** — not about your story. They want to anticipate, guess, judge, finish feeling competent, and believe they have lived the fictional experience
- **Emotional wallop comes from totality** — readers experience it when they are forced to study, question, and form an opinion about *themselves*, not just the story. They must take an emotional journey
- **Other mode is an orchestra, not a single technique** — a vast array of elements tuned together to create a soaring effect. The methods that follow in the rest of the book are how to achieve it

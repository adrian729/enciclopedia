## 1. Why Big Emotions Fall Flat

- **Life is felt, fiction often isn't** — we experience life as feelings, yet much fiction minimizes feeling or works from a limited, familiar emotional palette and leaves readers curiously empty
- **Non-emotional elements** — voice, closely written point of view, and imagery are necessary but not by themselves emotional. Voice grabs attention; intimate POV gives the "as if" illusion; deft imagery makes a place real. None of it makes readers feel
- **Big emotions resist direct evocation** — primary feelings like fear, rage, passion, glee, ecstasy, triumph, hope, astonishment, grief, humility, joy, or love often fall flat when aimed at directly. A dystopian world doesn't make readers stockpile guns; a body on page one doesn't shatter their security
- **Emotion comes by trickery, not force** — like stage magicians using misdirection, artful fiction surprises readers with their own feelings rather than pounding them with emotional hammer blows

## 2. Me-Centered Narration

- **Open hearts open hearts** — readers bond with characters whose hearts are first open to them. Most manuscripts under-disclose: a dry play-by-play of action, or a witty/ironic/snarky/perky tone that avoids true intimacy
- **Beyond first person's illusion** — first-person narration appears to deliver intimacy automatically but doesn't. Engagement happens when the reader cannot avoid self-reflection, not just when she's enjoying a character's patter
- **Strong feelings as invitation** — in life we bounce off the moods of others. In fiction, characters' strong feelings press readers to judge, sympathize, and engage on their own level
- **The "I Am" passage** — a declarative statement about self (Bob Dylan's "I Am a Man of Constant Sorrow," Helen Reddy's "I Am Woman," Simon and Garfunkel's "I Am a Rock," Lennon's "I Am the Walrus"). On the page its purpose is *not* to declare something true but to make readers suspect what's being forcefully asserted might be false
- **"I Am" example** — in Diane Setterfield's *Bellman & Black*, William Bellman gloats over his impending mourning emporium, surveying London as a future customer base. His self-congratulation breaks mid-sentence as a feather-stir of unease tells him he has forgotten something. The lesson: when characters celebrate themselves, tinge the celebration with apprehension. *Fireworks also cast shadows*
- **The "I Am Not" passage** — a declaration of what the character is not, which paradoxically makes readers wonder *Well then, who are you?* In Gillian Flynn's *Gone Girl*, Amy's journal entry mocks "dancing monkeys" husbands and insists *I am happy not to be in that club* — protesting too much, daring the reader to believe it
- **Goal: a moving target self** — me-centered narration goes beyond me-me-me to capture what makes *me* a topic for discussion, disagreement, and discovery. A complex character makes the reader feel complex too — readers chew on the character and on themselves

> **Emotional Mastery 3: Me-Centered Narration** — pick a scene mid-novel with the protagonist as POV; write what's happening with "me" (feelings about place, participants, events, self); track how feelings shift, what was overlooked, where the protagonist was wrong or dead right; identify a new feeling about the action and a new feeling about himself; use feelings to mislead the reader or to convey honest truth — *I am* to create uneasiness, *I am not* to create doubt; fold what works into the manuscript.

## 3. Emotional Scale

- **Big feelings via small details** — to evoke towering emotion, lay a foundation of small details preloaded with feeling. Details have the power of suggestion; suggestion draws feelings out rather than pounding readers
- **Big-via-small example** — in Stephen King's *Doctor Sleep*, Dan Torrance comforts dying ninety-one-year-old Charlie Hayes; rather than describing death, King flashes Charlie's life in tiny visual fragments (twin sons on swings, a wife in Belgian lace, a hubcap full of rain reflecting a red barn, Sinatra on a Motorola radio). Death becomes a poignant celebration of what was beautiful and light
- **Small emotions need to land big** — everyday feelings matter greatly to us but little to others. A monotonous action-reaction pattern (*churning*, the recycling of feelings readers have already felt) is easy to skim. The cure is again surprise
- **Reader as referee** — when characters struggle with feelings or feel the unexpected, readers run an instant inner debate (*Would I feel like that, too?*). That assessment is the goal
- **Small-via-contrast example** — in Kate Noble's *The Summer of You*, Lady Jane intends to sulk on the journey north but instead waves to a gnarled oak tree (the *Beast*, recast in childhood as a Fairy Lord's manor) and notices her perpetually-bickering brother Jason sitting suspiciously high in his seat too. Noble enlarges a roadside sight and underplays mutual pleasure to find feelings where readers aren't looking

> **Emotional Mastery 4: Small Details Equal Big Emotions** — at a moment of large primary feeling (e.g., greatest fear), list the small signs that something large is happening; the immediate repercussions; what the POV character will see or do differently from now on; insightful, compassionate, cutting, prescient, or ironic things she might say or think; a way of looking that scales the event down to manageable size. Craft a passage that conveys not the primary emotion but the experience — using details, unusual feelings, non-obvious observations, calm detachment, and wise compassion.

> **Emotional Mastery 5: Small Emotions Equal Large Experience** — pick a small but meaningful moment; discard the obvious POV feeling and write a contrasting feeling that's sharp, ironic, forceful, principled, or passionate; identify one implication and what the character must now do that's hard or unwelcome; render a self-judgment (condemned or exonerated, and how); ask whether this moment is heaven or hell, what's wonderful, what's unbearable, what the outrage and the wisdom are; craft a passage in which the character processes the big meaning of this small moment in a way unique to him and contrary to the reader's own feelings.

## 4. Higher Emotions and Moral Elevation

- **Higher emotions** — what readers feel when moved and inspired by a character's actions: the timeless virtues extolled in every religion. They make readers ponder, change, and become better people, and they make readers rate novels more highly
- **Moral elevation** — the scientifically demonstrated effect (Dr. Jonathan Haidt and others) by which reading about good people causes readers to be better themselves. Thomas Jefferson made the same argument in 1771 to Thomas Skipwith: virtuous fictional acts inspire readers to virtue. Maass adds that readers remember good acts more than bad — betrayal and cruelty fade; sacrifice, heroism, selflessness, and grace endure
- **What stirs the swelling chest** — reason prevailing over impulse, disgust replaced by insight, undeserved generosity, love given where rejection seems certain, sticking up for another, unasked-for help, humble apology, unexpected forgiveness, doors opened in welcome, truths spoken
- **Taking a stand example** — in R.J. Ellory's *A Quiet Belief in Angels*, Sheriff Haynes Dearing publicly faces down xenophobic neighbors at a 1939 birthday party in Augusta Falls, Georgia, defending the constitutional rights of German-American farmer Gunther Kruger and naming the ugly impulse (lynching) that's brewing
- **Even corrupt characters can rise** — in John Ajvide Lindqvist's *Let the Right One In*, the irredeemable pederast Håkan, soliciting child sex in a public bathroom, discovers the boy has had his teeth knocked out and instead hands him ten thousand kronor for new ones. The moment foreshadows Håkan's later self-sacrifice for the vampire Eli, and shows that even the worst example of humanity can still have a heart

> **Emotional Mastery 6: Good Deeds** — name one good thing your protagonist finds exceptionally hard to do; work backward to make it harder; find a way (perhaps after catharsis) for him to do it. Build a self-absorbed secondary character and engineer a selfless act for him. Reverse a low opinion late in the novel by showing hidden compassion. Build a justified grudge, then enact forgiveness. Choose a celebration in which a miserly character gives an unexpected gift. Find one way for the protagonist to self-sacrifice — to give up something or someone dear.

## 5. Moral Stakes

- **Personal stakes vs. moral stakes** — *personal stakes* attach plot problems to deeper personal meaning; *moral stakes* are what is lost when a protagonist doesn't act virtuously. Both matter
- **A good protagonist is the prerequisite** — whether the protagonist is a good person matters to readers; it's the first thing they look for and a prerequisite for involvement
- **Antiheroes work by trick** — dark protagonists we care about secretly signal that, however bad they seem, underneath they are good
- **Save the cat** — the screenwriter's technique of small early demonstrations of worthiness. Fiction extends the range: even miserable characters who show wry humor, a sharp eye, or a strong voice win readers over, since observant, alert, self-aware characters have the potential to become good
- **Moral struggle beats moral status** — *a character who is good is good; a character whom we want to be good is even better*. Readers must feel there is something to hope for; struggle is the key, and other characters can stand in for the reader by holding unearned faith
- **Arc completes with proof** — insight, understanding, or inner peace is fine, but the arc isn't done until change is visible: a selfish person turns selfless, an inward person looks outward, the transformed character puts good into the world
- **Demonstration example** — in Chris Bohjalian's *The Double Bind*, social worker Laurel Estabrook, herself a rape survivor, takes the soiled hand of a reeking, hallucinating transient on a Vermont street and walks him to the shelter, gets him fed and showered. Naïve neighbor Whit watches and is *awed* — the scene transmits inspiration through a witness so the reader can be inspired too
- **Moral choice example** — in Harper Lee's *Go Set a Watchman*, grown-up Jean Louise (Scout) discovers her father Atticus seated beside the segregationist Mr. O'Hanlon at a Maycomb County Citizens' Council. Her physical disgust (wet hand, shut stomach, trembling) is the moral heart of the novel: *his presence at the table with a man who spewed filth from his mouth … condoned*

> **Emotional Mastery 7: Moral Stakes** — identify a higher emotion to evoke (self-control, courage, perseverance, truthfulness, fairness, respect, generosity, forgiveness, service, sacrifice, discernment, integrity, humility, readiness, wisdom); choose a character who is the opposite and most needs to learn it; prepare the groundwork by reinforcing why the opposite way of being works; create three events forming an *anticipation phase* that build the necessity of change and reasons to resist it; finally, create the event that brings the better way of being home — the moment that stirs higher emotion in readers.

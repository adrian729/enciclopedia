## 1. The Wrong Question

- **Showing vs. telling debate** — fiction writers divide between those who prize *showing* (provoke emotion through action; don't spoon-feed feelings; capture vivid moments) and those who prize *telling* (go inside a character's mind and heart to render inner state in nuanced detail). Most authors lean one way and blend the two
- **The conventional question** — at writers' conferences the issue is framed as *What's the right balance between showing and telling?* Maass argues this is the wrong question
- **The better question** — not *how can I get across what characters are going through?* but *how can I get readers to go on emotional journeys of their own?* Showing and telling are part of the picture but not the most important part

## 2. The Reader's Emotional Journey

- **Readers are not living the story alongside characters** — they are having their own experience that is merely *occasioned* by what's on the page. The novelist isn't causing readers to feel as he does, or as his characters do, but inducing a unique emotional journey for each reader
- **Many story elements elicit feeling** — plot, setting, theme, mood, dialogue, and what characters themselves feel can all provoke a response. None of them is decisive on its own
- **No two readers read the same novel the same way** — Maass points to Goodreads comments as evidence that responses vary wildly. Readers don't adopt the author's outlook and outrage; they formulate their own
- **The author is a provocateur, not an author of feelings** — you curate characters' experiences and put them on display, but the exhibit's meaning is different in thousands of ways for thousands of museum visitors

## 3. What Readers Actually Remember

- **Most manuscripts and many novels provoke little feeling** — Maass observes that television commercials can stir more emotion in thirty seconds than many manuscripts do in three hundred pages
- **Classics endure because of impact, not art** — what makes them classics is not the artful storytelling but the experiences readers had while reading them. We remember not the art but the impact
- **Other effects fade fast** — hooks may hook, twists may intrigue, tension may turn pages, and prose may dazzle, but those effects fade as quickly as fireworks in a night sky
- **Characters feel real because of what we feel** — readers say they remember characters most, but characters become real to us only because of the feelings they cause. The characters aren't actually real; only the reader's own feelings are
- **Strong writing ≠ strong feelings** — beautiful prose may sparkle, but sparkling isn't a feeling. Even strong voices can fail to reach the heart, while poorly written novels can sometimes unsettle, anger, or move a reader to tears

## 4. Emotional Impact as Foundation

- **Emotional impact is fundamental, not an extra** — it is as basic to a novel's purpose and structure as its plot. The emotional craft of fiction underlies the creation of character arcs, plot turns, beginnings, midpoints, endings, and strong scenes. It is the basis of voice
- **Not a repackaging of old writing bromides** — emotional craft is a way of understanding what causes emotional effect on readers and deliberately using those methods, drawing on a tool always available: the writer's own feelings
- **Method, not magic or genius** — the approach does not depend on plot formulas or scene checklists, does not reduce writing to luck or accident, and does not require native genius. It starts with understanding how emotional impact is produced and then applying that in practice
- **Genre-agnostic** — mastering emotional craft will not change your story type, style, or intent. It applies whether the work is commercial or literary, plotted or pantsed, genre, mash-up, or trailblazing. All manuscripts need to make readers feel more

## 1. Meaning Over Resume

- **Resume vs. story** — a date-of-birth-and-occupation questionnaire reveals nothing important. Real questions probe meaning: what first made you happy, when you discovered life isn't fair, who first broke your heart, what you decided you had to grow up about, your biggest sacrifice. We are not the dry sum of our accomplishments; *we are stories*
- **Plot is outside, story is inside** — readers don't get the true story unless the writer puts both the *big meaning in small events* and *the overlooked implications of large plot turns* on the page
- **Meaning defined** — not a single gem of wisdom but the stream of insight, understanding, realization, and acceptance one continually gains from experience and that adds up to the subjective reality called *me*. *We are philosophers of I*

## 2. The Meaning of Everything

- **Dry information becomes alive** — every story requires explaining things (scientific, historical, occupational, local). Such material sits as *info dump* unless it means something to a POV character and that meaning is put on the page
- **Info-with-meaning example** — in M.R. Carey's *The Girl with All the Gifts*, dying Dr. Caldwell explains the *Ophiocordyceps unilateralis* fungus and the etiology of "hungries" to ten-year-old Melanie — the child she had intended to dissect. The science could be soporific; the meaning is poignant because Melanie is the only auditor providence sent in Caldwell's last hour to carry her trophies home
- **Spanning long time periods** — to readers a whole life can simply be long. Events alone can't hold decades together; *the constant of change itself* can. The recurring questions — *What do I want? Have I found it yet? How do things seem different to me now?* — unify a long story
- **Long-arc example** — Elena Ferrante's Neapolitan Novels (*The New York Times*'s "feminist potboilers") cover the lifelong friendship of Elena and Lina across four volumes. In *Those Who Leave and Those Who Stay*, Elena's contemplation of unfinished Naples skyscrapers and a vanished green pond is really about her, her friendship, the passage of time, and how things both change and don't — Ferrante writes around inner states using analogy, leading readers by implication to inescapable points of clarity
- **Making the mundane matter** — *there is poetry in doing the dishes if the metaphor is freshly made, a crux, a revelation, or a turning point*. Skilled authors crawl through a day and make every moment matter by commenting, pondering, and questioning everything as though every second is a diamond
- **Mundane-meaning example** — in Viet Thanh Nguyen's *The Sympathizer*, the unnamed Vietnamese spy-narrator inspects an outhouse on a Philippines movie set and meditates on toilet paper: *Paper was for writing things like this confession, not for mopping up excretions*. An unimaginable luxury becomes a meditation on confession itself, and on the worth of his own
- **Meaning lifts the mundane** — when a character is struck by the meaning of everyday things, the reader cannot help being struck by that character

> **Emotional Mastery 8: The Meaning of Everything** — for a small mid-manuscript event, ask: what is it symbolic of, what does it mean to the POV character personally, what general meaning could anyone see, how is the character's self-understanding changed even slightly. For dry information, ask who has the knowledge, how that character sees the facts as no one else does, what standard of judgment, what is good/bad/worrisome/reassuring/revealing, what she would change about the information or herself. For a long story, stop at four points and ask what the protagonist is now seeking, what she needs to find, why it's newly important, whether she's closer or further, what encourages and what says give up. Turn the notes into a passage and don't fear the slowdown.

## 3. Connecting the Inner and Outer Journey

- **The architecture metaphor** — the outer journey (plot) is the columns; the inner journey is the crosswise beams. The novel needs both, lightweight and strong, fastened together
- **Pin connections** — modern airport architecture replaces clunky shear connections (rigid plates and flanges) with a *pin connection*: a U-shaped clevis through which a small bolt passes, holding two structural pieces together. Thoughts and feelings work like pins between plot and inner life — the lightest pin holds the architecture together
- **Character arc** — also called *growth*, *inner journey*, or *transformative arc*: the change(s) a character goes through over a novel's length
- **Arc-as-jump failure** — most arcs are malnourished: an end-of-book event enacting a single change, an add-on bonus rather than an ever-present quest. Inner struggle is hard to write without making it a drag, so authors avoid it
- **Sense of movement is internal** — *a tidal pull, an emotional tug*. In life change is daily, even hourly; readers compare themselves to characters but there must be something to compare *to*
- **The fastening rule** — start with a plot event and go inside the protagonist to pinpoint what it means; or start with an inner moment and make something outward happen that symbolizes it. *Outer events lead inward. Inward struggles turn outward*
- **Superpower trick** — imagine the protagonist can project her inner state onto the world; what she feels inside makes things happen
- **Inner-and-outer example** — in L.A. Meyer's *Bloody Jack*, orphaned Mary in 1700s London eats a tavern stew on a held-horse penny and reflects four times *It's easier bein' a boy* — for tavern access, for being unbothered when alone, for being chosen for tasks, for owing nothing to anyone. She decides her name will be Jack. Outer survival and inner need fuse; readers buy both the gender switch and the naval adventure that follows

> **Emotional Mastery 9: Connecting the Inner and Outer Journey** — pick any plot event and ask what it means to the protagonist, what worry/hope/question/wonder it stirs, what it feels like to feel that feeling; create a metaphor for it and write it up. *Or* — pick an emotionally significant moment when the protagonist feels himself changing, shut off inner monologue, and find a way for him to show or speak the unfolding inner change so readers can't miss it.

## 4. Tension Versus Energy

- **Two psychological types** — human beings divide into those who *store tension* (turn inward; ponder, reflect, think, feel) and those who *store energy* (turn outward; act). Tea-and-conversation people vs. run-and-smack-a-ball people
- **Polarity** — Paul Rosenfels's term for this orientation. No type is better; each has best and worst selves. Writers should know which type they naturally write and get comfortable writing the other
- **Polarity swings as hidden plot** — when action follows an inner shift, action feels authentic; tension built up inside seeks release like steam from a pressure valve. Swings between polarities create a hidden inner trigger to action — they make plot developments happen when there is no plot, providing *emotional pace* independent of story circumstance
- **Examples of swings** — self-awareness turning into self-confidence; goodness hardening into righteousness; the feeling of safety transforming into the feeling of freedom; observing the world transforming into taking responsibility for it
- **Inconvenient characters** — characters are most interesting when they're inconvenient; writing fiction with emotional effect requires *feeling easy with uneasiness*. The reverse problem matters too — downshifting from action into reflection, backing away from outward conflict into inner turmoil
- **Tension-to-energy example** — in Alex Gordon's *Gideon*, Civil War veteran Joe Petrie freezes with palsied hand, unable to shoot the immolated witch Nicholas Blaine; Blaine taunts him as a coward who hid behind friends on the battlefield; Petrie breaks and runs through burning Gideon, ignoring cries for help. *Run, Joseph Petrie. It's what you do best* — frozen tension converts to fleeing energy with the recognition *I am a coward*
- **Energy-to-tension example** — in Natasha Pulley's *The Watchmaker of Filigree Street*, telegraphy clerk Thaniel Steepleton waits with his Whitehall office for a Fenian bomb at nine o'clock; the chimes pass, rain taps the panes, a cheer breaks. Thaniel takes his first deep breath in months: *someone had put a penny on his chest every hour since November, and now the weight of thousands of pennies had lifted at once*. Held energy releases into recognized tension
- **Pace as heartbeat** — polarity swings are steps in the emotional plot. Pacing the novel by them gives readers an inner heartbeat marching them ahead, a pulse independent of the visible story events that distract the eye

> **Emotional Mastery 10: Shifting from Tension to Energy** — for each pairing, push the protagonist to the bolder pole and describe how it manifests: witnessing injustice → getting involved; being good at something → a show of strength; helpful → reckless; insightful → kind; peaceful → exerting power to maintain peace; misfit → law-breaking nonconformist; impatient → wholly intolerant; attached → deeply involved; withdrawn → completely detached and uncaring; self-focused → vain; logical → intuitive and ingenious; attracted → leaning in for the kiss; can do magic → works miracles; wise → brings about the impossible.

> **Emotional Mastery 11: Shifting from Energy to Tension** — elaborate each: the protagonist's view of life; her unfulfilled need and how she regards it; in courage, what faith is also gained; in control, what is understood (or not); where she is assertive and where she yields; where adventure conflicts with romantic love; in strongest leadership, what must be taught; what she finds beautiful and why; about what is the audacious one perfectly sincere; how a rebellion is also heresy; how being alone is also poverty; how enacting justice also requires mercy.
